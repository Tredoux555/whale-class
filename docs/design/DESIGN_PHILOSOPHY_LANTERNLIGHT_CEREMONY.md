# Lanternlight Ceremony
*A design philosophy — Montree first-touch surfaces. Fable, Jul 16 2026.*

**The movement.** Lanternlight Ceremony is the aesthetics of founding — the visual language of a door being opened at night by someone holding a light for you. It rejects the fluorescent cheerfulness of SaaS onboarding entirely. Nothing here "converts"; everything *inducts*. The screen is not a form to fill but a hall to walk through, and every composition is built as a stage: one deep, near-black ground; one warm source of light; one subject held in it. The work must read as if a theatre's lighting director and a letterpress printer spent a week on each screen — meticulously crafted, labored over, unmistakably the product of deep expertise.

**Space and form.** Darkness is the primary material. The ground is not "dark green UI" — it is a forest at night (#030b08 to #071510), and it is generous: at least half of every composition is empty, breathing black-green. Into this dark, forms arrive as lit objects — glass panels with barely-there emerald edges, a single gold monogram treated like a bronze artifact in a museum case. Nothing floats arbitrarily; everything sits on one strong vertical axis or hangs from the golden thread that runs through the journey. Scale is dramatic and unashamed: the subject of each screen (a monogram, a key, a name) is two to three times larger than instinct suggests, and everything else steps far back to let it hold the room.

**Color and material.** Two materials only: deep glass and old gold. The glass is heavy — rgba blacks over blur, edged with emerald at 10–15% opacity, never brighter. The gold (#E8C96A into #cfa93f) is reserved the way a printer reserves foil: identity, keys, celebration, the thread of progress — never chrome, never decoration. Action lives in a single deep emerald, used sparingly so that when a button appears, it is obviously *the* thing to do. White is never white; it is candle-lit (92% warm white for voices, 60% for whispers). If a screen has more than one glowing thing, it is wrong.

**Rhythm and voice.** The journey moves like a procession, not a funnel: arrive, be named, receive the key, open the rooms, call the teachers, hand over the light. A golden thread — six small lanterns on a hairline — carries the walker's position and never shouts. Motion is ceremonial: one breath every five to six seconds on the hero light, a slow bloom at moments of completion, and stillness everywhere else; a screen that pulses in two places has broken the liturgy. Type is editorial and quiet-confident: Lora for what matters, spoken in short declarative lines ("Your school is founded."), Inter for the whisper-work, small caps letter-spaced for eyebrows. Text is sparse and load-bearing — a line of narration, an instruction, a blessing — never paragraphs.

**The companion.** A presence, not a widget: the guide occupies the top-left corner — where a reader's eye begins — as a small lit figure with a voice that narrates what is happening *right now* in second person ("You're adding your teachers…"), always ending in reassurance ("…don't worry, you can add more later"). It speaks like a colleague standing beside you, never like a tooltip. Its glass is slightly warmer than the room's, its avatar is gold, and it carries one quiet affordance to ask anything. The companion never blocks the stage, never dims the room, and never moves — constancy is its authority.

**The standard.** Every alignment, every 2-pixel inset shadow, every pause between words must look like the four-hundredth revision by someone at the absolute top of their field. Painstaking attention is the aesthetic: hairlines meet exactly, gold never bleeds past its purpose, and empty space is protected like the quiet in a chapel. The finished surface should make a stranger slow down — the unmistakable feeling of a place that was prepared for their arrival.
