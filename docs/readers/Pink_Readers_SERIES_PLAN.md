# Pink Readers — Series Plan (the bible)

A graded series of **decodable readers** for the Montree Pink Phase (UFLI
lessons 5–53). Each book is a real story a child can read **end to end with no
guessing** — every word is either phonics already taught, or a heart word
already on the binder ring.

This is the backbone document. Book 1 (`Book_01_Cat_Can_Nap.md`) is the built
sample. Books 2–15 + the teacher guide + the Canva production guide get built
once the sample voice is approved.

---

## Why this exists

The Pink Phase lesson content gives **words, phrases, and isolated sentence
cards**. It does not give **stories**. A child does not become a reader by
decoding sentence cards — they become a reader by reading *connected,
meaningful text they care about*. These readers are the missing rung between
"sentence card" and "a book in my hands."

---

## The iron rule — decodability

A decodable reader is defined by one discipline: **the child never meets a word
they cannot sound out.** For this series that means, for every book:

1. Every word uses only **letter-sounds taught up to that book's gate lesson**.
2. The only exceptions are **heart words already introduced** by that lesson
   (per the heart-word schedule in the lesson content).
3. `and` is treated as decodable from L13 (a-n-d — fully phonetic).
4. **No possessives, no `-s` plurals, no `-ing`/`-ed`** until the phonics
   supports them — these are not taught in Pink Phase, so a first-reader plural
   like "cats" technically isn't decodable. Books work around this.
5. Proper names must themselves be decodable (Sam = s-a-m). The recurring cast
   is built from decodable names only.

This is what makes the series **school-worthy** rather than a predictable
guessing-game "levelled reader." It slots straight into the existing sequence.

---

## What makes them GOOD (not just correct)

Most decodable readers are dull because the authors stop at "correct." The
craft on top of the iron rule:

- **A real arc.** Even at 9 pages and 13 words: a small want, a turn, a
  resolution. A reason to turn the page.
- **A recurring cast.** Children bond with characters across books. Sam and
  the cat anchor the whole series; a pup, Mom, Dad, a hen and a fox join as
  the phonics unlocks their names. By Book 15 it reads as one little world.
- **The pictures carry what the words can't.** The constrained vocabulary
  cannot say "Sam was amused." The *art* says it. Every art brief specifies the
  character's expression and the emotional subtext. This is the core technique
  — the words decode, the pictures tell.
- **Page-turn hooks.** Most pages end on a question, a trailing sentence, or an
  implied "what next." The child *must* turn the page.
- **Repetition with variation.** Young children love a pattern. A repeated
  sentence frame with one word changing lets the child predict — and
  predicting *is* the feeling of being a fluent reader. Confidence is the point.
- **Gentle humour.** CVC humour lands hard at 4–6: a cat in a pot, a pig in
  mud, things going comically wrong then right.
- **An ending that lands.** The last page is a "button" — warm, funny, or
  satisfying. Never just "The end."

## The readers (age & audience)

- **Age:** Montessori 3–6; in practice the 4½–6 band who are decoding.
- **Audience:** primarily **Mandarin-L1 ESL** children (per the Pink Phase ESL
  notes). So: concrete, universal situations; no culturally-specific
  references; short sentences; warm, simple emotional beats.
- **Length:** 8–12 pages. Book 1: 1 sentence/page. Later books: 1–3.
- **One book = one milestone.** A book is a *reward* for finishing a run of
  lessons, not a daily worksheet.

## Visual style — locked for all 15 books

So the series coheres as one shelf of books:

- **Illustration:** warm, flat, modern children's-book style. Soft rounded
  shapes, clean shapes, no clutter. One style for all 15 books.
- **Palette:** soft cream background, sage green, warm terracotta, gentle gold,
  with a soft **pink** accent (a quiet nod to "Pink Phase"). Brighter and
  warmer than the Montree app's dark forest — these are *children's books*.
- **Characters:** Sam and the cat are designed once and drawn the same in
  every book. Consistency is what makes the child bond.
- **Backgrounds:** simple and uncluttered — the scene supports the sentence,
  never competes with it. Plenty of calm space for the text.
- **Composition:** text always in the same zone per book (e.g. bottom third),
  on a clean band, never overlapping a busy part of the picture.

## Typography — non-negotiable for early readers

- **Lowercase print.** Children learn lowercase first (the sandpaper letters
  are lowercase). Text is lowercase except the first word of a sentence and
  names.
- **A literacy-designed font.** Use **Andika** (free, made for beginning
  readers — single-storey `a` and `g` that match the letters the child
  learned). Comic Sans is the fallback the flashcards already use. Never a
  decorative font — the child is matching letters to sounds.
- **Large and generously spaced.** ~28–36pt body text, wide line spacing, wide
  word spacing. One short line where possible.
- **High contrast.** Near-black text on the clean cream band. No text over a
  busy image.

## Per-book document format

Every reader doc (see `Book_01_Cat_Can_Nap.md`) contains:

1. **Header** — title, gate lesson, full word inventory used, heart words used,
   page count.
2. **Cover** — title + cover art brief.
3. **Each page** — the page text (exactly as printed), the **art brief** (scene,
   characters + expressions, key object, mood), and a **composition note**
   (where the text sits).
4. **Teacher note** — the pattern, the hook, the comprehension question, how to
   run the three reads.

The art briefs ARE the Canva inputs. The teacher + Canva production guides get
written once the series is approved.
