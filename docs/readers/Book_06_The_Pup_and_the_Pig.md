# Pink Reader · Book 6 — "The Pup and the Pig"

**Gate:** read after **Lesson 37** (Phase 2 — short a, i, o drilled to
automaticity).
**Pages:** cover + 12 story pages. **Two or three short sentences per page.**

*Working title in the series plan was "A Hot Dog and a Big Pig." Retitled to
feature the recurring **pup** (established cast, Book 2) instead of a one-off
dog — series cohesion. The book still leans on short a / i / o, with the
recurring `pup` and a handful of short-u words, all taught by Lesson 19.*

**Word inventory (every word in the book):**
`The · sun · is · hot · pup · can · not · nap · a · pig · in · mud · bad · hop ·
dig · had · fun · it · and · Sam · ran · to · you · are`
— 24 words total.

**Heart words used:** `the` (L11), `a` (L8), `and` (L13), `to` (L12), `you`
(L20), `are` (L20). All introduced well before the gate. ✓
**Decodability check:** every other word is pure CVC — `pig` (p-i-g), `mud`
(m-u-d), `bad` (b-a-d), `hop` (h-o-p), `dig` (d-i-g), `fun` (f-u-n), `sun`
(s-u-n). `ran` (r-a-n) and `had` (h-a-d) are decodable CVC past-tense words.
`Sam`, `pup`, `pig` are decodable nouns. **Nothing outruns the child's
phonics.** ✓

**The shape:** Book 6 is a real little story — a small want, a doubt, a
resolution. The **pup** is too hot to nap. It finds a **pig** sitting cool and
happy in the mud. The hook is the pup's hesitation — *"Is mud bad?"* — a doubt
the child recognises. The pig shows the way, the pup takes the leap, and the
mud turns out to be the best thing in the world. It closes warm and funny:
everyone happily filthy. **The pup's hesitation and then its joy live in the
art** — the words only carry the bare facts.

## Teacher note — how to use Book 6

**When:** hand it to the child during **Phase 2**, after Lesson 37. This is the
first of the "real story" readers — there is a problem (too hot), a doubt
(*is mud bad?*) and a resolution. Point that out: *"this one is a proper
story."*

**The pattern:** Book 6 is more story than pattern, but the cheers still give
fluent, predictable moments — *"Dig, pig, dig!"* (page 5) and *"Dig, dig,
dig!"* (page 10). Pages 3 and 9 are deliberately composed the same so the child
sees the pup has BECOME the cool, happy one.

**The three reads:**

1. **Read 1 — decode.** Child sounds out each word. By Phase 2 most CVC words
   should be quick — let the child feel that speed.
2. **Read 2 — smooth.** Child reads it again, "like talking." Read the pup's
   doubt on page 4 quietly, the joy on page 7 brightly.
3. **Read 3 — for the story.** Child reads it for fun. Talk about the pictures:
   *Why did the pup not jump straight in? What changed its mind?*

**The page-turn:** at page 4 (*"Is mud bad?"*) pause. Ask *"Do you think the
pup should hop in? What would YOU do?"* Then turn to page 5.

**Comprehension question:** *"Why was the pup hot at the start, and not hot at
the end?"* (Answer: the mud cooled it down.) Then: *"Was Sam angry about the
mud? How do you know?"* — the child reads Sam's laughing face on page 11. The
words never say Sam was amused; the picture does.

**A note on the doubt:** the small lesson of Book 6 is that the scary-looking
thing (the mud) turned out to be wonderful. If a child in your class is
hesitant about something new, this is a gentle book to read with them.

**ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch:** lots of short-vowel contrast practice here — `pup`
/ `pig`, `hot` / `hop`, `mud` / `mad` (if compared). Run the minimal pairs.
Final stops: /t/ in `hot`, `not`, `it`; /p/ in `pup`, `hop`, `nap`; /g/ in
`pig`, `dig`. Tissue test. The /r/ in `ran` — daily R/L drill.
