# Pink Reader · Book 1 — "Cat Can Nap"

**Gate:** read after **Lesson 17** (single consonants + short a/i/o + ck).
**Pages:** cover + 9 story pages. **One sentence per page** (the last page has
two short ones).

**Word inventory (every word in the book):**
`Sam · and · a · cat · the · can · nap · on · mat · in · pot · map · cap`
— 13 words total.

**Heart words used:** `a`, `the` (both introduced by Lesson 11). ✓
**Decodability check:** every other word is pure CVC/CV from letters s, a, t,
p, i, n, m, d, c, k, o — all taught by Lesson 13–17. `Sam` is a decodable
proper name (s-a-m). **Nothing in this book outruns the child's phonics.** ✓

**The shape:** a pattern book. The frame *"A cat can nap on/in a ___"* repeats
so the child can predict it and feel fluent — then page 7 breaks the pattern
with a question, page 8 pays it off, page 9 buttons it warm. The words stay
flat and simple on purpose; **Sam's face does the storytelling** — fond →
amused → laughing → delighted → sleepy. The art briefs carry that.

## Teacher note — how to use Book 1

**When:** hand it to the child after **Lesson 17**, as a milestone — *"you've
learned your sounds, here is your first real book."* It is a reward, not a
worksheet.

**The pattern:** pages 3–6 all run *"A cat can nap [on/in] a ___."* Let the
child notice the pattern — by page 5 most children predict the sentence and
read it almost fluently. **That feeling of fluency is the goal of Book 1.**
Don't rush past it.

**The three reads** (one sitting or three short ones):

1. **Read 1 — decode.** Child sounds out each word, finger under the text. Slow
   is fine. You read nothing for them — they own every word, because every word
   is decodable.
2. **Read 2 — smooth.** Child reads it again, a little faster, "like talking."
   Fluency and phrasing.
3. **Read 3 — for the story.** Child reads it for fun. Now talk about the
   pictures: *Where is the cat napping now? How does Sam feel on this page?*

**The page-turn:** at page 7 ("...on Sam?") pause. Ask *"Do you think the cat
will?"* Let them turn the page to find out.

**Comprehension question:** *"Where did the cat like to nap best?"* (Answer:
on Sam.) Then: *"How do we know Sam was happy about it?"* — the child reads
Sam's face, not the words. That is the comprehension skill.

**ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch:** the final /t/ in `cat`, `mat`, `pot` and the final
/p/ in `nap`, `map`, `cap` — Mandarin-L1 children drop word-final stops. Use
the tissue test from the lesson content. The repeated pattern is a gift here:
six clean reps of final /t/ and final /p/ in one short book.
