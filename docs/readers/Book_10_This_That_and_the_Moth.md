# Pink Reader · Book 10 — "This, That, and the Moth"

**Gate:** read after **Lesson 46** (Phase 3 — the digraphs `th` voiceless,
`wh`, and `th` voiced).
**Pages:** cover + 12 story pages. **One to two short sentences per page.**

**Word inventory (every word in the book):**
`A · moth · and · is · in · the · cat · can · not · nap · up · sat · on · this ·
that · hat · mat · ran · to · get · did · When · sit · Then`
— 24 words total.

**Heart words used:** `A` (L8), `and` (L13), `the` (L11), `this` (L30), `that`
(L34), `to` (L12). All introduced before the gate. ✓
**Decodability check:** the three new digraphs all feature — voiceless `th` in
`moth`; `wh` in `When`; voiced `th` in `Then`. The heart words `this` and
`that` are *also* the Lesson 46 voiced-`th` words, so they double as digraph
practice. Every other word is plain CVC. `sat`, `ran`, `did` are decodable
past-tense words. *(The gate also opens the heart words `any`/`many`; neither
fits this story naturally, so they are simply not used — available is not the
same as required.)* **Nothing outruns the child's phonics.** ✓

**The shape:** Book 10 turns the new `th`/`wh` digraphs into a gentle chase. A
soft **moth** flutters into the room and the **cat** cannot settle. The frame
*"The moth sat on this / on that…"* repeats — predictable, fluent, and pure
voiced-`th` practice. The hook uses the new `wh` word: *"When can the cat get
the moth?"* The answer is the warm series joke — the cat never catches the
moth by chasing; it gives up, sits still, and the moth comes to *it*. **The
cat's playful frustration and final delight live in the art.**

## Teacher note — how to use Book 10

**When:** hand it to the child during **Phase 3**, after Lesson 46 — once
voiceless `th`, `wh` and voiced `th` are all introduced. Book 10 uses all three.

**The pattern:** the frame *"The moth sat on this / on that…"* repeats on pages
3–5 and 9 — predictable and fluent, and every `this` and `that` is voiced-`th`
practice. Pause on `moth` (voiceless `th`), `When` (`wh`), `Then` and `this`,
`that` (voiced `th`) and let the child feel the difference: voiceless `th` has
no throat-buzz, voiced `th` does.

**The three reads:**

1. **Read 1 — decode.** Child sounds out each word. On every `th` word, run one
   finger under both letters — one sound. Hand on throat: does it buzz?
2. **Read 2 — smooth.** Child reads it again, "like talking." Soft, calm voice
   to match the gentle moth.
3. **Read 3 — for the story.** Child reads it for fun. Talk about the pictures:
   *Is the moth ever in danger? How can you tell it is just a game?*

**The page-turn:** at page 8 (*"When can the cat get the moth?"*) pause. Ask
*"Do you think the cat will ever catch the moth?"* Then read on — and notice
the answer is a surprise: it does not catch it; the moth comes to it.

**Comprehension question:** *"The cat chased and chased and never caught the
moth. So how did the moth end up on the cat?"* (Answer: the cat sat still, and
the moth landed on it on its own.) A gentle point worth a moment of talk —
sometimes being calm and still works better than chasing.

**ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch:** `th` is the hardest English sound for Mandarin-L1
children — there is no `th` in Mandarin, so `moth` may become "mos" or "mot".
Tongue tip BETWEEN the teeth, visible. Mirror work on every `th` page. Voiced
`th` (`this`, `that`, `then`) buzzes; voiceless `th` (`moth`) does not. Months
of patience — celebrate small progress.
