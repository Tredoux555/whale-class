# Pink Readers — Canva Production Guide

How to turn the fifteen reader manuscripts in this folder into finished,
printed little books using Canva. Written for a teacher, not a designer — every
step is concrete.

Each reader's `.md` file already gives you everything you need: the page text
exactly as it should be printed, an **art brief** for every page, and a
**composition note** saying where the text sits. This guide explains how to
build those into pages and assemble a book.

---

## The golden rule: one locked style for all fifteen

The fifteen books must look like **one shelf of books** — the same world, the
same friends, the same hand. A child bonds with Sam and the cat across the
whole series; that only works if Sam and the cat look *identical* in every
book. So:

- **Decide the look once, on Book 1, and never drift.** Sam, the cat, the
  palette, the font, the layout — all locked on Book 1 and copied forward.
- **Make a Canva template, not fifteen separate designs.** Build the Book 1
  layout, then "Duplicate" it for every other page and every other book. The
  template is the consistency.

---

## The locked visual style

### Palette

Warmer and brighter than the Montree app — these are *children's books*.

| Use | Colour | Hex |
|-----|--------|-----|
| Page background | soft cream | `#FBF6EC` |
| Text band | slightly deeper cream / off-white | `#FFFDF7` |
| Main text | near-black (never pure black) | `#2E2A22` |
| Leaves, grass, calm shapes | sage green | `#8BA888` |
| Warm accents (clay, earth, the fox) | warm terracotta | `#CC7E5B` |
| Sun, highlights, small joys | gentle gold | `#E8C96A` |
| The "Pink" accent — a quiet nod to the phase | soft pink | `#E9B7BE` |

Use the pink sparingly — a cushion, a cap, a sky tint, the cover sub-line. It
is a wink, not a theme.

### Illustration style

- **Flat, warm, modern children's-book illustration.** Soft rounded shapes,
  clean outlines or no outlines, no clutter, no gradients-gone-wild.
- **One style for all fifteen books.** If you use Canva's flat-illustration
  elements, stay in that family. If you use Canva Magic Media (AI images), use
  the *same prompt style words every time* — see below.
- **Backgrounds stay simple.** The scene supports the sentence; it never
  competes with it. A window, a plant, a rug — one or two shapes, lots of calm
  space. Empty space is good. It is where the child's eye rests.

### The characters — design once, copy forever

Design these on their first appearance and **save each as a Canva element you
re-paste** into every later page:

- **Sam** (every book) — a young child, about five, gender-neutral, round
  friendly face, simple warm clothing. Designed on Book 1 page 1.
- **The cat** (every book) — a plump, friendly tabby, expressive, often napping.
  Designed on Book 1 page 1.
- **The pup** (from Book 2) — small, round, floppy-eared, eager.
- **Mom / Dad** (from Books 2–3 / Book 5) — warm, soft, gentle faces.
- **The hen** (from Book 3) — plump, expressive, slightly comic.
- **The fox** (from Book 4) — slim, orange, bright-eyed, a touch of mischief.
- **The frog** (from Book 13) — rounded, friendly, soft green.

Each book's cover art brief says *"design [character] here — every later book
draws it exactly the same."* Honour that. The single most important
consistency check across the series is: **does Sam look like the same child in
Book 1 and Book 15?**

### If you use Canva Magic Media (AI images)

AI images drift in style. To keep fifteen books coherent:

- Build a fixed **style suffix** and append it to *every* image prompt:
  *"…flat warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes, soft cream
  background, sage green and terracotta and gold, gentle, uncluttered, no
  text."*
- Generate the **characters** as separate elements on transparent / plain
  backgrounds, then place the *same* character image into many scenes — do not
  re-generate Sam for every page, or Sam will look different every page.
- Always generate **"no text"** — you add the text yourself, in the font below.

### Character generation prompts

Every page of every reader has a ready-to-paste **Image prompt** (see the next
section). Those page prompts name the characters — *"Sam"*, *"the cat"* — but
they do not re-describe them, because **you generate the cast once, here,
first.** This is what keeps Sam the same child in Book 1 and Book 15.

**The two-step workflow:**

1. **Step 1 — make the cast.** Paste each character prompt below into Canva
   Magic Media. Pick the best result, remove its background (Canva: *Edit
   image → BG Remover*), and save it to your Brand Kit / a "Pink Readers cast"
   folder. Do this once for the whole series.
2. **Step 2 — build each page.** For each page, paste that page's **Image
   prompt** to generate the *scene and background*, then drop your saved
   character elements on top, posed as the page's art brief describes. The
   page prompt tells you the scene, the poses and the expressions; the saved
   element keeps the character on-model.

Each character prompt already ends with the style words — paste it exactly:

- **Sam** — *A young child about five years old, gender-neutral, round friendly
  face, soft dark hair, warm simple clothing in cream and sage green, a gentle
  smile, standing relaxed, full body, flat warm children's-book illustration,
  soft rounded shapes, plain white background, no text*
- **The cat** — *A plump friendly tabby cat, soft brown and grey stripes, big
  gentle eyes, curled in a relaxed sitting pose, flat warm children's-book
  illustration, soft rounded shapes, plain white background, no text*
- **The pup** — *A small round puppy, golden-brown, floppy ears, big eager
  eyes, short legs, sitting bouncy and alert, flat warm children's-book
  illustration, soft rounded shapes, plain white background, no text*
- **Mom** — *A warm gentle mother, soft round face, kind eyes, hair tied back,
  soft clothing in warm terracotta and cream, a calm smile, standing relaxed,
  full body, flat warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes,
  plain white background, no text*
- **Dad** — *A warm gentle father, soft round face, kind eyes, short hair,
  soft clothing in sage green and cream, a calm smile, standing relaxed, full
  body, flat warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes, plain
  white background, no text*
- **The hen** — *A plump cheerful hen, warm reddish-brown feathers, a small red
  comb, one bright alert eye, a slightly comic expressive face, standing, flat
  warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes, plain white
  background, no text*
- **The fox** — *A slim friendly fox, warm orange fur, a bushy white-tipped
  tail, pointed ears, bright eyes with a touch of mischief, standing alert,
  flat warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes, plain white
  background, no text*
- **The frog** — *A small rounded friendly frog, soft green, big gentle eyes, a
  wide gentle mouth, sitting, flat warm children's-book illustration, soft
  rounded shapes, plain white background, no text*

**One-off animals** — the pig (Book 6), the fish and the chick (Book 9) and the
crab (Book 13) appear in a single book each, so they are described in full
inside that book's page prompts; they do not need a cast-sheet entry. Generate
them the same way the first time they appear in their book, and reuse the saved
element across that book's pages.

## Typography — non-negotiable for early readers

The child is matching letters to the sounds they learned. The print must match
the letters they were taught.

- **Font: Andika.** It is free, and it was *designed for beginning readers* —
  it has a single-storey `a` and a single-storey `g`, exactly the letterforms
  on the sandpaper letters. Add it in Canva via **Brand Kit → Add a font**, or
  search "Andika" in the font list. If Andika is ever unavailable, the
  flashcards' fallback (Comic Sans MS) is acceptable — but never a decorative
  or serif font. The child must recognise every letter.
- **Lowercase print.** Type the text in lowercase, exactly as the manuscript
  prints it — only the first word of a sentence and names (`Sam`, `Mom`) are
  capitalised. Children learn lowercase first.
- **Large.** Body text around **28–36 pt**. One short line per line of text
  where possible.
- **Generously spaced.** Wide line spacing (1.5+) and slightly widened letter
  spacing. Crowded text is hard for a decoding eye.
- **High contrast.** Near-black text (`#2E2A22`) on the clean cream text band.
- **Never over a busy image.** Text always sits on the **clean band** the
  composition note describes — almost always the bottom third of the page.
  If the illustration creeps into the band, fade or clear that area behind the
  text. The words must never fight the picture.

## Page size and layout

- **Page size:** a square page works beautifully for a little book — set the
  Canva design to **square, e.g. 20 × 20 cm** (or US letter folded — see
  assembly). Square is forgiving and child-friendly.
- **One manuscript page = one Canva page.** The cover is its own page.
- **The text band:** reserve the **bottom third** of every page for text. Place
  a soft rounded rectangle in the band colour (`#FFFDF7`) across that third,
  and set the text on it. Keep the band in the *same place on every page of a
  book* — the composition notes are written to this rule.
- **The art fills the upper two-thirds**, following the page's art brief. Keep
  the main character and the "key object" of the page centred and clear.
- **Margins:** keep all art and text at least 1.5 cm from the page edge —
  printing and trimming eat the edge.

### Building one page, step by step

1. Duplicate your locked template page.
2. Read the manuscript page's **art brief**. Build the scene: background shapes
   first, then the key object, then the characters (paste your saved character
   elements — do not redraw them), then expressions. The art brief tells you
   every character's expression — get those right; they carry the story.
3. Read the **composition note**. Place the characters and object where it
   says; place the text band where it says.
4. Type the **page text**, exactly as printed in the manuscript — same words,
   same punctuation, lowercase. Andika, ~32 pt, near-black, on the band.
5. Check: is the text clear of the busy art? Is every character on-model? Is
   there calm space?

## Assembling, exporting and printing a little book

1. **Order the pages** in Canva: cover, then page 1, 2, 3 … to the button page.
2. **Export** as **PDF Print** (Canva → Share → Download → PDF Print). Tick
   "crop marks and bleed" off for a simple home/school print; on for a print
   shop.
3. **Print** on the heaviest paper your printer takes (160 gsm if possible —
   little hands are hard on books). Print **single-sided** for the simplest
   assembly: each manuscript page becomes one printed sheet.
4. **Make the book:**
   - *Simplest (stack and staple):* stack the printed sheets in order, fold the
     stack once down the middle if you sized pages to print two-up, and
     **staple twice along the fold** (a long-arm stapler helps). Trim the outer
     edges straight.
   - *Even simpler (no fold):* keep single sheets in order, stack them, and
     staple twice down the **left edge**. A perfectly good little book.
5. **Laminate the cover** if the book will live in a busy classroom — or
   laminate every page for the most-loved early titles.
6. **Write the book number** small on the spine or back so the set stays in
   order on the shelf.

### Print the set as a matched shelf

Print all fifteen the same size, the same paper, the same way. Fifteen matched
little books on a shelf — numbered 1 to 15 — *look* like an achievement, and a
child works through them like one.

## A consistency checklist before you print

Run this over every finished book:

- [ ] Sam looks like the same child as in Book 1.
- [ ] The cat (and every returning character) is on-model.
- [ ] The palette is the locked palette — no stray bright colours.
- [ ] The font is Andika (or the agreed fallback), lowercase, large, well
      spaced.
- [ ] The text band is in the same place on every page of the book.
- [ ] No text sits over a busy part of the picture.
- [ ] The page text matches the manuscript **exactly** — this is a decodable
      reader; a single swapped word can break the decodability promise.
- [ ] Every character's expression matches the art brief — the pictures carry
      the feelings the words cannot.
- [ ] The cover sub-line reads the correct "Pink Reader · Book N".

The last point about exact text matters most. The manuscripts are
word-by-word decodability-audited. If you "improve" a line in Canva — add a
word, swap a word — you may quietly introduce a word the child cannot decode,
and the iron rule is broken. **Type the manuscript text exactly. Do not edit
it.**
