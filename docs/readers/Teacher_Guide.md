# Pink Readers — Teacher Guide

A graded series of fifteen **decodable readers** for the Pink Phase (UFLI
lessons 5–53). This guide explains what these books are, why they are built the
way they are, and exactly how to use them with a child.

---

## What a decodable reader is

A decodable reader is a real story in which **the child never meets a word they
cannot sound out.** Every word is either a letter-sound the child has already
been taught, or a heart word already introduced.

That is the whole discipline — and it is what makes these books different from
the "levelled" or "predictable" readers a child might meet elsewhere.

**A predictable reader** teaches a child to *guess*. The text repeats a frame
("I see a dog. I see a cat. I see a…") and the pictures give the answer, so the
child reads by memorising the pattern and looking at the picture — not by
decoding. It feels like reading. It is not. When the patterns run out, the
child has no strategy left.

**A decodable reader** teaches a child to *decode*. Because every word is
within reach, the child sounds words out, builds them, and earns them. The
picture supports the story and the feeling — it never rescues the word. When
the child closes a Pink Reader, every word in it was genuinely read.

This is why the series slots straight into the Montessori language sequence:
it is the missing rung between the lesson content's isolated sentence cards and
"a real book in my hands."

### The iron rule, in one line

For every book, every word is a letter-sound or pattern taught **up to that
book's gate lesson**, or a **heart word already introduced** by that gate (plus
`and`, decodable a-n-d from Lesson 13). Nothing in any book outruns the child's
phonics. That is a promise to the child — and it is checked, word by word, in
the header of every reader.

---

## A book is a milestone, not a worksheet

Each reader "unlocks" after a particular lesson — its **gate**. The book is a
**reward** for finishing that run of lessons, not a daily exercise.

Hand a child Book 1 after Lesson 17 with a real sense of occasion: *"You have
learned your sounds — here is your first proper book."* A child who has worked
through seventeen lessons of sandpaper letters and movable alphabet has earned
a story. Treat it that way. A book handed over as a milestone is read with
pride; the same book handed over as a worksheet is read with a sigh.

Do not race a child through the series. One book marks one milestone. Between
books, the child keeps doing lessons. Re-reading a favourite Pink Reader is
always welcome — but the *next* book waits for the *next* gate.

## The three reads

A decodable reader is read **three times** — in one sitting or across three
short ones. Each read does a different job.

**Read 1 — decode.** The child sounds out each word, finger moving under the
text. Slow is completely fine. *You read nothing for them.* Every word is
decodable, so every word is the child's to earn. Resist the urge to supply a
word — wait, and let them build it. This is the read that proves the book.

**Read 2 — smooth.** The child reads it again, a little faster, "like talking."
Now the work shifts from decoding to *fluency and phrasing* — reading words in
groups, with the rhythm of speech. The repeated frames and cheers (*"Run, pup,
run!"*, *"Dig, dig, dig!"*) are gifts here: read them with bounce.

**Read 3 — for the story.** The child reads it for *fun*. The words are no
longer an obstacle, so attention is free for meaning. This is the read where
you talk about the pictures and the story.

A child may want a **fourth read — aloud, to someone**: a parent, a teacher, a
younger friend. From around Book 5 onward, reading TO another person is the
natural next step, and the warm full-cast books are built for it.

## Page-turn hooks — keep the child turning

Most pages end on a small hook: a question, a trailing sentence, an implied
"what next." The child should *want* to turn the page.

Use the hook. When you reach a hook page — Book 1 page 7 (*"A cat can nap on
Sam?"*), Book 4 page 7 (*"the box did tip!"*), Book 8 page 9 (*"Will the pup
get the bell?"*) — **stop**. Ask the child what they think happens next. Take a
guess or two. *Then* turn the page. That pause is where a child learns that
reading is something you do *to find out* — the root of all reading for
meaning.

Each book's teacher note names its hook page and the question to ask.

---

## The comprehension questions

After Read 3, talk about the story. Every book's teacher note gives two
questions. They come in a deliberate pair.

**The first question is about the events.** *"Where did the cat like to nap
best?" "Who was hiding in the box?" "Why was the pup hot at the start, and not
hot at the end?"* The answer is in the words and the pictures together — a
check that the child followed the story.

**The second question is about a feeling — and the answer is in the picture,
not the words.** *"How do we know Sam was happy about it?" "How did the fox
feel when the cat came out?" "Was Mom cross? How can you tell?"*

This second question is the heart of comprehension teaching in this series. The
decodable vocabulary is too small to *say* "Sam was overjoyed" or "the fox was
startled." So the **art carries the emotion** — and the child learns to *read
the picture*: the wide grin, the flat ears, the fond eyes. Reading a face is a
real comprehension skill, and these books teach it on every page. Always ask
the feeling question. Always let the child point to the picture for the answer.

## The Mandarin-L1 watch points

The Pink Readers are written for a classroom of **Mandarin-L1 ESL** children —
concrete, universal situations, short sentences, warm and simple feelings. A
few sound patterns need steady attention. Each book's teacher note flags the
ones relevant to that book; here is the overview.

**Final stops — the most common slip.** Mandarin syllables do not end in /t/,
/p/, /k/, /d/, /g/, /b/. A Mandarin-L1 child will drop the final consonant —
"cat" becomes "ca", "nap" becomes "na", "dig" becomes "di". The Pink Readers
are *full* of final stops on purpose: `cat`, `mat`, `pot`, `hat`, `nap`,
`hop`, `cap`, `pig`, `dig`. Use the **tissue test** from the lesson content:
hold a small piece of tissue in front of the mouth — on a real final /t/ or
/p/ the tissue flutters. If it does not flutter, the stop was dropped. The
repeated patterns give many clean reps of the same final sound in one book —
that is by design.

**The hard consonants.** Keep the daily minimal-pair drills from the lesson
content running alongside the readers:
- **/r/** (Books 2, 3, 13) — the hardest single sound. R/L drill: red/led,
  rip/lip, run/… Months of patience.
- **/th/** (Book 10) — no `th` in Mandarin; "moth" drifts to "mos" or "mot".
  Tongue tip *between* the teeth, visible. Mirror work every `th` page.
- **/v/, /z/, /j/** — substituted with /w/, /s/, /z/-ish. Mirror work.

**Consonant clusters — the Phase 3 wall.** Mandarin has no consonant clusters
at all. A Mandarin-L1 child will either break the cluster with an extra vowel
("se-led" for "sled") or drop a consonant ("fas" for "fast"). Books 11–14 lean
hard on blends for exactly this practice. The fix: keep the mouth moving from
one consonant straight into the next — no reset, no vowel between. Slow first,
then quick. Triple blends (Book 14) often take Mandarin-L1 children three to
four months to master. **That is completely normal. There is no rush.**

**The vowels.** Short /ă/ and short /ĕ/ are the hardest English vowels for
Mandarin-L1 children. Book 7 (*Cat? Cot? Cup?*) is built as a vowel-
discrimination game — use it, and pair it with the Lesson 40 minimal-pair
drill.

## A few practical notes

- **Let the child hold the book.** A decodable reader is a physical object the
  child owns the experience of. Finger under the text, their own pace.
- **Do not pre-teach the words.** The point is that the child can decode them
  cold. If a word genuinely stumps them on Read 1, sound the first letter with
  them and wait — do not just say the word.
- **Re-reading is reading.** A child who wants Book 6 again for the fifth time
  is building fluency. Let them.
- **Recognise the cast.** Sam and the cat anchor every book; the pup, Mom, Dad,
  the hen, the fox and the frog join as the phonics unlocks their names. From
  Book 4 onward, ask: *"Have you met this friend before?"* The crossover is
  part of the joy.
- **A child who is not ready is not behind.** If a book feels hard, the child
  is simply not at its gate yet. Go back to the lessons, re-read an easier
  Pink Reader, and return. Finishing well matters more than finishing fast.

---

## What comes after the Pink Readers

A child who reads Book 15 (*The Big Pink Trip*) confidently — including the
blends in `splash`, `trip`, `frog` — has finished the Pink Phase and is ready
for the **Blue Phase**: the silent-`e` / VCe pattern, longer words, and the
first vowel teams. The decodable-reader discipline continues there — the child
simply has new tools.

A child who is not quite there yet should re-read favourite Pink Readers and
run more Phase 3 review. Come back to Book 15 when it feels easy. Every word in
these fifteen books was earned — that is the foundation everything else is
built on.
