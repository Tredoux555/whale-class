# Pink Reader · Book 9 — "A Fish and a Chick"

**Gate:** read after **Lesson 43** (Phase 3 — the digraphs `sh` and `ch`: two
letters, one sound).
**Pages:** cover + 12 story pages. **One to two short sentences per page.**

**Word inventory (every word in the book):**
`Sam · has · a · fish · the · is · in · dish · chick · ran · up · to · what ·
sat · at · it · can · dash · chat · not · get · be · pal · Yes · over · and ·
had · fun · nap`
— 29 words total.

**Heart words used:** `a` (L8), `the` (L11), `to` (L12), `what` (L27), `be`
(L19), `over` (L42), `and` (L13). All introduced by the gate. ✓
**Decodability check:** the new digraphs carry the book — `sh` in `fish`,
`dish`, `dash`; `ch` in `chick`, `chat`. Every other word is plain CVC — `pal`
(p-a-l), `Yes` (y-e-s), `get` (g-e-t). `ran`, `sat`, `had` are decodable
past-tense words. **Nothing outruns the child's phonics — and no vowel-team
words** (the lesson lists `beach`/`reach` under `ch`, but `ea` is a later
phase, so they are kept out). ✓

**The shape:** Book 9 opens Phase 3 by making the two new digraphs the heart of
the story — a **fish** (`sh`) and a **chick** (`ch`) — and the verbs lean on
them too: the fish can `dash`, the chick can `chat`. The mirrored cheer pages
(*"Chat, chick, chat!"* / *"Dash, fish, dash!"*) give the child two fluent,
predictable beats. The gentle question at the centre — *can two so different
be friends?* — is a universal one for the ESL classroom, and the answer is
warm: yes, even across the rim of a dish.

## Teacher note — how to use Book 9

**When:** hand it to the child during **Phase 3**, after Lesson 43 — once `sh`
and `ch` are secure. Book 9 makes those two digraphs the whole story.

**The pattern:** the mirrored cheers — *"Chat, chick, chat!"* (page 5) and
*"Dash, fish, dash!"* (page 6) — are the fluent, predictable beats, and they
are also `ch` and `sh` practice. Pause on `fish`, `dish`, `dash`, `chick`,
`chat` and remind the child: *two letters, one sound.*

**The three reads:**

1. **Read 1 — decode.** Child sounds out each word. On the digraph words, run
   one finger under `sh` and `ch` together — one sweep, one sound.
2. **Read 2 — smooth.** Child reads it again, "like talking." Read the
   chick's chatter brightly, the quiet of page 7 softly.
3. **Read 3 — for the story.** Child reads it for fun. Talk about the pictures:
   *How are the fish and the chick different? How are they still friends?*

**The page-turn:** at page 8 (*"Can a chick be a pal to a fish?"*) pause. Ask
*"What do you think — can they be friends?"* Let the child decide, then turn
the page.

**Comprehension question:** *"The chick could not get into the water. So how
did the chick and the fish stay friends?"* (Answer: the chick stayed close at
the glass, and the fish swam over to meet it — they met in the middle.) A
gentle, real point about friendship across difference — worth a few moments of
talk in an ESL classroom of many backgrounds.

**ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch:** English `sh` is forward and flat; Mandarin `sh`
(*shī*) curls the tongue back — mirror work. Same for `ch`. The digraphs say
ONE sound — Mandarin-L1 children may want two ("c-h-at"). One sweep. Final
stops: /t/ in `chat`, `at`, `it`, `not`; /k/ in `chick`. Tissue test.
