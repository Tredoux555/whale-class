# HeyGen Pitch Video Library — Agent Pitch Flow

**For:** Agents pitching Montree to principals
**Tier:** HeyGen Creator+ (high tier — credits not the constraint, quality is)
**Avatar:** Stick with the "GB - Riley" voice setup from prior production; default avatar; clean background; talking head; no B-roll
**Format:** All scripts are paste-ready into HeyGen Builder → Script to Video

**The premise this library is built on:**

The principal you are pitching is overworked, runs a 250-student school with no education background, takes ChatGPT advice over her qualified staff, and is treating school like a media campaign. She is exhausted, performative, and quietly worried she doesn't know what she's doing. What you are offering her isn't *software*. It is *a break*. It is the feeling of being prepared for the first time. Lead with that. Always.

---

## How to use this library

Send these as a **sequence** over 24–48 hours. Not all at once. Each video earns the right to send the next.

| # | When | Length | What it does |
|---|---|---|---|
| **V1** | Day 0, ~2 hours before the meeting (or the night before) | 30 sec | Personalised first-touch. Sets the tone. Single ask. |
| **V2** | In the meeting OR sent immediately after the warm intro | 60 sec | "The magic." Photo → AI observation. The wow moment. |
| **V3** | Mid-meeting, when she leans in | 90 sec | The Weekly Wrap. What parents receive. The biggest time-saver. |

## V1 — First touch (≈30 seconds)

> Use this version if the meeting is tomorrow. Personalise the bracketed parts. Send by WhatsApp or email the night before. **Do not send any other video before this one.**

**Script:**

> Hi [Jennie]. Tredoux here.
>
> Quick thirty-second note before we sit down tomorrow.
>
> I've been quietly running a piece of software in Whale Class for a while now — it's called Montree, and I've been using it to keep up with the children, write the parent reports, and stay on top of what each one needs. It's been the single most useful thing I've added to my classroom in a year.
>
> I'd love ten minutes tomorrow to show you what it does. Not to sell you anything. Just to show you. I think you'll see immediately whether it's something that could give you back some of your own time.
>
> See you in the morning.

**Why it works:**
The principal already knows you. She doesn't need a pitch — she needs permission. The video doesn't say "I'm pitching you tomorrow." It says "I'm going to show you something useful." The ask is ten minutes, not a decision.

The word "quietly" is doing important work. She's a media-campaign principal — she will respect that you've been doing something effective *without making it about yourself*.

## V2 — "The magic" (≈60 seconds)

> Use this as the FIRST demo video. Show it once. Don't talk over it the first time — let it play. The wow comes from the silence after.

**Script:**

> Here's how it works.
>
> A teacher sees a child doing something worth noting. They take a photo. That's it. They go back to teaching.
>
> Montree takes it from there. It looks at the photo. It identifies the Montessori work. It writes the observation. It logs the child's progress. It does the paperwork the teacher would have done that evening at home, after the children were asleep.
>
> All the teacher did was take a picture.
>
> If your teachers are doing observation forms by hand right now — and most of them are — this is roughly five hours a week that goes back into the classroom.
>
> Five hours. Every teacher. Every week.

**Why it works:**
The story is the demo. No screens, no jargon. Just: photo → done. The five-hours-a-week claim is grounded — it's roughly what an experienced Montessori teacher spends on paperwork — and you can defend it if pressed.

The line "the paperwork the teacher would have done that evening at home, after the children were asleep" is calibrated for THIS principal. She overworks teachers. You're telling her, gently, that she doesn't have to anymore.

## V3 — The Weekly Wrap (≈90 seconds)

**Script:**

> Every week, every child in the school gets a report.
>
> Not the kind teachers spend their Sundays writing. The kind that reads like a story.
>
> It tells the parent what their child worked on this week. What progressed. What's coming up next. Why what they did matters. It's written in proper, warm English — and in Mandarin, and in nine other languages — automatically.
>
> The parent gets a real, specific, child-by-child report every Friday. Not "Hayden had a great week" — but *"Hayden mastered the Pink Tower this week. He's moved into the Brown Stair. Here is what that means for his sense of mathematics in two weeks' time."*
>
> That report? It used to take a teacher forty minutes per child. For your school, that's six hundred hours a year of teacher time.
>
> Now it takes Montree four minutes per child, and a teacher just glances at it before it goes out.
>
> Parents will tell you it's the best communication they've ever had from a school. And teachers will tell you that for the first time, they're not behind.

**Why it works:**
The exact phrasing — "the kind teachers spend their Sundays writing" — names the exact pain point she is causing without accusing her. The "forty minutes × 250 children × 38 weeks = 600 hours" math is correct and devastating.

The Mandarin mention is critical for this principal's market. Don't bury it.

**Note:** the line "I've used her for a meeting myself" is honest — you have, you used her to prep for the meeting you're in right now. You can tell her that. It's a strong move.

## V5 — The cockpit (≈75 seconds)

> Use only if she asks something like "but how would I keep track of this across 15 classrooms?"

**Script:**

> Your view, as principal, is different from a teacher's.
>
> You open Montree, and you see your whole school at a glance. Which classrooms are running smoothly. Which teachers have been quietly carrying the load. Which children haven't been observed in a few days. Which parents are due a check-in. Which work cycles are starting to drift.
>
> You don't have to *ask* the teachers for the data. You're not chasing observation forms anymore. The data has been quietly accumulating all week, and Montree has organised it for you.
>
> If something needs your attention, Tracy surfaces it. If nothing needs your attention, she doesn't bother you. That's the deal.
>
> A 250-child school is a lot to hold in your head. The point of this isn't more information — it's that you can stop trying to hold it all.

**Why it works:**
She is currently holding everything in her head and it's burning her out. "You can stop trying to hold it all" is the offer. The cockpit is the proof.

The line "you don't have to ask the teachers for the data" is the sentence that should make her sit up. You'll know if she does.

## V6 — Bilingual reports (≈60 seconds)

> Use if she has a Chinese parent base, or if she ever brings up language. Probably essential for her market.

**Script:**

> Most schools have a real mix of families. Some parents read English. Some read Mandarin. Some only read Mandarin.
>
> Every report Montree writes — every parent message, every weekly wrap, every developmental note — comes out in twelve languages. Not translated. *Written*, in proper, native, AMI-aligned Montessori language. A Mandarin-speaking parent receives a report that reads exactly like a Mandarin-speaking Montessori teacher wrote it, because the same intelligence that wrote the English version wrote the Mandarin version directly.
>
> If a parent prefers Mandarin, you set their language once. Every communication from then on arrives in Mandarin. Including this conversation we're having now — Tracy would have generated this preparation for you in Mandarin if I'd asked her to.
>
> Twelve languages. Real ones. Not Google Translate.

**Why it works:**
This is differentiation. Competitors don't do this well. Naming the AMI alignment in passing reassures her about pedagogical rigor. The throwaway line "she would have generated this in Mandarin if I'd asked" makes the capability feel native, not bolted on.

## V7 — The close (≈45 seconds)

> Use at the end of the meeting, after you've shown 2–4 demos. Don't ask for a yes. Just describe what happens next.

**Script:**

> Here's what I think makes sense.
>
> Take the next thirty days. We turn it on for one classroom — pick one, your choice — and you watch what happens. No credit card. No setup fee. No commitment.
>
> In thirty days, you'll know. The teacher will tell you. The parents will tell you. You'll see your own report and you'll know.
>
> If it's not for you, we walk away as friends. If it is — you'd be making one decision, not fifteen. Just one.
>
> That's the offer. Have a think.

**Why it works:**
No close. Just an offer. The phrase "you'll know" appears three times because she will trust her own conviction more than yours. The "one decision, not fifteen" frame respects how busy she is — she doesn't have time to evaluate fifteen software products, but she has time to watch one classroom for thirty days.

The "walk away as friends" line is important. She is a relationship-driven principal. You are signalling that this stays a friendship whichever way she lands.

## V8 — Objection-handler micro-videos (≈30 sec each)

> Eight short videos for the most common worries. Send one only if she raises it. Sending unprompted will feel defensive.

### V8a — "Cost"

> The pricing is straightforward — seven US dollars per child per month, billed monthly, after a thirty-day free trial.
>
> For your school, full deployment would land somewhere around seventeen hundred dollars a month. For context, that's roughly the cost of half a part-time teaching assistant.
>
> The value it returns, in teacher time alone, is conservatively eight to ten times that. The parent-retention story usually pays for the rest several times over.
>
> But you don't need to take my word for it. The trial covers one classroom for thirty days, free, for exactly that reason.

### V8b — "Our teachers don't have time to take photos"

> This is the most common worry, and it's the wrong way round.
>
> Teachers don't *take more photos* with Montree. They keep doing exactly what they're already doing — most teachers already snap a quick picture of work the children are doing. They just stop having to do anything with it afterwards.
>
> The five hours a week they save in paperwork is the five hours that goes back into the classroom. The photo is the easy part. Montree does the hard part.

### V8c — "AI worries me. I don't want machines raising the children."

> Nobody's raising the children with AI. The teachers are. The teachers are entirely the teachers.
>
> What the AI does is take the paperwork off them. It doesn't decide what the child should learn. It doesn't replace the lesson. It doesn't talk to the child. It writes the report afterwards, so the teacher doesn't have to.
>
> Same way you wouldn't say a calculator does the maths for the accountant. The accountant is still the accountant. The tool just makes them faster.

### V8d — "We already have a system"

> Most schools do, and most of those systems were built ten or fifteen years ago — they manage attendance, billing, maybe a roster. Montree doesn't replace any of that. It sits alongside it.
>
> What Montree does is the *observation, the developmental tracking, and the parent communication* — the parts your current system either doesn't touch or does very badly.
>
> If you'd like, I'll show you specifically where Montree fits next to what you have. It tends to be obvious within five minutes.

### V8e — "What about teacher buy-in? They'll resist."

> Teachers tend to be the most enthusiastic adopters once they realise they'll get their evenings back.
>
> What I'd suggest is the trial classroom is the teacher who is currently most overworked. The relief is immediate. They tell the other teachers. By the end of the thirty days, the other teachers are asking you when they get it.
>
> The pushback you might expect simply doesn't show up. We have yet to see it.

### V8f — "What about privacy? Children's data?"

> This is a fair worry and worth being precise about.
>
> Photos are stored encrypted. They never leave the school's account. Parents see only their own child's photos. The AI that processes a photo to identify the work doesn't retain it after the processing finishes — it's not training on your data.
>
> The data is yours, the children are yours, and you can export everything at any time.
>
> If you'd like a fuller privacy walkthrough, I can send our one-pager.

### V8g — "What if you go out of business?"

> Reasonable question.
>
> Montree is owned by a Hong Kong company, set up specifically because we wanted long-term institutional permanence — not a Silicon Valley acquisition exit. The reports a teacher writes through Montree are exportable to standard formats. The child progress data is exportable. If we ever did disappear, you would walk away with everything you put in.
>
> But beyond that — we are growing because schools like ours pay seven dollars per child per month, not because we're chasing venture funding to flip the company. We're built to last.

### V8h — "Can I think about it?"

> Of course. Always.
>
> All I'd suggest is this — don't think about it longer than the trial would have taken. Thirty days of one classroom, free, would have shown you everything you'd think about. The decision is harder *without* the data than with it.
>
> Whenever you're ready, I'm here. No follow-up nudges. You'll know when you're ready, and you'll let me know.

## Production notes for HeyGen

1. **Avatar consistency.** Use the same avatar for V1–V9. The principal will see them as one voice over 24 hours. Switching avatars breaks that.
2. **Pacing.** All scripts are written to be spoken **unhurried**. The 60-second scripts run 65–70 seconds at natural pace. Don't try to speed them up — the pauses are doing emotional work.
3. **Tone selection in HeyGen.** Pick "warm, professional, conversational." Not "energetic." Not "enthusiastic." This is a tired principal — she will hear sales-energy as a wall.
4. **Subtitles.** Turn on. Some of her staff may end up watching these too, and they may prefer subtitles in Mandarin.
5. **Render quality.** Maximum. This is the principal's first impression of Montree's polish.
6. **Bilingual version.** For V2, V3, V4, V6, V7, V9 — generate a Mandarin version too. If she's bilingual, send the Mandarin version. Or send both and let her pick.
7. **Total credit cost.** At 8 credits per video × 9 core videos × 2 languages = ~144 credits for the full library. On the Creator tier (660 credits/month) this is one quarter of a month. Reasonable.

## What I am NOT giving you scripts for

Three things I deliberately left out. You should NOT make videos for these.

1. **Comparing Montree to competitors by name.** If she asks "how does this compare to Montessori Compass?" — you answer in person, off the cuff. A video comparing yourself to a named competitor lives on the internet forever and looks petty.
2. **A "founder's story" video.** Tempting, but it pulls focus toward you and away from her. She doesn't care about your origin story until *after* she's signed up. Save it for the onboarding sequence.
3. **A pricing-deep-dive video.** Pricing should be one sentence in V7 and one paragraph in V8a. Anything longer turns the conversation into "is this affordable?" instead of "is this worth it?" Wrong frame.
