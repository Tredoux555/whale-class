# Montree — The Four Masterpieces

> Four anchor videos, composed back-to-front. Each one earns its closing line. Each one hits hard. Each one can stand alone.

---

## The Composition Method

You write back to front. Your last line is your first line. The most powerful sentence is the one everything else builds toward.

So I started with your closing — the line that already does all the work:

> **"Helping people. Making people's lives easier. This is the philosophy behind Montree. This is the way forward."**

That is Masterpiece I. Every line before it earns it.

The other three masterpieces are built the same way. Each one has a closing line that lives in your text already, and each line above it is chosen so that, by the time the closing arrives, it lands like a hammer.

The 12 shorts in `montree-launch-playbook.html` are the feeder content — quick punches for the algorithm. **These four are the anchors.** They go on YouTube long-form, LinkedIn, the website hero, cold-email embeds, and the conference reel. They are what people remember.

---

## How To Use This File

- **Each masterpiece script is paste-ready for HeyGen Script-to-Video.** Triple-click the script block, copy, paste into HeyGen.
- **Each script has production direction in `[brackets]`** — visual cues, music notes, pace marks. Strip those before pasting if HeyGen reads them aloud (it can — check the first render).
- **Production specs at the bottom of each masterpiece** — avatar, voice, music, B-roll, aspect ratio, credit estimate.
- **Use the same continuity avatar and voice across all four.** This is one author's voice telling four chapters of the same story.

# MASTERPIECE I — *The Way Forward*

**The brand anthem. The philosophy. The thesis.**

This one builds from the biggest, most universal idea (every great business helps people) and narrows down to the specific (this is what Montree does, and that's why it exists). Then the closing returns to the philosophy — but now it means something, because the product earned it.

**Run-time:** ~90 seconds · **Words:** ~210 · **HeyGen credit estimate:** 12

---

## Script

> [Open: extreme close-up of a child's hands placing the third cylinder block into its hole. Soft ambient classroom sound. No music yet.]

> [Cut to wide angle. The teacher in the background, watching.]

**Think about the biggest businesses on Earth.**

> [Cut to abstract: Uber logo, Google logo, a hospital corridor — fast cuts, fade to black between each.]

**Uber. Google. The pharmaceutical companies. The platforms that lasted.**

> [Black frame. Beat.]

**What do they all have in common?**

> [Single word on screen, white on black, Lora serif:]
> ### They help people.

> [Fade. Music begins — soft, slow, building.]

**They help people. They make lives easier.**

**That's it. That's the whole formula.**

> [Cut to Tredoux at a window, soft natural light. Half-body, looking off-camera.]

**Three years ago, building something like Montree would have cost ten million dollars and a team of engineers working for three years.**

**I built it in six months. As one teacher. For under a thousand dollars.**

> [On-screen text, sparse: "1 teacher · 6 months · <$1,000"]

**This is what AI lets us do now.**

> [Cut to: phone snapping a photo of a child working. Then phone screen showing the work identified. Then a parent reading the report on their phone.]

**A teacher takes a photo. Montree does the rest.**

**The system identifies the work. Logs it to the child. Writes a beautiful description for the parents.**

**Twelve languages. Every Montessori classroom in the world, in its own language.**

> [Music swells slightly. Cut back to Tredoux, direct to camera.]

**This is not a product launch.**

**This is the start of something.**

> [Beat. Hold on his face. The music gets quieter for the closing.]

**Helping teachers. Making parents understand their children. Letting children thrive.**

**This is the philosophy behind Montree.**

> [Final visual: Montree sprout logo, gold on deep forest green, slow fade in. Hold for 3 seconds.]

### This is the way forward.

> [Smaller text below the logo:]
> *montree.xyz*

# MASTERPIECE II — *Built By One*

**The founder story. The disruption story. The vision.**

This one is more personal. More intimate. Almost documentary. The opening line strips away every reason to dismiss what you've done. The closing reaches for something far bigger than Montree.

**Run-time:** ~2 minutes · **Words:** ~280 · **HeyGen credit estimate:** 15

---

## Script

> [Open black. Single warm light comes up on Tredoux at a desk. Code on a laptop screen reflected in the window behind him. Not glamorous. Real.]

> [Direct to camera. Quiet.]

**I am not a team of coders.**

**I do not have ten million dollars.**

**I do not have a Stanford degree, a Y Combinator badge, or a Silicon Valley exit.**

> [Beat. He almost smiles.]

**I am one teacher.**

> [Cut to: black-and-white classroom footage. Children at work. A teacher kneeling beside them.]

**Ten years inside a Montessori classroom.**

**Six months at this desk.**

**Under a thousand dollars in API credits.**

> [On-screen text appears one line at a time, like a receipt:]
> *1 teacher*
> *6 months*
> *< $1,000*

**That's what Montree cost to build.**

> [Cut to: Tredoux walking through a classroom. Hand grazing a Pink Tower as he passes.]

**Three years ago this would have been impossible. Ten million dollars. A team of engineers. Three years of development.**

**Today, one teacher can build the future of an industry.**

> [Beat.]

**This is what people mean when they say AI is disruptive.**

> [Cut to: rotating product shots — Montree work identification, parent report, Astra interface, twelve languages flashing on screen.]

**But Montree is not the destination.**

**It is the stepping stone.**

> [Music shifts. Softer strings. Cut to: South African landscape. Children walking to school. A child's hands.]

**Since I was fourteen years old I've wanted to build a school.**

**Not a school like the ones that exist. A school that teaches every skill a child needs to thrive — physical, mental, spiritual. To create their own ecosystem.**

**Not to get a job.**

**To build a life.**

> [Hold on the South African landscape.]

**Right now those schools only exist for the obscenely rich.**

**Mine will start in South Africa. For local children. Who deserve more.**

> [Cut back to Tredoux at the desk. Direct to camera. The most personal moment of the piece.]

**Montree is how I get there.**

> [Music builds.]

**Ten years from now this is the standard.**

**The only question is whether your school is early.**

**Or whether your school is late.**

> [Cut to: a wave rolling on a coastline. Then: a surfer rising on it.]

**Climb on.**

**Surf the wave.**

### Don't get crushed by it.

> [Fade to Montree sprout logo. Music resolves.]

> *montree.xyz*

# MASTERPIECE III — *A Teacher Takes a Photo*

**The product anthem. Intimate. Cinematic. Almost a poem.**

This one is the opposite of a product demo. It does not explain features. It shows the *moment*. A child working. A teacher watching. A photo. And then everything cascades from that one action. The closing returns to where it started — the teacher. Teaching.

**Run-time:** ~75 seconds · **Words:** ~170 · **HeyGen credit estimate:** 10

---

## Script

> [Open: extreme close-up. A child's hands placing a brown stair, fourth piece. Their breath audible. No music yet.]

**A child works.**

> [Pull back slowly. Reveal the teacher in soft focus behind the child. Watching.]

**A teacher watches.**

> [Cut to the teacher's face. She's calm. Thirty seconds is what she has.]

**She has thirty seconds before she's needed somewhere else.**

> [The teacher raises her phone. Quietly. Doesn't speak. The child doesn't look up.]

**She takes a photo.**

> [Click. Soft camera shutter sound.]

**That's it.**

> [Beat. The teacher pockets her phone and walks away. The child keeps working.]

> [Cut to phone screen, dark UI, work identified — "Brown Stair · presented."]

**Montree does the rest.**

**The system reads the photo. Identifies the work. Logs it to the child.**

> [Cut to: a parent at home. On their phone. Reading. Their face softens.]

**Later, that same photo becomes a description for the parent.**

**What their child did. Why it matters.**

**In language any parent understands.**

> [Cut to: a clock spinning. Hours released. Calendar pages flipping.]

**Every week, fifteen hours come back to the teacher.**

> [Cut back to the same classroom. Same teacher. Now kneeling beside a different child, hand on their work, in conversation. Music swells gently.]

**To do what only she can do.**

> [Beat. Hold on her face.]

**Teach.**

> [Fade to Montree sprout logo.]

**The magic of Montree.**

### A teacher takes a photo. Montree does the rest.

> [Smaller text:]
> *Twelve languages. Thirty days free. No credit card.*

# MASTERPIECE IV — *The Founding Agents*

**The call to arms. Personal invitation. The offer that disappears.**

**Listen.**

**And I want to share it with the schools I cannot reach myself.**

> ### Founding Agents Program
> ### 20% of every school you sign
> ### Forever

**Twenty percent of every school you bring in. Forever. For as long as that school uses Montree, you get paid.**

**This is not a kickback. This is a partnership.**

> [Cut to: numbers landing on screen one at a time, like math being shown on a whiteboard:]

# How To Produce These — The HeyGen Workflow

Same as the 12 shorts but with three differences worth knowing.

> **Credit caveat.** HeyGen's Creator plan typically bills ~8 credits per Script-to-Video render up to ~60 seconds, scaling roughly linearly above that. The estimates throughout this doc (10–15 credits for the masterpieces) assume that scaling. **Verify on HeyGen's pricing page before queuing a long render** — if the actual cost differs, scale your supporting shorts up or down to match your 146-credit budget.

## 1. Render time will be longer.

A 30-second video renders in ~2 minutes. A 90-second masterpiece can take 4–6 minutes. A 2-minute Masterpiece II can hit 8–10 minutes. **Don't queue them all at once and walk away** — HeyGen sometimes silently fails on longer renders, and you want to catch that early.

**With only 46 credits available**, the play is: render Masterpiece II in 16:9 first (~15 credits). Watch it through. Sleep on it. Then decide whether to render the vertical cut (~15 more credits) or pivot the remaining 16 credits to something else. See the "46-credit reality" section below for the full allocation plan.

## 2. B-roll matters more than the avatar.

In a 30-second short, the avatar carries 60% of the screen time. In a 90-second masterpiece, the avatar should be on screen for 25–35% of the time. The rest is B-roll. HeyGen has a stock B-roll library, but for the masterpieces it's worth:

- Pulling real classroom footage from your existing Whale Class media (your Supabase has hundreds of photos and likely some video)
- Recording 30 seconds of yourself at the window with iPhone (for Masterpiece II's most personal moments)
- Capturing 10 seconds of South African landscape from a stock library (Pexels has free 4K Cape Town and Drakensberg footage)

You can edit the B-roll into HeyGen's output in Premiere, DaVinci Resolve (free), or even iMovie afterward.

## 3. Render 16:9 first, then 9:16.

For the masterpieces, the horizontal version is the canonical one. The vertical cut-down for shorts is the secondary. This is opposite to the 12 shorts in the playbook (where vertical is canonical).

**Why:** People watch masterpieces at full attention — on YouTube, on LinkedIn, embedded on a website. Vertical is for the algorithm scroll. The masterpieces are for the *commitment* viewer.

# HeyGen Via Web Claude — End-to-End Workflow

The prompts below let Claude in Chrome drive HeyGen for you. **Two prompts only**: one to set up the continuity avatar + voice (do this once), then one render prompt you reuse for each of the four masterpieces and the supporting shorts.

> **Realistic friction.** Web Claude is good at navigation, clicking, pasting, and toggling settings. It is not good at aesthetic choices that need your eyes and ears (which avatar feels right, which voice sounds like Tredoux, which background music carries the emotion). The setup prompt below offers candidates and waits for you to choose. After that, the render prompt is mechanical.

> **Credit safety.** Tell Claude *never* to click Generate without your explicit "go." HeyGen bills credits at render time, not at planning. One stray click can burn 12 credits on the wrong settings.

> **The Video Agent trap.** Do NOT let Claude open Video Agent — it consumes credits during the planning phase (you burned ~54 credits this way in Session 68). The correct path is **Create Video → Avatar Video → Script to Video** (sometimes labeled "Studio" or "Builder" depending on HeyGen's current UI).

If Masterpiece II lands *immediately* and feels clean — no re-render needed — you still have 16 credits to play with. That's roughly:
- One more masterpiece in 16:9 (Masterpiece I or III, ~10–12 credits), OR
- Two supporting shorts (~16 credits, V07 Astra + V08 The Magic — the two strongest from the playbook), OR
- Hold the 16 credits for the next batch.

### What NOT to do with 46 credits

- **Don't try to render all four masterpieces.** Even just both aspects of two of them blows past 46.
- **Don't render the supporting shorts before the anchor.** The anchor (Masterpiece II) is what you build the rollout around. Shorts without it are decoration.
- **Don't burn a render on Masterpiece I or III first.** Both are excellent, but neither carries the personal stake that makes II travel. Save them for when credits replenish.
- **Don't render two aspects of one masterpiece without watching the first.** If something needs fixing in the script or voice, you don't want to discover it after burning 30 credits on two versions of the wrong take.
