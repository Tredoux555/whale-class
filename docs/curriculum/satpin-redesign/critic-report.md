# Adversarial Critic Report — Dark Phonics SATPIN Readers, Design Bible v2

**Reviewer stance:** try to break it before the author does. I re-decoded every printed child-owned word letter-by-letter against the cumulative pool (wk1 s · wk2 +a · wk3 +t · wk4 +p · wk5 +i · wk6 +n; glue `a` wk2+, `is/it` wk5, `an/in/I` wk6), then attacked naturalness, the ah-moment, research compliance, voice, and art.

## Headline
**The decodability hard gate PASSES.** No child ever owns an illegal word in any of the six books. I re-decoded all printed child text (wk3 `Sat`/`at`; wk4 `Sat/Sap/Pat/Tap/SPAT/A`; wk5 `Sit/It/is/a/pit/tip/Sip/Spit/sits`; wk6 `An/ant/naps/in/a/pan/tin/SNAP/Nip/I/nap/it`) — every letter is inside its slot's pool. The design also correctly refuses illegal glue ("no `on`/`in` legal yet" at wk4 p9). **There are no BLOCKERS.** The kills are on naturalness, one plot hole, one reintroduced picture-guessing trap, and bookkeeping/voice — all fixable without a redesign.

## NUMBERED FINDINGS

### 1. MAJOR — wk3, all spreads (p1–p5) — the "At the park?" / "Sat!" non-sequitur
The teacher asks a yes/no location question ("Where is Segina? **At** the park?") and the child's decoded reply is `Sat!`. Grammatically `Sat!` is not an answer to "at the park?" — this is precisely the flagged "Nan can fan Dan" failure in dialogue form. A 4-year-old parses "At the park?" as a question expecting yes/no, not "she sat here and left." The meaning survives only because the art (dent, ribbon) rescues it; the words alone are a non-sequitur.
**Fix (child word unchanged, legal):** rewrite the read-aloud narration so `Sat!` completes it. p1 "Where is Segina? Here she…" → child `Sat!` (reads "Here she Sat!"); p2 "The zoo? She…" → `Sat!`, etc. Narration is readAloud-exempt, so any words are legal; the child's `Sat!` (s,a,t) stays intact.

### 2. MAJOR — wk3, p7 — internal plot hole ("the whole time")
Spreads 1–6 establish that Segina *just left* each location (warm dent, spinning stroller). Spread 7's SCENE says she was "asleep in her own red chair, having sat down and nodded off **the whole time**." If she was home asleep the whole time she could not have left warm dents across town five minutes earlier. The premise contradicts its own resolution.
**Fix:** delete "the whole time." Reframe p7 scene as "the class finally catches up — she got home, sat down, and nodded off." Restores the chase logic (she was always one step ahead, ending at home). No printed-text change; `Sat.` (s,a,t) stays.

### 3. MAJOR — wk5, p4 — "tip" (rubbish dump) is outside a preschooler's / US oral vocabulary
`It is a tip!` uses *tip* = garbage heap, a British/Australian sense absent from a US 4-year-old's lexicon (they say "dump"). The child decodes a real word but bonds it to a meaning they don't hold — comprehension fails even though decoding succeeds (research Q1: vocabulary must be in the oral repertoire). Flagged candidate confirmed.
**Fix (decodable-legal):** replace with `It is sap!` — `It`(i,t) `is`(i,s) `sap`(s,a,p, a wk4 word) — all legal ≤ wk5; art = cat stuck in a sticky sap heap (gross-cute callback to wk4, on-brand rule 6). Trade-off: drops one `a` and the single `tip` payoff exposure (both non-critical). If parallel "It is a ___!" phrasing must be preserved, note that the only pool-legal concrete nouns at wk5 are pit/tip/sap, so `sap` is the sole natural substitute.

### 4. MAJOR — wk6, p3 & p4 — predictable cumulative frame + directly-depicted noun = the picture-guessing trap the redesign exists to kill
`An ant naps in a pan.` (art: an ant in a pan) and `An ant naps in a tin.` (art: an ant in a tin) put a highly labelable noun as the changing slot of a fixed, predictable frame, with the noun *shown in the picture*. This is exactly the Level-A patterned-book affordance research Q2 warns against: a child reads "An ant naps in a ___" and lifts pan/tin off the picture instead of decoding it. It also violates the design's own Rule 3 (art shows consequence, never the labelable object that IS the word) — here the art IS the label. The design's Rule 2 (payoff word = visual punchline) and Rule 3 (never depict the target) collide on these two spreads.
**Fix:** (a) enforce the "cover the right page, decode left, then reveal" model with an explicit teacher note on these spreads; and/or (b) make the pan/tin art carry a *consequence* beyond the bare object (the ant's absurd blissful comfort, other kitchen chaos) so the noun is not the sole recoverable content. Text stays legal; the risk is pedagogical, not decodability.

### 5. MAJOR — wk2, all spreads — the book conflates "reading the heart word `a`" with the oral /a/ sound-hunt, and prints no decodable `a`
The premise says the child's job every page is "read `a`," and the ledger claims heart word `a` "read 8× (first bank of exposures)." But no page prints a bare `a`: pages print `Ah`, `AH!`, and `An` (the latter using **n, untaught until wk6**). This is a readAloud soundHunt, so nothing is illegal — but a teacher following the doc will hunt for "a" to point at and find "An," and the exposure ledger banks 8 *oral* /a/ productions as if they were reading decodes. The child's real task here is producing the /a/ sound, not reading a word.
**Fix:** restate the child's job as "hunt/say the /a/ sound" (oral), and relabel the ledger "heart-word `a` previewed orally (readAloud — not genuine decodes)." `a`'s real decode budget is banked wk4 p9 + wk5 + wk6 and clears comfortably, so nothing downstream breaks — this is an honesty/clarity fix.

### 6. MINOR — wk3, title — "Where Did Segina Sit?" uses untaught `i` and the wrong tense
The title prints `Sit` (i untaught until wk5) while the entire book teaches `Sat`. It's readAloud-exempt so technically allowed, but it wastes the market pattern of a decodable title and contradicts the book's own hero word.
**Fix:** retitle `Segina Sat!` — "Segina" is a cast name (exempt), `Sat` (s,a,t) is fully decodable at wk3, turning the title into the child's first decoding win.

### 7. MINOR — wk6, p8 — "I nap in it!" text/art preposition mismatch
Text says the ant naps "**in** it" (in isn't the issue — `on` is illegal, no o) but the art shows the ant sprawled *on top of* the cat's head. Text and picture disagree.
**Fix:** art note — the ant flops back into the dented **tin** now sitting on the cat's head, so "I nap in it!" literally means "in the tin." Text unchanged (all legal); `in` exposure preserved (needed — see finding 12).

### 8. MINOR — wk5, p6 — "Is it? Sip it." is cryptic as language
Decoded alone, "Is it? Sip it." doesn't convey the scene (cat ignoring the mat and daintily sipping tea); "Sip it" as an imperative has no clear speaker. Meaning lives in the art. Borderline but the weakest comprehension line in an otherwise strong book.
**Fix:** acceptable under the "art carries story" thesis; if tightening, `Sip! Sip!` (a true rule-of-three-adjacent double, cat sipping) reads more naturally and both words are legal.

### 9. MINOR — wk4 — "sap" and title "SPAT!" are borderline oral vocabulary
*Sap* (tree resin) is marginal for a 4-year-old and `SPAT!` reads as a subjectless past-tense verb rather than natural onomatopoeia. Both are mitigated by strong art and are pool-forced (SPIT needs i, untaught at wk4). Keep, but be aware the climax word is the least intuitive decode in the strongest book.

### 10. MINOR — wk4, p9 — the /p/-week potato-flip variation is left on the table
Voice bible Rule 2 sanctions a variation *specifically for the /p/ week*: "POTATO" finally starts with the just-taught target sound, so the gag flips (class shouts "YES?!"). wk4 is the /p/ week and runs the standard blank-noun gag instead, wasting a documented, delightful, on-theme beat.
**Fix (optional):** on wk4 p9 lean into "…starts with /p/! (YES?!) …still not the answer" per the bible.

### 11. MINOR — wk4, p8 — labelled a "rule of three" but it isn't one
`Sat! Sap! Tap!` is three *different* words; voice Rule 1 (and the design's own Rule 4.1) is tripling ONE payload word. Same slippage on p3 `Sap! Sat! Sat!`. Fine as a callback-montage, but it is not the house rule-of-three the design claims to be executing.
**Fix (optional):** if a true triple is wanted, `Tap! Tap! Tap!` (cat taps Sam thrice, echoing p5) is legal.

### 12. MINOR — Rule 2 cross-week recurrence promise is unmet for wk5/wk6 payoff words
Rule 2 defers payoff-word exposures to "cross-week recurrence" ("a word introduced in wk4 must reappear in wk5 and wk6"). But wk6 is terminal, so its payoff words (`pan`, `tin`, `nip`, `snap`) have no future week and land 1× in-book only; and wk5's payoff words (`sip/tip/pit/spit`) are **not** in wk6's REVIEW flashcard list (only `sit/it/is` recur), so their promised recurrence never happens. Likewise `pat/tap/sap` recur in wk5's review but not wk6's. The lifetime-exposure claim (~6–10) is not actually delivered for these words.
**Fix:** add the wk5 payoff words to wk6's REVIEW bank / materials, and lean on the materials layer (flashcards, sentence cards) explicitly for wk6's own payoff words since no later book exists.

### 13. MINOR — Cross-cutting art / style coherence: reusing OLD hosted webp for wk1–wk2 breaks the locked style
The design offers wk1–wk2 object pages (and the wk1/wk2 Potato, wk2 Ant) as REUSE of the hosted webp inventory, which is in the OLD spotlit-photo style. Shipping wk1–2 in photo style beside wk3–6 pen-and-ink violates the locked style (brief constraint 9, "do not restyle") and means Ant's first appearance (wk2, photo) mismatches his canonical sheet (wk6, pen-ink). The design's own Rule 6 wants series unity.
**Fix:** treat the pen-and-ink re-render (which the design already provides prompts for) as mandatory, not "interim"; the webp reuse belongs only in the materials/flashcard layer.

### 14. MINOR — Omni-reference plan is incomplete for the Potato at wk3 p8 and wk5 p9
Potato appears in every book's final page, but `--oref Potato` is stated only for wk4 p9 and wk6 p9. wk3 p8 and the wk5 p9 "NEW" option (cat lifting to reveal the potato) don't name the Potato (and wk5 p9 also omits the Cat) sheet references.
**Fix:** add `--oref Potato` (+ Cat for wk5 p9) to those NEW renders.

### 15. MINOR — wk3, p8 — "POTATO!" sits in the CHILD READS column
Listing the shouted reveal `POTATO!` under "CHILD READS" invites a teacher to think the child *decodes* the illegal word POTATO (o's, never legal). Rule 9 intends it as the blank-page class shout from the picture.
**Fix:** move it to a "class shouts (blank + picture)" note; the child decodes nothing on the potato page.

### 16. MINOR — wk5 lacks a clean mid-arc reversal (voice Rule 8)
wk4 (cat taps fallen Sam "fair is fair") and wk6 (ant nips the cat) land clear comeuppances. wk5's arc — cat defies, spits at Sam, then finally complies to applause — has no clear "smallest wins / smug one gets got" turn; the cat is both the smug one and the eventual winner-by-compliance. The book under-uses Rule 8.
**Fix (optional):** stage p7 `Spit!` so the cat's insolence triggers its own comedic downfall on p8, or give Sam a small win.

### 17. MINOR — wk6, p4 art note references drawn letters
The table art-direction for p4 says "tiny 'z's implied by pose only." The C.2 prompt correctly omits them and the locked suffix forbids letters, but the table note could mislead an illustrator into adding z's.
**Fix:** change the note to "sleep conveyed by pose only, no z's."

## AXIS SUMMARY

- **A. Decodability (hard gate):** PASS. Every child-decoded word re-verified letter-by-letter as legal. Teacher/child split is clean in wk3 (two columns). Only ambiguities: wk3 p8 POTATO-in-child-column (#15) and wk2's "read `a`" that prints no `a` (#5).
- **B. Naturalness / comprehension:** the main casualties — wk3 Q/A non-sequitur (#1) and plot hole (#2), wk5 "tip" (#3), wk5 p6 cryptic line (#8), wk4 "sap/SPAT" (#9), wk6 "in it" (#7). "nip" (wk6) and "pit/sip" survive scrutiny.
- **C. Ah-moment:** strong overall; hardest decode does land on the biggest beat (SPAT, Spit, SNAP, the SOCK drop). Scores below.
- **D. Research compliance:** target-word budgets independently recounted and all clear ≥5 in-book (wk4 sat/sap/pat/tap = 5 each; wk5 sit 5 / it 6+ / is 5; wk6 ant 6 / an 6 / naps 5 / in 5). Failures: recurrence promise unmet for payoff words (#12) and the wk6 picture-guessing trap (#4). Market patterns (decodable titles, one line/page, NEW/REVIEW flag) largely honored except wk3 title (#6).
- **E. Voice:** whisper→SHOUT, call-and-response, potato ritual, blank last noun, deadpan absurdity, mild grossness all present every book. Wasted/broken: /p/-week potato-flip (#10), mislabeled rule-of-three (#11), wk5 missing reversal (#16).
- **F. Art prompts:** locked suffix matches the brief VERBATIM and is appended to every prompt; cast descriptions match locked specs; no prompt requests text/letters. Gaps: style-coherence of reused webp (#13), incomplete Potato omni-ref (#14), the p4 z-note (#17).
