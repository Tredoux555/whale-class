# The English Corner — Curriculum Revamp

**An authentic Montessori path to reading and writing, tuned for 3–6 year-olds
learning English as an additional language (EAL).**

*Working document for the Montree English curriculum revamp. Pairs with the
existing 128-lesson Pink/Blue/Green progression in
`lib/montree/english-sequence/lesson-map.ts` — this document is the
*preparation science underneath that scheme* and the plan for the physical
corner and its independent materials.*

---

## Base summary — the whole thing on one page

The goal of the English corner is **not "to teach English."** It is to prepare
the child so completely — the hand, the ear, the eye, and above all the spoken
vocabulary — that reading and writing **emerge from the child** almost
inevitably, as fruit, not as a lesson drilled in. This is the core difference
between the Montessori method and a phonics programme: phonics is *what* you
teach; the Montessori sequence is the *biological order of preparation* that
makes the phonics land.

Three principles govern everything below:

1. **Writing comes before reading.** The child's impulse to *encode* (push out
   what they want to say) arrives before the ability to *decode* (take in
   someone else's words). So we let them build words with a moveable alphabet —
   "writing" — before they can hold a pencil or read a book. Reading then
   emerges from their own writing.

2. **Everything is prepared indirectly, long before any letter appears.** The
   pincer grip that will one day hold a pencil is built by buttoning a frame and
   lifting beads with tongs. The left-to-right, top-to-bottom motion of reading
   is patterned by *how every material on the shelf is used*. The lightness of
   touch for handwriting is built on sandpaper boards. The child is being
   prepared for writing for a year before they meet a letter — and never knows
   it.

3. **For EAL, spoken language is the long pole.** A child cannot decode, or
   read for meaning, a word they have never heard or understood. A native-English
   room can assume the oral vocabulary is already there; **our corner cannot.**
   So the English corner front-loads spoken-language enrichment far more heavily
   than a standard Montessori primary, and holds the child in the oral and sound
   stages longer before symbols appear. This is the single most important
   adaptation in this document.

**The scientific chain** (the "top-to-bottom, left-to-right" method the revamp
is built on) is really one long chain of preparations, each making the next
possible:

> control of movement + the pincer grip *(Practical Life)*
> → refined senses, lightness of touch, and left-right / top-bottom orientation *(Sensorial + the way every material is worked)*
> → a rich English **spoken** vocabulary *(Spoken Language — the EAL heartland)*
> → **hearing the separate sounds inside words** *(Sound Games / phonemic awareness — the ~40 English sounds)*
> → **linking each sound to its symbol by touch** *(Sandpaper Letters — taught by sound, not name)*
> → **building words before holding a pencil** *(the Moveable Alphabet — this is "writing")*
> → forming the letters with control *(Metal Insets → handwriting)*
> → **reading emerges** *(Pink → Blue → Green series)*
> → total reading: fluency, comprehension, and reading to learn.

Every stage carries a built-in **control of error** so the child can work
**independently** and correct themselves without an adult — which is what makes
the corner run itself, and what makes the work quietly enjoyable rather than
test-like.

## Part 0 — Readers first: working backwards from the end

You asked to start at the reader and work back. Good — because the sequence only
makes sense when you see it as a chain of prerequisites running *backwards* from
the goal. Build the chain in reverse and you build the curriculum.

**What is a fluent, independent reader (our actual end goal)?** A child who can:
(a) **decode** any regular word by sound, (b) recognise the irregular
"puzzle words" at sight, and (c) **read for meaning** — understand what the
words say, not just bark at them.

Now walk it backwards. Each link cannot exist without the one beneath it:

| To do this… | …the child first needs this | …which is built by |
|---|---|---|
| **Read for meaning** | To already *know the word* in spoken English | Spoken Language (vocabulary) |
| **Decode any word** | Sound↔symbol for all ~40+ graphemes (incl. digraphs, phonograms) | Pink → Blue → Green reading series |
| **Read at all** | To have *written/built* words themselves first (encoding before decoding) | The Moveable Alphabet |
| **Build a word** | To know which symbol matches each sound | Sandpaper Letters (sound, not name) |
| **Match sound to symbol** | To *hear the separate sounds inside a word* | Sound Games (phonemic awareness) |
| **Hear sounds in words** | A rich bank of English words worth analysing | Spoken Language (the EAL long pole) |
| **Hold any of it** | A controlled hand, a discriminating eye, left→right / top→bottom movement | Practical Life + Sensorial (indirect prep) |

Read that table bottom-to-top and you have the **forward teaching order** —
exactly the order of the parts below. The reason EAL changes the picture is that
two rows do the heavy lifting and must be deliberately *widened*: the **Spoken
Language** rows (the child has less English to begin with) and, slightly, the
**Sound Games** row (English has sounds their home language may not — and Montree
already notes the Mandarin-L1 articulation issues in the Pink lessons).

The rest of this document is each link in that chain, in forward order: the
science of *why*, what it looks like on the shelf, the EAL tuning, the
**independent materials you can make** for it, the control of error, and how it
maps onto Montree's existing 128-lesson scheme.

## Part 1 — The hidden foundation: indirect preparation

*The hand, the eye, and the direction of movement — built before any letter.*

**The science.** Montessori observed that you cannot teach a child to write by
starting with writing. The capacities that writing requires — a controlled
three-finger grip, a light and steady touch, the ability to follow a left-to-right
and top-to-bottom path, and the visual discrimination to tell *b* from *d* —
are built **indirectly**, through work that has nothing overtly to do with
literacy. By the time the child meets a letter, the body is already ready.

This is the "top-to-bottom, left-to-right" you asked about. It is not taught as a
rule; it is **patterned into the child by how every material is used**. Each
work is taken from the shelf left-to-right, worked top-to-bottom, and returned —
hundreds of times a week — until the orientation of reading and writing is
literally a motor habit before it is ever a literacy instruction.

**Two areas feed the English corner:**

**Practical Life** builds the *hand and the sequence.* The pincer/tripod grip
that will hold a pencil is forged by: dressing frames (buttons, zips, buckles,
bows), tonging and tweezing small objects, using an eye-dropper, spooning and
pouring, threading beads, and using pegs. Each also builds **order, sequence, and
concentration** — a child must do the steps in order, left to right — which is
the same control reading and writing demand. (AMI is explicit that practical-life
and sensorial work is the indirect preparation for handwriting.)

**Sensorial** builds the *eye and the touch.* The child learns to discriminate
fine visual differences (size, shape, dimension — the knobbed cylinders, which
also demand the exact pencil grip), and learns the **lightness of touch** for
handwriting on the rough-and-smooth and touch boards. Crucially, sensorial work
sharpens the visual discrimination that lets a child later see that *was* and
*saw* are different — the same faculty, trained on shapes first.

**EAL tuning.** This stage is **language-light, so it is the perfect on-ramp for
a child with little English.** A 3-year-old who can barely follow an English
instruction can still button a frame and pour rice — succeeding, concentrating,
and belonging from day one while their English grows in parallel. Use these works
as the child's confident home base, and narrate them in simple English ("button…
push… through…") so practical-life time doubles as gentle oral-language exposure.

**Independent materials you can make (enjoyable, self-correcting):**

- **Pincer baskets** — small trays: tongs + pom-poms to sort by colour into an
  ice tray; tweezers + beads; pegs around the rim of a tin. *Control of error:*
  pom-poms fall / don't fit if grip is wrong; the child sees it.
- **Threading & lacing cards** — punched cards the child laces left-to-right.
  *Control of error:* the lace runs out or skips a hole.
- **Dressing frames** (or a "busy board" of fasteners on a cheap picture frame).
- **Transfer trays** — spooning/pouring dry beans or water between vessels; a
  sponge-squeezing tray. *Control of error:* spills are visible and the child
  wipes them (more practical life).
- **Sandpaper "touch" boards** — rough vs smooth strips to stroke with two
  fingers, building the exact light touch the sandpaper letters will need.
- **Top-to-bottom, left-to-right trays** — e.g. a vertical-line "rake the sand"
  tray, or chips moved across a frame L→R, quietly drilling the reading path.

**Montree mapping.** This stage sits *before* Pink Lesson 1. In the Pink page,
Lessons 1–4 are reserved for "pre-reading review" (rhyming, alphabet sequence,
sound matching, concepts of print) — the bridge out of pure indirect prep into
explicit sound work. The practical-life/sensorial works themselves can live as
**photo-audit-able "readiness" works** so Montree can show a child is being
prepared even before Lesson 5's first letter-sound.

## Part 2 — Spoken language: the EAL heartland

*You cannot read a word you have never heard. This is where our corner spends
its richest time.*

**The science.** All written language rests on spoken language. In Montessori,
the child arrives at the symbols already in possession of thousands of words and
the sounds that compose them; the literacy work merely *encodes* what the child
already says. For an EAL child this base is thin in English — so we must **build
the spoken layer deliberately and generously** before and alongside everything
else. Every later stage borrows from the vocabulary you bank here.

**The method — the Three-Period Lesson.** This is the engine of vocabulary, and
the most important teaching technique in the corner. To give a child a new English
word (say with a real object or a picture card):

1. **Naming —** "This is a *frog*." (Clear, isolated, repeated. No questions.)
2. **Recognition / association —** "Show me the *frog*. Put the *frog* on the
   shelf. Give the *frog* to me." (Lots of playful repetition; the child acts,
   doesn't have to speak yet — perfect for a shy EAL child.)
3. **Recall —** "What is this?" (Only when ready. If the child hesitates, drop
   back to period 2 — never a test, never a failure.)

This three-step structure is precisely what makes the technique so effective for
second-language learning: it separates *understanding* from *producing*, so the
child builds comprehension long before they're asked to speak.

**The materials that carry it (in rough order of richness):**

- **Real objects** → **miniature objects** → **matched object-to-picture** →
  **picture cards (classified/nomenclature cards)** → **three-part cards**
  (picture, label, control card) once reading begins.
- **Classified cards by theme** — farm animals, fruit, kitchen, clothing,
  vehicles, the classroom itself. Themes the child can *touch and live*, not
  abstract lists.
- **Enrichment of vocabulary** — not just nouns: verbs (jumping, pouring),
  qualities (big/small, rough/smooth — borrowed straight from sensorial),
  prepositions (on/under/in — vital and easy to act out), and the language of
  the classroom.
- **Conversation, stories, songs, rhymes, poetry** — daily. Rhyme and song are
  disproportionately powerful for EAL: they smuggle in stress, rhythm, and the
  sound-shapes of English, and they pre-load the phonemic-awareness stage.

**EAL tuning (the crux of the revamp).**

- **Hold the child here longer.** Do not rush to letters. Weeks of rich oral
  work — naming, matching, songs — is the investment that makes every later
  stage faster. A child with 300 English words to analyse will fly through sound
  games; a child with 30 will stall.
- **Comprehension before production.** Lean on Period 2 (acting, pointing,
  matching). Never force a nervous child to speak English to "prove" a word.
- **Anchor to the known.** Match English to objects/pictures the child can
  already name in their home language — meaning transfers, only the label is new.
- **Choose vocabulary that the Pink phonics needs.** Front-load words that are
  CVC and decodable later (cat, dog, pig, sun, cup, bed) so the oral vocabulary
  and the first reading words are the *same words* — the child later reads what
  they already say.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **Matching baskets** — a basket of 6 miniature objects + matching photo cards;
  child pairs them. *Control of error:* a self-check photo on the back, or a
  colour dot pairing.
- **Classified card sets** (themed nomenclature) — laminated picture + label
  cards. *Control of error:* a control booklet/sheet showing the correct pairs.
- **"Sound-friendly" object boxes** — themed boxes whose objects are all future
  Pink CVC words, so the same box is reused at the sound-game and reading stages.
- **Listening/song station** — Montree already holds per-word and per-letter
  audio (`curriculum/assets/audio/elevenlabs-master/`); a tap-to-hear card set
  lets a child self-serve correct English pronunciation independently.

**Montree mapping.** This is where the EAL revamp adds the most. Today the
sequence begins explicit work at Pink L1–4 (pre-reading) → L5 (first letter).
The revamp inserts a **named "Spoken Language" pre-stage** ahead of L1 — a bank
of vocabulary/3-period/classified-card works the photo audit can recognise — so a
child's *oral* English readiness is tracked, and no child is advanced to symbols
before the spoken base exists. The existing word-audio assets become the
self-correcting backbone of these works.

## Part 3 — Sound games: hearing the sounds inside words

*The first explicit literacy work. Still no letters — purely the ear.*

**The science.** Before a symbol can mean anything, the child must be able to
**hear that a word is made of separate sounds** — that *cat* is /k/–/a/–/t/.
This is phonemic awareness, and in Montessori it is trained entirely by ear,
through **Sound Games** (the classic "I Spy"), with **no letters present at all.**
This is deliberate: we isolate the auditory skill so it is rock-solid before it
has to carry a visual symbol. English has **~40 sounds (phonemes)**, not 26 —
some written with one letter, some with two — and it is these *sounds*, not the
alphabet, that the child learns to detect.

**The progression within sound games (easy → hard):**

1. **Initial sound** — "I spy something beginning with /m/…" (mug, mat). Start
   here; it's the easiest to hear.
2. **Final sound** — "…something ending with /t/" (cat, hat).
3. **Medial / middle sound** — the vowel in the middle (hardest).
4. **Full segmenting / blending** — say all the sounds in a short word, and
   blend sounds back into a word. This is the direct doorway to the moveable
   alphabet.

Always say the **pure sound** — /m/, not "muh," and not the letter name "em."
A clipped, unvoiced consonant ("/t/", not "tuh") is what makes blending work
later; a sloppy "tuh" turns *cat* into "cuh-a-tuh."

**EAL tuning.**

- **English has sounds the home language doesn't.** A Mandarin-L1 child, for
  instance, has well-known difficulty with certain English contrasts — Montree
  already documents Mandarin-L1 articulation notes inside the Pink lesson HTML.
  Spend extra ear-time on the contrasts that don't exist in the child's first
  language *before* attaching them to letters.
- **Minimal pairs as a game** — pit two near-identical sounds against each other
  with objects (e.g. a *ship* and a *sheep*, a *pen* and a *pan*) so the ear
  learns the boundary playfully.
- **Lean on the songs and rhymes** banked in Part 2 — they've already primed the
  English sound system.
- **Use objects the child can name.** Sound games only work on known vocabulary —
  another reason the spoken stage comes first.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **Sound baskets / "/s/ basket"** — a basket of small objects that all start
  with the target sound; the child names and sorts. *Control of error:* all
  objects share the sound, so an odd one out is felt.
- **Initial-sound sorting mats** — 2–3 header objects/pictures, a pile to sort by
  starting sound. *Control of error:* colour-dot or picture self-check on the
  reverse.
- **Minimal-pair matching cards** (ship/sheep). *Control of error:* control card.
- **I-Spy object box** — the same future-CVC object boxes from Part 2, reused.
- **Montree's Sound Games (digital)** — the app already runs Sound Games from the
  curriculum assets; the physical baskets and the app reinforce each other (one
  is self-serve audio with a built-in correct answer, the other is hands-on).

**Montree mapping.** Sound games span the **pre-reading lessons (Pink L1–4:
rhyming, sound matching)** and continue *underneath* the early letter lessons —
a child should still be playing medial-sound games while meeting their first
sandpaper letters. Treat sound-game mastery (initial → final → medial) as the
**gate** that should be satisfied before a child is pushed past the first
handful of letters. This is a natural Montree "readiness flag."

## Part 4 — Sandpaper letters: the sound becomes a symbol

*Now, and only now, a letter appears — and it enters through the fingertips.*

**The science.** The child can now hear /m/; the sandpaper letter gives /m/ a
**body**. The letter is cut from sandpaper and mounted on a card; the child
**traces it with two fingers** (the same two fingers prepared on the touch
boards) while **saying its sound** — building the symbol simultaneously through
*touch, sight, movement, and sound*. Three things are non-negotiable and define
authentic practice:

1. **Taught by SOUND, not name.** It is "/m/", never "em." Names are useless for
   blending and come much later; the sound is what builds words.
2. **Traced, not just looked at.** The finger movement lays down the *motor
   memory of writing the letter* — this is the direct preparation for handwriting,
   happening before a pencil is involved. The tracing direction is the correct
   writing stroke.
3. **Colour-coded by type.** Classically: **vowels on blue, consonants on pink,**
   and **double-letter graphemes (digraphs/phonograms) on green** — the same
   colour logic that later organises the reading series.

The sandpaper letters are introduced a few at a time (not alphabetically), in an
order that lets the child **build real words as soon as possible** — which is
exactly why the modern SATPIN-style order exists.

**EAL tuning.**

- Keep **saying the sound aloud** as the finger traces — the simultaneous
  articulation is extra valuable for a child still mastering English sounds.
- Pair each new letter with a **known object** that starts with it (from the
  spoken-language bank), so the symbol attaches to meaning, not to nothing.
- Don't withhold the next letter for perfect articulation of a hard English
  sound — let the motor + visual memory build while the ear keeps maturing.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **DIY sandpaper letters** — letters cut from fine sandpaper, glued on stiff
  card, **blue for vowels, pink for consonants, green for digraphs**. (Lowercase;
  classically cursive, but **match whatever script Montree's letter assets and
  your handwriting model use** so the child sees one consistent form — see note
  below.)
- **Sound-and-object trays** — each sandpaper letter paired with a tiny object/
  picture starting with that sound.
- **Sand / salt tray** — after tracing the sandpaper letter, the child writes it
  in a tray of sand. *Control of error:* compare to the sandpaper letter; wipe
  and retry freely (low-stakes, very enjoyable).
- **Tap-to-hear letter cards** — using Montree's per-letter audio
  (`assets/audio/.../letters/`) so a child can self-check the correct sound
  independently. *Control of error:* the audio is the answer key.

> **One decision to make for the revamp: cursive vs print, and pure-sound vs
> letter-name.** Authentic AMI practice uses *cursive* sandpaper letters and
> *sounds only*. Montree's current assets and the UFLI-aligned Pink order are a
> print/structured-literacy lineage. Pick one script and one naming convention
> and keep the child's whole environment consistent — mixing print sandpaper
> letters with a cursive handwriting model (or teaching names and sounds at once)
> is the one thing that reliably confuses an early reader. My recommendation for
> an EAL corner: **print letters, pure sounds first** (names introduced later,
> separately), because it matches your existing phonics order and the world of
> English print the children will actually meet.

**Montree mapping.** Sandpaper-letter work *is* the felt, physical version of
**Pink L5–L31** (one new letter-sound per lesson, SATPIN order) — the digital
lesson and the tactile letter are two faces of the same step. The green-coded
digraphs (sh, ch, th, etc.) land at **Pink L42–46**.

## Part 5 — The Moveable Alphabet: writing before reading

*The hinge of the whole method. The child writes — builds words — before they
can read them or hold a pencil.*

**The science.** This is the stage that most surprises people and most defines
Montessori. Once a child knows a handful of sandpaper letters by sound and can
segment a short spoken word, you give them a box of cut-out letters — the
**Moveable Alphabet** — and they begin to **build words they choose**, by sound:
they say "cat," hear /k/–/a/–/t/, and lay down c–a–t. **This is writing
(encoding)** — the hand-and-pencil mechanics are simply removed so nothing stands
between the child's mind and the word.

Why it works: the child's drive to *push out* their own thoughts (encode) is
stronger and earlier than the patience to take in someone else's (decode). The
moveable alphabet rides that drive. Children often pour out word after word in a
burst Montessori called the **"explosion into writing."** And then — usually
weeks later, often spontaneously — the child reads back what they built, and
**reading is born out of their own writing.** We did not teach reading; we
prepared writing, and reading arrived on its own.

Note the spelling is *phonetic and "wrong"* at first (*kat*, *wos*) — and that is
**correct and celebrated.** It proves the child is encoding sound to symbol
independently. Accuracy comes later through the reading series; here, fluency of
encoding is the win.

**EAL tuning.**

- The moveable alphabet lets an EAL child **say it in English in their head and
  externalise it** without the double load of forming letters — powerful for
  building confidence.
- Start from the **CVC words you deliberately banked** in the spoken-language
  stage, so the first words built are words the child *knows and can picture*.
- Provide **object/picture prompts** in the box so the child always has a known
  word to build, never a blank.
- Welcome home-language thinking → English output: the child sees a picture they
  can name in either language and encodes the English sounds.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **A large moveable alphabet** — cut/printed letters in a compartment box,
  **vowels one colour, consonants another** (mirrors the sandpaper coding). DIY:
  laminated card tiles, or wooden tiles.
- **CVC building cards / object boxes** — a picture (or miniature) + space to
  build the word beneath. *Control of error:* the correct spelling on the card
  back, or a control strip.
- **Picture-and-build trays** themed to the banked vocabulary (animals, food).
- **A "writing" mat** — a simple felt strip that quietly enforces left-to-right
  placement.

**Montree mapping.** The moveable alphabet is the *productive* counterpart to the
**Pink reading series (CVC)** — the child builds Pink words before/while reading
them. As they progress it carries straight into **Blue** (blends, VCe) and
**Green** (phonograms): the same box, longer and more complex words. In Montree
terms, "built the word with the moveable alphabet" and "read the word" are two
trackable evidences of the *same* lesson number — and photo audit can capture a
child's moveable-alphabet work as proof of the encoding side of each lesson.

## Part 6 — Metal Insets & handwriting: the pencil, at last

*The hand learns to make the letters it already knows by sound and by touch.*

**The science.** Handwriting is split from composition so neither overwhelms the
child. The *thinking* part of writing was handled by the moveable alphabet; now
the **Metal Insets** handle the *mechanical* part — pencil control — as the
**direct preparation for handwriting**, on top of all the indirect practical-life
preparation. The child traces a metal shape with a pencil, then fills it with
**controlled, even, up-and-down strokes, lightly, moving left to right** — the
exact muscular control, density, and directionality handwriting needs, practised
on shapes before letters so there's no pressure to "get the letter right."

Letter formation itself then draws on the **motor memory already laid down by
tracing the sandpaper letters** — the child's fingers learned the stroke before
the pencil did. Large muscle before small; shapes before letters; letters before
words on paper.

**EAL tuning.** Handwriting is **language-neutral mechanics** — like Practical
Life, it's a stage where an EAL child competes on equal footing and can shine.
Keep it about control and enjoyment, not English. Let composition stay in the
moveable alphabet until the hand is genuinely ready, so writing English never
feels like a fine-motor punishment.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **DIY metal-inset substitutes** — sturdy plastic/card stencils of the ten
  classic shapes; the child traces the frame and the insert and fills with
  strokes. *Control of error:* strokes that cross the outline are visible.
- **Pattern / pre-writing stroke cards** — lines, zigzags, loops, left-to-right
  trails to trace (laminated, dry-wipe, infinitely reusable). *Control of error:*
  trace-over-the-line is self-evident; wipe and retry.
- **Sand/salt tray and chalkboards** — endlessly repeatable letter practice with
  zero paper-waste anxiety.
- **Handwriting strips** matching your chosen script, so the form the child
  copies is the form on the sandpaper letters and in Montree's assets.

**Montree mapping.** Handwriting runs *parallel* to the reading series rather than
as a numbered lesson — but "forms letters / writes CVC words on paper" is strong
photo-audit evidence and a natural milestone to surface in the English tracker
alongside Pink progress.

## Part 7 — Reading emerges: the Pink, Blue, Green series

*Decoding, built on everything above. This is the part Montree already maps in
detail — the revamp's job is to seat it correctly on the foundation.*

**The science.** Reading is **decoding for meaning** — and in this method it
arrives *after* the child has been writing with the moveable alphabet, often
spontaneously. The classic structure (a Montessori-teacher refinement, not
Montessori's own term) is a **three-colour series** of increasing complexity,
each using **object boxes**: miniature objects or pictures the child reads a label
for and matches — so **reading is always tied to meaning**, never barking at
abstract text.

- **Pink — phonetic CVC words.** Three-letter, one-sound-per-letter words
  (cat, sun, pig). Object boxes: read the word, match it to the object. The child
  reads what they can already build and say.
- **Blue — blends and longer phonetic words.** Consonant blends (st-, -nd),
  then longer/2-syllable phonetic words, then VCe ("magic e"), where each sound
  is still heard.
- **Green — phonograms.** Letter teams that make a *new* sound (ai, ee, igh, oa,
  oo, -tion…) — the irregular richness of English. Often organised in **phonogram
  folders/booklets**, one phonogram at a time.

Across all three sit two more strands:

- **Puzzle words / sight words** — high-frequency irregular words (the, was,
  said) that can't be sounded out, learned as wholes.
- **Reading classification & comprehension** — moving from single words to
  phrases, to commands the child reads-and-acts ("hop to the door"), to sentences,
  to little books — always **reading for meaning.**

**EAL tuning.** Two adjustments matter. (1) **Comprehension cannot be assumed** —
a child may decode "jug" perfectly and not know what a jug is, so the
object/picture in every box is doing double duty (decoding *and* vocabulary), and
puzzle words must be *understood*, not just recognised. (2) **Pace by the ear,
not the eye** — if a Green phonogram contains an English sound the child still
can't hear/say, loop back to sound games for it before expecting the reading to
stick.

**Independent materials you can make:**

- **Pink/Blue/Green object & picture boxes** — miniatures or photos + printed
  word labels + a control card. *Control of error:* control card or
  picture-on-back. (Montree's three-part-card pipeline already targets exactly
  this.)
- **Word-and-picture matching cards, phrase cards, command cards.**
- **Phonogram booklets** — one phonogram per booklet, words to read.
- **Puzzle-word ring** — a ring of sight-word cards, tap-to-hear via Montree
  audio for self-check.
- **Little decodable readers** — staged to Pink/Blue/Green so a child only meets
  words they can decode → the book itself is the reward and the control of error.

**Montree mapping (this is the spine you already have).** Parts 1–6 are the
*preparation* that earns the child their place at **Pink Lesson 1**; from there
the existing **128-lesson Pink (1–53) → Blue (54–83) → Green (84–128)** scheme in
`lesson-map.ts` is the reading curriculum, and the English Progression tracker is
its dashboard. The revamp doesn't rewrite that order — it **builds the runway**
beneath it so EAL children arrive ready, and adds the encoding (moveable-alphabet)
and oral (spoken-language) evidence alongside the decoding lessons.

---

## Part 8 — Total reading: the horizon

*Where the sequence is heading, so the corner is built pointing the right way.*

Once decoding is fluent, reading turns into a tool rather than a task. The child
moves into **"total reading"**: reading whole books for pleasure and for
information, and beginning **word study and grammar** — the function of words
(the noun, the article, the adjective, the verb), introduced through Montessori's
vivid, concrete grammar materials and games. That word-study/grammar arc is its
own curriculum (and its own future document); for the English corner revamp the
point is only this: **everything in Parts 1–7 should leave the child a confident,
meaning-driven reader who *likes* reading** — because that is the soil grammar
and composition grow in. Build the corner so the visible end of the shelf is *a
child curled up with a little book they chose*, and the whole sequence stays
honest.

## Part 9 — Setting up the English corner in reality

*The prepared environment. The shelf itself teaches the sequence.*

The corner is not a set of materials; it is a **prepared environment** whose
*arrangement* does half the teaching. Principles:

**Order = the sequence, left to right.** Arrange the shelves so the work flows
**left-to-right and top-to-bottom in the order of the method**: spoken-language /
vocabulary works first (left), then sound games, then sandpaper letters, then the
moveable alphabet, then handwriting, then Pink → Blue → Green reading (right).
The child internalises the path simply by where things live — and the left-right
flow reinforces the reading direction one more time.

**Everything at child height, everything has a home.** Each work on its own tray
or basket, complete, beautiful, and returnable by the child. A work the child
can't fetch and replace independently isn't in the corner yet.

**Beauty and restraint.** Few things, well chosen, uncluttered. Real materials,
natural where possible, isolated to one concept each. An overstuffed corner
overwhelms; a calm one invites.

**Control of error in everything.** Every material answers the child's "did I get
it right?" *without an adult* — a control card, a self-check photo, audio that
states the answer, objects that only fit one way, a book the child can or can't
read. This is what lets the corner **run itself** and what protects the child's
dignity: mistakes are between the child and the material, never a correction from
a teacher.

**One script, one convention, everywhere.** As flagged in Part 4 — the sandpaper
letters, the moveable alphabet, the handwriting model, the labels, and Montree's
digital assets must all show the **same letterform** and the same **sound-first**
convention. Inconsistency here is the most common cause of early-reader confusion.

**Rotation and observation.** Don't shelve all 26 letters or three colour-series
at once. Put out what the children near that stage need; rotate as they grow.
*Observation drives rotation* — which is exactly what Montree's photo-audit +
English tracker exist to support: the data tells you who is ready for the next
tray.

**The teacher's role.** Give the individual presentation (the 3-period lesson,
the sound game, the sandpaper-letter tracing), then **step back** and let the
child repeat freely. The corner's success is measured by how much happens
*without* the teacher.

**A workable starter layout (one shelf bay):**

1. *Top-left:* vocabulary baskets + classified cards (spoken language).
2. Sound-game baskets (initial → final → medial).
3. Sandpaper letters (the few currently in rotation) + sand tray.
4. Moveable alphabet + CVC picture-build cards.
5. Metal-inset / pre-writing tray + chalkboard.
6. *Bottom-right:* Pink object box → Blue → Green, and a small basket of staged
   little books.

**How it ties into Montree.** The physical corner and the app are one system: the
shelf delivers the hands-on, self-correcting work; **photo audit** captures what
each child does; the **English Progression tracker** (the 128-lesson map) shows
where each child sits and who's ready to advance; and the **weekly reports** turn
that into something parents see. Build the corner in the method's order and
Montree's tracking maps onto it one-to-one.

## Part 10 — The independent-materials build list

*Everything you can make yourself, by stage, EAL-tuned, each with a built-in
control of error so a child can use it alone and enjoy it. Ordered to match the
shelf.*

| Stage | Material to make | What the child does | Control of error | Why they enjoy it |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Indirect prep** | Pincer baskets (tongs + pom-poms, tweezers + beads, pegs on a tin) | Transfer/sort small objects with a 3-finger grip | Objects drop / don't fit if grip is wrong | Cause-and-effect, visible success |

| **Indirect prep** | Transfer & pouring trays; sponge squeezing | Spoon/pour/squeeze between vessels | Spills are visible; child wipes | Real, grown-up work |
| **Indirect prep** | Rough/smooth touch boards | Stroke with two fingers, light touch | Feels different under the fingers | Sensory, calming |
| **Spoken language** | Object↔picture matching baskets | Pair miniatures to photos | Self-check photo / colour dot on back | Matching is satisfying |
| **Spoken language** | Themed classified / nomenclature cards | Name & sort by theme | Control booklet of correct pairs | Themes they love (animals, food) |
| **Spoken language** | Tap-to-hear word cards (Montree audio) | Hear correct English, repeat | The audio is the answer | They control the "teacher" |
| **Sound games** | "/s/ basket" of same-initial-sound objects | Name & confirm the shared sound | All share the sound; odd one stands out | A treasure basket to rummage |
| **Sound games** | Initial-sound sorting mats | Sort objects under header sounds | Colour-dot self-check on reverse | Game-like sorting |
| **Sound games** | Minimal-pair cards (ship/sheep, pen/pan) | Match the near-twins | Control card | Funny near-misses |

| **Sandpaper letters** | Sand / salt writing tray | Write the just-traced letter | Compare to sandpaper letter; wipe & retry | Zero-stakes, endlessly redoable |
| **Sandpaper letters** | Letter + object sound trays | Match letter to an object starting with it | Picture/colour self-check | Little collections |
| **Moveable alphabet** | Large moveable alphabet (vowels/consonants coloured) | Build chosen words by sound | Spelling on card back / control strip | "I wrote it myself!" |

| **Handwriting** | DIY metal-inset stencils | Trace frame + insert, fill with strokes | Strokes crossing the line are visible | Patterns become pretty |
| **Handwriting** | Dry-wipe pre-writing stroke cards | Trace lines, loops, L→R trails | Off-the-line is self-evident; wipe | Reusable, no waste worry |

**Two cross-cutting build tips.** (1) **Reuse one object set across four
stages** — the same themed box of CVC miniatures serves spoken language → sound
games → moveable alphabet → Pink reading; build those boxes first, they earn
their keep four times over. (2) **Laminate + dry-wipe everything you can** —
self-correcting, reusable, waste-free, and children adore wiping and redoing.

## Appendix B — EAL adaptation summary (the revamp in one glance)

Everything that makes this an **English-as-additional-language** corner rather
than a standard primary:

1. **Front-load and lengthen spoken language.** The oral vocabulary base is the
   bottleneck; build it generously before symbols and keep building it alongside.
2. **Comprehension before production, always** — lean on the 3-period lesson's
   middle step; never force a shy child to speak to "prove" a word.
3. **Anchor English to what the child already knows** in their home language —
   transfer meaning, teach only the new label.
4. **Spend extra ear-time on English sounds the home language lacks** (e.g. the
   documented Mandarin-L1 contrasts) *before* attaching letters; use minimal
   pairs as games.
5. **Make the first words the same words** across oral → sound → moveable
   alphabet → Pink reading, so the child reads what they already say.
6. **Pace by the ear, not the eye** — loop back to sound games whenever a reading
   phonogram outruns the child's ability to hear it.
7. **Use the language-neutral stages (Practical Life, handwriting) as the child's
   confident home base** so every EAL child belongs and succeeds from day one.
