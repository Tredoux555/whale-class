# VIDEO SCRIPT BRAIN

_A brain component for writing higher-converting video scripts — both **short-form**
(TikTok / Reels / Shorts) and **long-form YouTube** — for Tredoux's products: Lyf
Coach first, also Montree, Jeffy, and personal brand. Reference knowledge for the
assistant when helping write or critique scripts. NOT user-facing coaching content._

_Structure: **Module 1** = the psychology (applies to every format). **Module 2** =
the short-form build system. **Module 3** = the long-form YouTube build system._

---

## 0. HOW TO USE THIS — the reasoning hierarchy

When writing or critiquing a script, reason in this order:

1. **Lead with what replicates.** Some persuasion effects survive scrutiny; many
   don't. Prefer the strong tier (§2). Treat weak-tier tactics as hypotheses to
   A/B test, never as guarantees.
2. **Effect sizes are small.** Even robust persuasion effects are modest
   (correlations roughly r = .08–.20). No single line "doubles conversions."
   Stack honest small edges; promise small.
3. **Honesty is a constraint, not a tradeoff.** Tredoux's authenticity *is* the
   product. For his exact format (a real person, solo, on a phone) authenticity is
   also the highest-converting mode. Persuasion and honesty point the same way
   here — so use honesty as the lever, not despite it.
4. **Match the principle to the behaviour.** Signing up for a life coach is a
   *prevention / self-improvement* behaviour. That changes which framing wins
   (§4). Don't default to fear.

---

## 1. THE HONESTY FIREWALL (hard constraints — never cross)

These override everything below. A single violation collapses the trust the whole
story is built on, and trust is the entire asset.

- **No fabricated scarcity or urgency.** Real numbers only. No invented countdowns.
- **No fake social proof.** Never invent users, ratings, or testimonials.
- **No fake authority.** Don't imply credentials he doesn't have.
- **No clinical overclaiming.** It's support — available and affordable. Never a
  cure, a treatment, a diagnosis, or "better than therapy."
- **No fear-spine** for a self-improvement product. Fear framing is for *detection*
  behaviours (e.g. cancer screening), not "build a better daily life."
- **Never exploit acute crisis** or imply replacement of emergency/clinical care.
- **No "covert / subconscious control."** It does not work that way (see §8). Writing
  as if it does produces clever-feeling scripts that don't convert.
- **Small effects → small promises.** Don't sell any line as a silver bullet.

---

## 2. THE PERSUASION LEVERS, RANKED BY HOW WELL THEY REPLICATE

Use as honest framing devices, never as tricks.

**STRONG TIER — use freely:**
- **Social proof / conformity** — the strongest-replicating lever. Real people,
  named, doing the real thing. (See the scale caveat in §6.)
- **Authority / credibility** — who is speaking and *why* they're trustworthy.
  Driven more by apparent lack of ulterior motive than by credentials (§5).
- **Commitment & consistency** — a small "yes" leads to a bigger "yes." Open with a
  micro-agreement; build a CTA ladder (§G).
- **Liking / unity** — warmth, similarity, a shared identity ("for us, the ones who
  hold it together for everyone else"). Faces and real voices beat polish.

**MODERATE TIER — real but context-dependent, test it:**
- **Scarcity** — a genuine effect on purchase intent, but easily overplayed and
  **product-dependent** (read §7 before using it — for a trust-first product it can
  backfire).
- **Reciprocity** — robust face-to-face, weaker at scale. A free month is a *real
  gift*; frame it as generosity, not a hook.
- **Framing / loss aversion / anchoring** — same fact, different frame, different
  response; the first number anchors what follows. Real but small — a tiebreaker,
  not a spine (§4).

**WEAK / FRAGILE TIER — never build a script's spine on these:**
- Exotic priming, ego-depletion framings, most "one weird trick" tactics. Cite
  nothing here as proof. If a draft leans on one, flag it and swap in a strong-tier
  replacement.

> Rhetoric is the oldest version of all this: **ethos** (credibility), **pathos**
> (emotion), **logos** (logic). Strong scripts open on pathos, carry ethos
> throughout, and pay off with a little logos.

---

## 3. NARRATIVE TRANSPORTATION — the biggest unfair advantage

Being absorbed into a story increases persuasion partly by *lowering the viewer's
urge to counter-argue* — a transported viewer is inside a story, not defending
against a pitch. (van Laer et al. meta-analysis, 64 articles.) The effect is
**stronger** when the story is:

- **commercial in domain** (a product story — ✓),
- **user-generated, not studio-produced** (a real person on his phone beats a
  glossy ad — ✓),
- **received one viewer at a time** (a person alone with earbuds at night — ✓).

**Tredoux's constraints are his moat.** The rough, real, solo-filmed first-person
story is the research-optimal format — not a compromise.

Script implications:
- Open *inside a concrete scene*, not a claim.
- One narrator, his own face and voice.
- Specific sensory detail sustains transportation; abstraction breaks it.
- Emotion out-engages humour for this content.

---

## 4. FRAMING — lead with the gain

For prevention / self-improvement behaviours, **gain-framed** messages modestly
beat loss-framed ones (O'Keefe & Jensen meta-analyses; r ≈ .08). Loss framing wins
mainly in *detection* contexts — not a life-coach signup.

- **Spine = gain:** steadiness, a thinking partner at 2am, "it held her," "she uses
  it every day."
- **Loss = one quiet true beat only**, never the spine ("I couldn't afford a
  therapist"). Let loss whisper underneath.

---

## 5. SOURCE CREDIBILITY — trust comes from low apparent self-interest

Trust is driven more by *perceived lack of ulterior motive* than by credentials.

- **Keep the anti-credential.** "I'm not a therapist — I'm a kindergarten teacher
  who built this for my wife" *raises* trust. Don't dress him up as an expert.
- **Specificity multiplies credibility.** Named people, real numbers, real places
  read as testimony; vagueness reads as marketing. ("70,000 Rand." "My wife." "2am.")
- **One overclaim collapses everything.** If a line makes the viewer think "yeah,
  right," it has cost more than it earned. Cut it.

---

## 6. SOCIAL PROOF — and the "trusted by millions" trap

Social proof is the strongest lever, and Tredoux already holds the best kind:
**named, specific, related-party proof** — a wife who uses it daily, a son who
thanks him. More persuasive than any star rating because it's concrete and
emotionally legible.

- **Name the real user and the real behaviour.** "My wife uses it every day" beats
  "users love it." Frequency *is* the proof ("every day" = habit, not novelty).
- **Grow the numbers honestly.** Inflated numbers break a trust-based product.

**The scale trap (decided, Lyf Coach):** don't compete on crowd size at all — claim
neither "first 10" *nor* "trusted by millions." Two reasons:
1. **It isn't true yet** — and faking it in a *privacy* product is the fastest way
   to torch trust.
2. **"Trusted by millions" fights the brand.** Lyf Coach sells private, intimate,
   your-words-stay-yours, made-by-a-real-person. "Millions are using this" signals
   the opposite — big, faceless, corporate, your-data-in-a-machine. A privacy
   product bragging about scale is a contradiction the audience feels.

So: **stay silent on the count.** Replace head-count social proof with the proof you
*can* stand behind honestly — a real person behind it (the origin story / his
face), the privacy promise itself, and one or two genuine testimonials. For this
category, one real "this helped me at 2am, and no one ever saw it" beats a vanity
number.

---

## 7. SCARCITY & RECIPROCITY — real-only, and product-aware

- **Reciprocity (use freely):** the free month is a genuine gift. Frame as
  generosity — *"I want you to actually feel it work first"* — not as a trap.
- **Scarcity (handle with care):** a real "first N" offer is honest, but for a
  trust-first, vulnerable-moment product, **scarcity framing can backfire** — a
  countdown signals "new, untested, growth-hacking," and "no one else is here yet"
  feels lonely in a product people come to at their lowest. **Decision (Lyf Coach):**
  keep the genuine free-month offer, but frame it as *"try it free, no pressure,
  subscribe later only if it helps"* — generosity, not a ticking clock. Reserve overt
  scarcity for status/hype products (e.g. a launch waitlist), not this one.

---

## 8. ERICKSONIAN LANGUAGE PATTERNS — the legitimately useful "NLP" residue

**Honesty note:** the user originally framed this as an "NLP" database. NLP's core
claims — eye-accessing cues, representational-system matching, embedded commands
acting below awareness — have repeatedly failed controlled testing; systematic
reviews found no reliable evidence. **Do not present NLP jargon as science and do
not promise covert control over viewers.** What *is* real is the language craft NLP
borrowed from Milton Erickson — treat it as good rhetoric, not brain magic. Use
sparingly and only in service of a true message; overused, it reads as manipulative
and tanks trust.

- **Presupposition** — embed the assumption: "*When* you start using it…" (not "if").
- **Open loop** — tease a payoff, delay it: "the part that surprised me, I'll get to."
- **Pace then lead** — first state what they already agree with, then introduce the
  new idea. Builds momentum.
- **Embedded suggestion** — fold the ask into a sentence about something else; softer
  than a bare command.
- **Yes-set** — three quick statements they'll nod at, then the ask.
- **Contrast frame** — "Most people do X. The few who get steady do Y."

# MODULE 2 — THE BUILD SYSTEM (short-form: TikTok / Reels / Shorts)

## A. The spine every script follows

`HOOK (≤3s) → SCENE / STORY → TURN → PROOF → GAIN → CTA LADDER`

No filler between beats. Each beat must earn the next three seconds of attention.

## B. Word budget & runtime (memorise)

- Speaking pace ≈ **140–160 words/min.**
- **30s ≈ 75 words · 45s ≈ 110 · 60s ≈ 150 · 3min ≈ 450.**
- **Write to the LOW end.** Silence and a held look out-convert a crammed script.
- **Sweet-spot length:** TikTok in-feed ≈ 21–34s; Reels often shorter; Shorts strong
  at 15–30s. Default promo to **21–34s** unless the story genuinely needs longer.

## C. Hook rules (the first sentence is everything)

_(Platform rules of thumb, not lab science, but consistent across creators:)_ most
ad recall forms in the first few seconds, and hooks that hold the 3-second mark get
far more reach. Losing more than ~a third of viewers in the first 3 seconds = the
hook is too weak.

- **Land in under 3 seconds.** Open in a scene or on a concrete fact — never a claim
  or a cliché question ("Ever feel overwhelmed?").
- **First frame is visually interesting before a word is spoken.** No "hey guys," no
  logo, no slow build.
- **Trigger at least one of four:** curiosity, pattern interrupt, self-relevance,
  emotional arousal. Stack two when you can.
- **Works on mute** — a 4–7 word, high-contrast text overlay carries it sound-off.
- **Always generate 3–5 hook variants** so he can pick by feel. Quantity then filter.

Hook sources that fit Tredoux:
- **The loss** — "I missed my flight. 70,000 Rand, gone."
- **The confession** — "I couldn't afford a therapist, so I built one."
- **The contradiction** — "I'm a kindergarten teacher. I built an AI life coach."
- **The result-first / open story** — "My family should've melted down that day. They
  didn't — and I think I know why."

## D. Transportation checklist (run on every draft)

- [ ] Opens inside a scene, not an abstraction
- [ ] One narrator, his real voice
- [ ] ≥ 2 concrete specifics (named person, real number, real place)
- [ ] Emotional, not jokey
- [ ] No counter-argument bait (no overclaim that makes the viewer go "yeah, right")

## E. Body rules

- **Deliver on the hook's promise immediately** — don't make them wait.
- **Pattern-interrupt every 3–5 seconds** (cut, angle, text, B-roll). Keep spoken
  chunks under ~8 seconds before a visual change.
- **Number the points** if listing ("2 of 3") so viewers track progress.
- **Cut any sentence** that doesn't advance the story, prove a point, or build to the
  payoff.
- **Captions always on** — sound-off is the default, and captions lift watch time.

## F. Framing default

Spine = gain; loss = one quiet true beat (see §4).

## G. The CTA ladder (always three rungs — layer it, don't bet on the end)

Most viewers leave before the final frame, so don't put the only ask there.

1. **Micro (internal yes)** — a line that earns a silent nod, no ask. ("You're
   carrying more than you let on.")
2. **Mid (felt yes)** — let them feel the gain through the proof beat (the proof beat
   doubles as this), seconds ~10–20.
3. **Final (behavioural yes)** — one clear, low-friction action, named precisely.

**Lyf Coach CTA (current framing):** *"It's `lyfcoach.co` — a website, not an app,
just click. First month's free, no card, no pressure — subscribe later only if it
helps."* Real gift, stated plainly, zero countdown. (The link `lyfcoach.co/first10`
still works for QR/printed assets.)

> **DECIDED UPDATE (Lyf Coach, cold/story videos):** do NOT put the product name or URL
> in the video itself — story-only; the viewer swipes the moment they smell a pitch. The
> pitch goes in the **caption** ("How? Link in bio.") and the **bio link**. The CTA wording
> above is for the caption / bio / landing page, NOT for spoken or on-screen video copy.
> See the TikTok Growth Brain (`tiktok_growth`) Decided Position 1.

## H. On-screen text rules

- One short line per beat, **≤ 6 words.** Reinforce the audio, don't duplicate it.
- **Put a key number on screen** — visual encoding beats audio alone for recalling
  specifics. (But NOT the product name or URL on cold/story videos — those live in the
  caption + bio; see `tiktok_growth` Decided Position 1.)
- Captions always on.

## I. OUTPUT FORMAT — deliver every script like this

1. **3–5 hook variants**
2. **Beat-by-beat script:** spoken line · [direction] · on-screen text
3. **CTA ladder**, explicitly labelled (micro / mid / final)
4. **Word count + runtime**
5. **Voice flags** — anything that sounds unlike Tredoux, called out
6. **Test note** — which one moderate/weak-tier lever (if any) to A/B test

## J. Voice guardrails (auto-flag violations)

Tredoux is **direct, warm, real, carries weight.** NOT corporate, chirpy, fake, or
salesy. Auto-flag and rewrite: buzzwords ("unlock," "transform your life,"
"game-changer"), exclamation stacking, hype adjectives, anything implying a clinical
claim, anything that reads like an ad-read rather than a person talking.

## K. Per-product quick notes

- **Lyf Coach** — private AI life coach. Lead with the 2am/overwhelm gain + his real
  origin story; honest privacy as the trust signal; no scarcity, no scale claims.
- **Montree** — education product. Lead with the teacher's real pain (Sunday-night
  admin), classroom footage as authority, "3,000 teachers use this" *only if true*.
- **Jeffy** — mission content. Lead with pathos + unity ("for us"); real farm/classroom
  footage is the edge over AI video.
- **Personal brand** — build-in-public honesty; show real numbers, including the
  unflattering ones.

## L. After posting — read the retention curve

- **Drop at 3 seconds** → the hook is weak; rewrite frame one.
- **Drop at 5–7 seconds** → the body didn't pay off the hook's promise; tighten the
  turn.
- Reply to early comments fast (the algorithm rewards it); a video reply to a good
  comment often outperforms a fresh post.

# MODULE 3 — LONG-FORM YOUTUBE (the 6–15 minute build)

Module 1 (the psychology) carries over **unchanged** — transportation, gain-framing,
real social proof, the honesty firewall all still apply. What changes is the
*structure*: you're holding attention for minutes, not seconds, and the **title +
thumbnail now do half the work** before the script is even read.

## M3.0 What's different about long-form

- YouTube ranks on **watch time and satisfaction**, not just the click. A video
  that's clicked then abandoned *hurts* the channel; one watched to the end that
  leaves people glad they watched gets pushed. Retention is the whole game — and
  satisfaction means **the payoff must match the promise.** (Clickbait that doesn't
  deliver = short-term clicks, long-term channel death. Same honesty firewall.)
- **The package gets tested before the script does:** title + thumbnail + first 30
  seconds. A great script can't rescue a weak title/thumbnail — those decide whether
  anyone presses play at all.

## M3.1 The package — do this BEFORE writing

- **Title** — an honest curiosity gap or a clear specific payoff. Promise something
  the video actually delivers.
- **Thumbnail** — one readable idea; his real face/emotion beats graphics. It must
  set the *same* expectation the title and cold open pay off. Title + thumbnail +
  hook = one aligned promise.

## M3.2 The long-form spine

`COLD OPEN (0–30s) → SETUP (→60s) → VALUE SEGMENTS (re-hooked) → PAYOFF → NEXT-VIDEO LOOP`

- **Cold open (0–30s) — the cliff.** Most drop-off happens here. State the payoff or
  open a strong loop in the first lines, show you'll deliver, front-load the single
  most interesting moment. **No "hey guys, welcome back, smash subscribe"** — the #1
  retention killer. *Script the cold open word-for-word.*
- **Setup (→60s).** Stakes + a roadmap of what's coming + quick credibility. Tell them
  why this is worth their next eight minutes.
- **Value segments.** Deliver in chapters, each its own mini hook→payoff. **Re-hook
  every 1–2 minutes:** open the next loop *before* closing the current one ("but the
  part that actually changed things is next"). Pattern-interrupt with cuts, B-roll,
  angle/location changes; cut every second of dead air.
- **Payoff.** Deliver exactly what the title and cold open promised — satisfaction is
  the ranking signal you're protecting.
- **Next-video loop.** End by pointing to the logical next watch (end screen). That's
  how a channel compounds.

## M3.3 CTA placement (long-form)

Viewer count is highest in the middle, so don't tack the only ask onto a dead ending.
- **Soft subscribe** right after the first real value moment ("if that helped,
  subscribe — there's more coming"), ~1–3 min in.
- **Main CTA** at a payoff peak, named and low-friction (`lyfcoach.co` for Lyf Coach;
  the relevant link/keyword for Montree / Jeffy).
- **One ask per moment** — don't stack CTAs.

## M3.4 How to script it

- **Outline + key lines, not word-for-word**, for talking-head/vlog (reading verbatim
  sounds stiff) — *except* the cold open, which you script exactly.
- **Word budget:** ~150 wpm → 6 min ≈ 900 words · 10 min ≈ 1,500 · 12 min ≈ 1,800.
  Write to the low end; concision still wins.
- **Length is downstream of value** — go as long as the value holds, no longer. A
  tight 6 minutes beats a padded 12.

## M3.5 Output format for a long-form script

1. **Title (2–3 options) + thumbnail concept** — the package first
2. **Cold open**, scripted word-for-word (with on-screen text)
3. **Segment outline** — each segment's mini-hook · the value · the re-hook into the next
4. **CTA placements** — soft (early) + main (at a payoff peak)
5. **Next-video loop** suggestion
6. **Runtime + word count**
7. **Voice flags + one test note**

## M3.6 Reading the long-form retention curve

- **Cliff in the first 30s** → cold open / package mismatch (the title promised what
  the open didn't pay off).
- **Steady dips mid-video** → that segment dragged; tighten or cut it.
- **A spike** → people re-watched something; do more of that.
- **A drop at a CTA** → the ask came before you'd earned it; move it later or soften it.

# MODULE 4 — THE HOOK SWIPE FILE (write 5, keep the one that makes YOU stop)

The hook is ~80% of the outcome. Always generate **5 variants** in different families, read
them cold, and keep the one that would stop *you* mid-scroll. Each family below = the pattern,
why it works, and a Lyf Coach example to adapt (never reuse verbatim — keep it true).

**Loss / cost** — a concrete number gone. _Why:_ specificity + stakes kill the urge to scroll.
- "I lost $4,000 on a flight. My fault." · "Therapy quoted me $180 an hour. I had $0."

**Confession** — admit the thing people hide. _Why:_ vulnerability earns trust + curiosity.
- "I couldn't afford a therapist, so I built one." · "I haven't told anyone this until now."

**Contrarian strike** — challenge a belief they hold. _Why:_ pattern interrupt + self-defense
makes them stay to argue. - "Journaling made my overthinking worse." · "Stop 'self-caring'."

**Identity call** — name an ultra-specific person. _Why:_ self-relevance = instant stop.
- "If you're the one everyone leans on and no one checks on — this is for you."

**Open loop** — promise a payoff, withhold it. _Why:_ the brain can't drop an open loop.
- "The thing that finally quieted my 3am brain wasn't what I expected."

**Result-first / reverse** — lead with the outcome, then earn it. _Why:_ curiosity gap.
- "My family should've melted down that day. They didn't — here's why."

**The number** — a surprising count/stat from real life. _Why:_ concrete + scrolls slowly.
- "I sent 47 messages at 2am last month. Not one to a person."

**Question done right** — only if it's specific and they can't answer glibly. _Why:_ most
question hooks ("ever feel stressed?") are dead; specific ones aren't. - "What do you do with
a thought at 3am when everyone you love is asleep?"

**Hook hard rules:** land it in <3s · first FRAME is interesting before a word (Module 2 §C +
the cover lever) · works on mute (≤6-word overlay) · one concrete specific minimum · never a
cliché question or "hey guys."

# MODULE 5 — STORY STRUCTURES (named arcs — pick the shape before you write)

- **The One Moment (micro-story).** One scene, one turn, full stop. _Use for:_ cold/story
  videos where the product never appears. Shape: scene → tension → quiet turn → stop. _Ex:_ the
  $4,000-flight beat. The caption carries the rest.
- **Before–After–Bridge.** Old normal → new normal → the bridge between (the bridge is the
  product, named only in caption/bio for cold videos). _Use for:_ transformation proof.
- **Problem–Agitate–Relief** (NOT problem-agitate-*sell*). Name the ache → make it specific and
  felt → offer *relief*, never fear. _Use for:_ Tier-1/Tier-2 pain content. Relief = the gain.
- **The Confession arc.** Admit something real → what it cost → what changed → leave them with
  a feeling, not a pitch. _Use for:_ founder/origin content, highest trust.
- **The Demonstration.** Show the thing working, no claims — screen-record a real exchange.
  _Use for:_ "show, don't tell" proof. Let the quality of the response be the argument.
- **The List with a turn.** "3 things…" but the third reframes the first two. _Use for:_
  saves/shares (listicles over-index on saves). Number the beats so viewers track progress.

Match the arc to the behaviour (self-improvement = gain-framed, Module 1 §4). Spine = gain;
loss whispers underneath, one beat only.

# MODULE 6 — THE RETENTION-EDITING MASTERCLASS (the edit is half the script)

- **Cover / first frame (0:00).** Its own deliverable. Arresting, readable, a face or motion —
  never a logo/title card/slow build. A 0:01 drop = the frame failed, not the words.
- **0–1s.** Motion or a face already moving on frame one; the spoken hook starts immediately —
  no dead air, no intro animation.
- **Cut on every beat (every ~1.5–3s).** Jump-cut out breaths, "ums," and the silence between
  words. Dead air is where they swipe.
- **Text choreography.** One ≤6-word line per beat, timed to the audio, high-contrast, inside
  the safe zone (clear of TikTok's right-side buttons + bottom caption). Reinforce, don't
  duplicate, the spoken line.
- **Pacing curve.** Fast hook → slow and still on the one key insight (a held look out-converts
  a crammed line) → fast close. Vary it; monotony reads as boring even when the words are good.
- **The loop.** End the last frame so it flows back into the first — manufactures rewatches and
  completion at once. Hide a second-pass detail that only lands on replay.
- **Captions always on** (30%+ watch muted) and **sound** chosen per Growth Brain (rising
  trending sound early for a spike, original audio for durable reach).
- **Length is downstream of retention** — cut to the shortest version that still lands the turn.

# MODULE 7 — THE SCRIPT DOCTOR (grade every draft before it ships)

Run this rubric on any script (yours or a critique). Score each 0–2; below ~10/14 = rewrite.

1. **Hook (0–3s)** — concrete, <3s, would stop me cold? (Module 4)
2. **First frame/cover** — arresting before a word? (Module 6)
3. **Transportation** — opens in a scene, real voice, ≥2 specifics, emotional not jokey?
4. **Honesty firewall** — zero fabricated proof/scarcity/authority, zero clinical claim? (§1)
5. **Spine = gain** — not fear; loss only whispers? (§4)
6. **Specificity** — named person/number/place, not vague marketing?
7. **CTA placement** — story-only for cold videos (product in caption/bio, NOT in the video);
   layered ladder elsewhere? (Decided positions + §G)
8. **Voice** — sounds like Tredoux (direct, warm, real), not corporate/chirpy/salesy? (§J)

**Common failure modes → the fix:**
- _Flat opening_ → cut the first 2 sentences; start on the hardest line.
- _Pitch creep_ → delete every product mention from the video; move it to the caption.
- _Abstraction_ → swap one general claim for one concrete specific.
- _Crammed_ → halve the word count; add a held silent beat.
- _"Yeah, right" line_ → an overclaim cost more than it earned; cut it.
- _Joke where feeling belongs_ → emotion out-engages humour for this content.

**Banned-phrase kill list (auto-rewrite):** "unlock," "transform your life," "game-changer,"
"level up," "it's giving," exclamation stacks, any "cure/treat/fix your anxiety," any "better
than therapy," anything that reads like an ad-read instead of a person talking.

**Output every script in the Module 2 §I / Module 3 §M3.5 format** (hook variants → beat-by-beat
→ CTA ladder → word count/runtime → voice flags → one test note).

_Persuasion ≠ manipulation. The goal is to make a true, valuable message impossible
to ignore — never to trick anyone. The honest version is also the one that converts._
