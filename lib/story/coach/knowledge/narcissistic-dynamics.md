# Narcissistic Parent Dynamics — survival & healing
*(For when Tredoux raises his mother / family. He's in LOW CONTACT. Lead by believing him. Reflect and steady — never diagnose, never push him toward or away from contact. His call, always.)*

**The core.** A narcissistic parent runs the relationship around their own needs — to be admired, central, right, unchallenged — instead of the child's. Love was conditional and transactional; the child became the emotional adult, managing a parent's moods (parentification + emotional neglect). The bond is real AND the harm is real — both true at once. Distance isn't cruelty; it's the sane response to a relationship that costs more than it returns.

**The roles (assigned, not earned).** Scapegoat (carries the blame, "too sensitive," often the most honest one), golden child (idealised for mirroring the parent, still conditional), lost child (invisible to stay safe), mascot (defuses tension). Who he was told he is, is not who he is.

**Naming the tactics disarms them.** When a move has a name it stops being his fault or his craziness:
- **Gaslighting** — rewriting events until he doubts his memory. → *His memory doesn't need her signature.*
- **FOG (Fear, Obligation, Guilt)** — the fog she generates so he complies. → *Guilt is a feeling, not a command.*
- **DARVO** — Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim & Offender — confront her and suddenly she's the victim. → *Being made the villain for naming a problem is the tell.*
- **Hoovering** — sudden warmth, a crisis, "I've changed," to vacuum him back when distance is working. → *A pull-back-in isn't change; change is shown over time.*
- **Triangulation / flying monkeys** — comparisons, relatives sent to pressure him. → *He can go direct; he doesn't owe everyone his case.*

## Practical tools & scripts (low contact)
- **Don't JADE** — don't Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain. A boundary needs no defence. "No" is a full sentence.
- **Gray rock / yellow rock** — brief, neutral, boring, no emotional fuel, a thin coat of politeness so it can't be spun as "cold." It's a costume for contact, not who he is. It works because the behaviour runs on *reaction* — calm and flat starves it.
- **A boundary is what HE will do**, not a rule he enforces on her: "If X, I will do Y" — then he does Y. Control the channel (text > live calls), the frequency, the time limits — decided when calm, not mid-ambush.
- **Scripts:** Decline — "That doesn't work for me." (stop). End a turning call — "I'm going to go now, talk soon" → hang up. Guilt trip — "I know you're disappointed. My decision is the same." Unannounced visit — "I need notice; without it I won't be able to answer the door." Hoovering — "Thanks for letting me know, I'll think about it." Flying monkeys — "I love you, and this is between Mum and me."
- **The extinction burst:** when he holds a boundary, expect it to get *worse* first (more calls, more guilt). That's the sign it's working, not failing. Hold steady, repeat, remove himself — it settles.

## Healing (this part is for him, not for managing her)
- **The wound has a name** — years of this often leave Complex PTSD. The **emotional flashback** (Pete Walker): a sudden flood of old shame/panic/smallness with no picture, so it feels like *now*. Naming it halves its power: *this is an old feeling visiting, not a current truth.* Ground: feet on floor, name the year, "that was then, I've got us now," turn down the inner critic (it's her old voice, not the truth).
- **Self-reparenting** — give himself now what he didn't get then: "My needs are allowed." "I don't have to earn rest or love." "I believe myself." "I can disappoint her and still be a good person."
- **Rebuild self-trust** against the gaslighting — a quick reality log after hard interactions; validate himself first instead of waiting for her acknowledgement (that keeps the power with her).
- **Grief is the quiet centre** — mourning the mother he *needed*, not the one who exists. Lindsay Gibson's key: as he releases the need for her emotional approval, the relationship gets more bearable and he gets freer. Radical acceptance ("this is who she is, she's unlikely to change") isn't giving up — it's putting down a weight he's carried for decades.
- **Guilt isn't his compass** — the guilt he feels protecting himself is proof of how deeply he was taught her comfort was his job, not proof he's wrong. Goal: act WITH guilt present, not obey it. Guilt rides shotgun; it doesn't get the wheel.

## How to coach him on this (stay in your own voice)
Believe his reality first — never subtly side with the parent or imply he's exaggerating; that *is* the original wound. Don't pathologise or label third parties; talk in patterns, not diagnoses (consistent with your reflect-don't-diagnose rule). Protect his autonomy — support his chosen level of contact, help him hold it, don't moralise "but she's your mother." Catch self-blame ("maybe I'm the difficult one") and hand him back the facts. Normalise guilt and grief as part of the process. Watch for emotional flashbacks in the moment and offer grounding, not problem-solving. When the weight is heavy, encourage trauma-informed support warmly — reparenting-by-committee, not a brush-off. Then help him name one tactic, take one concrete step, and get back to his life — don't spiral on analysing her.
