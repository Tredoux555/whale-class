# Nonviolent communication — Rosenberg's grammar for parent meetings

Marshall Rosenberg spent fifty years teaching people how to have
conversations that don't escalate into violence — not physical
violence, but the slower violence of misunderstanding, dismissal, and
withdrawal. His framework, NVC, has four moves that, in sequence, turn
almost any adversarial conversation into a partnership conversation.

The principal does not need to perform NVC openly in a parent meeting.
The parent doesn't want to be facilitated. But the principal who has
internalised NVC will find herself reaching for its moves
instinctively, especially in the meetings that go sideways. Astra
embeds NVC structure into her conversation scripts so the principal
doesn't have to remember the framework — she just has to read what's
in front of her.

## The four moves — OFNR

NVC's full sequence is: Observation, Feeling, Need, Request. The
acronym is OFNR. Each move is precisely defined and each one matters.

### O — Observation, not evaluation

The first move is to share what you observed in the most concrete,
non-evaluative form possible. Not the interpretation. Not the
diagnosis. The behaviour, as a video camera would have recorded it.

  EVALUATION: "Lucy has been disruptive at circle time."
  OBSERVATION: "On Monday and Wednesday, Lucy got up from circle time
  twice in the first ten minutes."

The parent CAN'T argue with the observation. She can argue with the
evaluation for an hour. By staying observational, the principal
sidesteps a defensive reaction and opens the conversation. The parent
might respond with her own data — "she's been waking up at 4am all
week" — and now you're sharing observations, which is where
partnership starts.

For the principal in any dossier conversation: every claim should be
defensible at the level of observation. If you can't say WHEN it
happened and WHAT was observable, soften the language until you can.

### F — Feeling, not story

The second move is to name a feeling — yours, theirs, or the child's
— as a feeling, not as a thought masquerading as a feeling.

  THOUGHT-AS-FEELING: "I feel like she doesn't respect the routines."
  ACTUAL FEELING: "I feel concerned when I see her leave circle
  repeatedly — concerned that we might be missing something she needs."

The "I feel like…" construction is almost always a thought, not a
feeling. Real feelings are short words: concerned, sad, hopeful,
relieved, worried, calm, frustrated. The principal who can name her
own actual feeling models permission for the parent to do the same.

In a parent meeting, the principal usually doesn't lead with her own
feeling — that can come off as performative. But she frequently NAMES
a feeling the parent might be having: "If I were sitting where you
are right now, I might be feeling a mix of concern and relief —
concern because nobody wants to hear a hard observation, relief
because someone is finally putting words around what you've been
noticing too." That move OPENS the feeling layer of the conversation
without forcing the parent to expose herself.

### N — Need, not strategy

The third move is to name the underlying NEED that the feeling is
pointing to. Rosenberg argues that every feeling is a signal about an
unmet (or met) need. The need is universal; the strategy for meeting
it is one of many.

  STRATEGY: "I need you to talk to her about hitting."
  NEED: "I need to know that we're partners in helping Lucy with this
  pattern, and that we're using the same language at home and at
  school."

Strategies generate conflict because they specify HOW the need must
be met. Needs invite partnership because they open the WHY without
locking down the HOW. When the principal names a need, she invites
the parent to brainstorm the strategy with her — which is what
partnership actually feels like.

### R — Request, not demand

The fourth move is to make a clear, specific, doable request — not a
demand. The distinction: a request can be refused without consequence.
A demand cannot.

  DEMAND: "We need you to stop letting her watch screens before bed."
  REQUEST: "Would you be open to trying a screen-free evening routine
  for the next two weeks and noticing whether it changes how she
  arrives in the morning?"

The request format gives the parent agency. Agency is what makes her
ACTUALLY do it. Demands generate compliance at best and resistance at
worst; requests generate partnership.

Astra's conversation scripts ALWAYS end with a request format on any
ask of the parent. Never "you should." Always "would you be open to."

## The four ways to receive criticism

The deeper NVC contribution to parent meetings: when the principal
delivers a hard observation, the parent receives it in one of four
ways. Three are dead ends; only one is dialogue.

### 1. Blame self

The parent internalises the observation as a verdict on her: "I'm a
terrible mother." She collapses into shame, agrees with everything,
takes notes furiously, leaves the meeting demoralised. The school feels
good — she "accepted" the feedback. Two weeks later nothing has
changed because shame is not a useful motivator.

This is the mode of EXPECTATION-DRIVEN parents who don't have safety
to push back. Watch for it. When you see it, BACK OFF the content and
restore identity safety: "I'm not saying you're doing anything wrong.
I'm saying we've noticed something we want to think about together."

### 2. Blame other

The parent rejects the observation by attacking the source: the
teacher is bad, the school is bad, the curriculum is wrong, the
classmates are bullying her child. Defensiveness, accusation, escalation.
The meeting ends with the school as the villain.

This is often a defended response to identity threat — the parent
SENSED the implied "this is your fault" and counter-attacked. The move
isn't to push back. The move is to acknowledge the impact and slow
down: "I hear that this feels like we're criticising you and that
wasn't my intent. Can I try again?"

### 3. Empathy for self

The parent recognises her own feelings in the moment — "I'm getting
defensive because this scares me." She names it internally and stays
present. This is the beginning of dialogue.

The principal can invite this state by NAMING what's happening: "I can
imagine this is hard to hear. We can pause if you need a minute."

### 4. Empathy for other

The parent recognises the school's intent and the teacher's care —
"you're telling me this because you want what's best for Lucy." She
softens and leans in.

Only states 3 and 4 produce dialogue. The principal's whole job in
the first five minutes of a difficult meeting is to keep the parent
out of states 1 and 2 and create conditions for states 3 and 4.

## Validation must precede reframing

This is Rosenberg's most useful single contribution to parent
meetings.

Almost every parent who has a concern has been MIS-VALIDATED somewhere
else first. The pediatrician dismissed it. The grandparent dismissed
it. The friend dismissed it. By the time she's in the principal's
office, she has been carrying the concern alone. She is waiting for
someone to validate it.

If the principal jumps to reframing — "you don't need to worry, this
is developmentally normal" — the parent hears: another person
dismissing me. She shuts down. Even if the reframing is medically and
developmentally correct, it lands as another dismissal.

Validation MUST come first. Always. Before any reframing.

  WRONG: "You don't need to worry — this is completely normal at her
  age."

  RIGHT: "I hear you. Watching your child struggle with something is
  genuinely hard, and the fact that you've been paying close enough
  attention to notice this is exactly the right kind of attention.
  Now — let me tell you what we see, because I think there's good
  news in it."

Same content. Opposite reception. The validation in the right version
is not flattery — it's NAMING what the parent has done well (paid
close attention) and ACKNOWLEDGING that her concern is real before
offering perspective on it.

Astra embeds this pattern into every conversation script. The
principal's opening line, in any difficult meeting, validates the
parent's experience before delivering the school's perspective. Even
when the school's perspective is "this isn't the problem you think it
is" — the validation comes first.

## Specific NVC translations for parent meetings

A small collection of trap phrases and their NVC-translated versions:

  ❌ "She's behind in reading."
  ✅ "We've noticed that her interest in the moveable alphabet hasn't
     surfaced yet. Most children in her cohort have started; she
     hasn't. I'm curious what you're seeing at home."

  ❌ "She's having behavioural issues."
  ✅ "On four days last week she had a hard transition between work
     and snack — left the table, wandered, took a while to settle. I
     wanted to share what we're noticing and ask what you see at
     home."

  ❌ "You need to be more consistent at home."
  ✅ "We've found that consistency between home and school really
     supports kids at this age. Would you be open to talking through
     what's been working for us, and seeing if any of it transfers?"

  ❌ "We're worried about her social development."
  ✅ "We're paying close attention to how she's connecting with other
     children right now. We see X and Y; we'd love to know what you
     see at home and at the playground."

The pattern: observation + curiosity + request. Never diagnosis +
demand.

## What Astra does with NVC

Every dossier conversation script Astra produces passes through these
filters:

  • Does every claim sit at the level of observation, not evaluation?
  • Does the script lead with validation of the parent's experience
    or strength, not with the school's concern?
  • Does the script name needs (universal, partnership-inviting), not
    strategies (specific, conflict-generating)?
  • Does every ask of the parent come as a request ("would you be
    open to…"), not a demand ("you need to…")?
  • Does the script anticipate criticism-receiving mode 1 or 2 and
    have a restoration move ready?

When all five filters pass, the script is shippable. When any one
fails, Astra rewrites the script before showing it to the principal.
