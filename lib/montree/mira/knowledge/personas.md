# Principal personas — how each one buys

These are the buyer archetypes. They are loose — most real principals are 60% one and 30% another. The dossier should pick the closest match and tune the script accordingly.

## 1. The Western single-school owner-principal

**Geography:** US, UK, Canada, Australia, NZ, Ireland.

**Profile:** Often the founder of the school. Often AMI- or AMS-trained themselves, but increasingly delegates classroom work to lead teachers. Tech-comfortable. Reads emails, replies in 24-48 hours, makes decisions in a day.

**Pain points:**
- Teacher retention. Their best teachers are burning out on the paperwork.
- Parent communication. Reports are uneven across teachers.
- They want to be in the room with the children, not at a desk.

**What lands:**
- Astra. Specifically the parent-meeting dossier — they have hard parent conversations weekly, and currently prepare for them by re-reading the child's file at 11pm.
- "Built by a Montessori teacher." They distrust software that doesn't get the language right.
- The free month with no card.

**What loses them:**
- Salesy tone. They've seen it all.
- Price-led pitches. They aren't shopping on price.
- Promises of outcomes.

## 2. The Chinese international school principal

**Geography:** Mainland China, especially Beijing / Shanghai / Shenzhen / Guangzhou / Chengdu / Hangzhou. Also Hong Kong, Taipei, Singapore (for Chinese-speaking families).

**Profile:** Often runs a school with bilingual instruction. May not be Montessori-trained themselves, but bought into the Montessori philosophy. Frequently relies on a Western lead teacher and a Chinese-speaking admin team. Time-poor, expectation-driven, family is the dominant relationship.

**Pain points:**
- Communicating with both Chinese and English-speaking parents in their own language.
- Demonstrating academic progress to results-driven parents.
- Bringing the international curriculum credibility (Montessori in English) into a Chinese parent context.

**What lands:**
- **Lead with bilingual reports.** "Your teachers write notes in English; the parent receives the weekly wrap in Mandarin, written in your school's voice." This is the killer feature for this audience.
- Astra preparing for difficult parent meetings.
- The 30-day free trial. They are very risk-averse on commitment but very willing to try free.
- Whale Class — the founder runs his classroom in Beijing. This is gold.
- Alipay / WeChat Pay invoice rail — they can pay in renminbi.

**What loses them:**
- Pitching in English when they have a Chinese-speaking admin.
- Salesy tone — Chinese principals respect quiet confidence over enthusiasm.
- Overstating Western-school credentials when the principal is sophisticated.

## 3. The Latin American association director (multiplier)

**Geography:** Argentina, Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, Chile, Peru, Uruguay.

**Profile:** Runs a Montessori association, training centre, or a network of affiliated schools. Often AMI-trained. Has 5-50 affiliate schools they could put us in front of.

**Pain points:**
- Their affiliate schools all use different systems (paper, Google Sheets, occasionally Compass).
- Spanish-language software in Montessori is rare and poorly translated.
- They want to professionalise the network's data + reporting.

**What lands:**
- The multiplier partnership pitch (20% revenue share on every affiliate school they bring on).
- Argentine Spanish UI (voseo, AMI Spanish terminology).
- Whale Class as the proof.
- The 60-day pilot for their first affiliate school.

**What loses them:**
- Treating them as a single-school owner.
- English-only pitches.
- Missing the point that they have a NETWORK to influence.

## 4. The European school director

**Geography:** Germany, France, Italy, Netherlands, Switzerland, Austria, Belgium, Spain, Portugal.

**Profile:** Often runs a Maria-Montessori-Schule or equivalent. State-funded or independent. AMI- or AMS-trained. Formal, careful, decision-maker but consensus-oriented.

**Pain points:**
- GDPR. Will ask about data residency and EU compliance.
- Native-language reports for their local-language families.
- Integration with state reporting requirements (varies by country).

**What lands:**
- Native UI in their language (we have DE, FR, IT, NL, PT — but not Spanish in Iberian dialect yet, just LatAm).
- The privacy + encryption story (AES-256-GCM on messages + meeting notes).
- Stripe via SEPA-compatible payment methods.

**What loses them:**
- Casual American-style pitches.
- Vague answers on data residency.
- Anything that sounds like surveillance ("we know everything about every child" lands badly here — replace with "every observation has a clear audit trail").

## 5. The startup-founder principal (Indian / Southeast Asian)

**Geography:** India, Indonesia, Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Thailand.

**Profile:** Founder of a single-school operation, often with ambitions to scale to a small chain. Tech-comfortable, English-fluent or English-functional. Reads on WhatsApp more than email. Price-conscious but quality-aware.

**Pain points:**
- Demonstrating Montessori credibility against the "we are Montessori-inspired" competitors.
- Reaching aspirational parents who want to know academic rigour.
- Hiring + retaining trained Montessori teachers.

**What lands:**
- WhatsApp follow-up beats email follow-up.
- Real numbers. "20 students × $7 = $140/month for our base service" is a clearer pitch here than abstract value.
- Built-in pedagogical rigour (the AMI-aligned curriculum); the principal doesn't need to be Montessori-trained to run a Montessori-rigorous program.
- Indian English UI is well supported (Hindi parent reports are not yet — be honest).

**What loses them:**
- Pretending price isn't a consideration.
- Lecturing on Montessori philosophy.

## 6. The founder-principal new to Montessori

**Geography:** Anywhere, but often emerging Montessori markets (UAE, Saudi Arabia, parts of Eastern Europe, parts of Africa).

**Profile:** Has bought into the Montessori philosophy, often hired AMI/AMS teachers, runs the business side. Doesn't have the lived classroom experience.

**Pain points:**
- Doesn't trust their own pedagogical instincts. Doesn't know what's good Montessori practice and what's not.
- Worried their teachers know more than they do.

**What lands:**
- "The pedagogical rigour is built in — you don't need to be Montessori-trained to run a Montessori-rigorous program. Astra and Guru are your AMI Montessori experts on tap."
- The 60-day free trial. They want a low-risk way to try.
- Mira herself (in the pitch deck): "you have a growth partner who knows how to bring Montree to other schools like yours."

**What loses them:**
- Assuming pedagogical knowledge.
- Anything that makes them feel less qualified to run their own school.
