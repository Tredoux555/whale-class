The Montessori Mathematics Curriculum

100 works from zero to abstraction — built for the home
Montree · Home Curriculum Series

The Mathematical Mind

Montessori mathematics begins with the hands. Before a child ever writes a numeral, they hold quantity — they feel that four is heavier than two, that a thousand-cube is something you need both arms to carry. The genius of the method is that abstraction arrives not because we explain it, but because the child discovers it after hundreds of concrete encounters.

This curriculum gives you 100 works that follow that exact journey: from a toddler's first experience of "one and many" all the way to long division and the passage into abstract thought. Every work uses materials you already have at home or can make in minutes. No catalogue required.

"The hands are the instruments of man's intelligence." — Maria Montessori

Present them in order. Follow the child's pace. When the hands understand, the mind will follow.

Phase 1 · Works 1–25

The Concrete Mind

Quantity before symbol. The child holds mathematics in their hands — counting real objects, feeling weight and length, building an unshakeable foundation of what numbers actually mean.
01

One and Many

Develops: First concept of quantity — the difference between singular and plural
Ready when: Child can follow simple instructions; points to objects when named
At home: Place one apple on the table. Then place a group of apples. "This is one apple. These are many apples." Repeat with spoons, blocks, shoes — any familiar objects. Let the child sort: one here, many there.
02

Big and Small Sorting

Develops: Size discrimination — the visual foundation for understanding magnitude
Ready when: Child notices size differences; reaches for the bigger cookie
At home: Collect pairs of objects in two sizes — big spoon and little spoon, big ball and little ball. Have the child sort them into two groups. Introduce "bigger" and "smaller" as they work.
03

Long and Short Sorting

Develops: Length discrimination — preparation for measurement and the number rods
Ready when: Child can visually compare two objects and indicate which is bigger
At home: Cut ribbons, straws, or strips of paper in clearly different lengths. Ask the child to find the longest, then the shortest. Line them up from short to long.
04

Counting to Three with Objects

Develops: One-to-one correspondence — the understanding that each count word maps to exactly one object
Ready when: Child attempts to count (even inaccurately); points at objects sequentially
At home: Place three stones on a tray. Touch each one as you count slowly: "One. Two. Three." Then ask: "Can you give me two stones?" Keep it physical — the hand touches each one.
05

Number Rods — Quantities 1–5

Develops: Fixed quantity — each rod IS the number, not a collection of separate pieces
Ready when: Child can count to 3 reliably with objects; interested in comparing lengths
At home: Cut 5 strips of cardboard in lengths proportional to 1–5 (e.g., 10cm, 20cm, 30cm, 40cm, 50cm). Alternate red and blue sections in 10cm units. Name each: "This is one. This is two." Let the child build the staircase.
06

Number Rods — Quantities 1–10

Develops: The full set of base quantities; muscular memory of how "ten" feels compared to "one"
Ready when: Child confidently names rods 1–5 and builds the staircase independently
At home: Extend to 10 strips. The ten-rod should be noticeably heavy and long — the child needs to carry it with both hands. This physical weight IS the mathematical concept. Build the staircase, name each rod.
07

Sandpaper Numerals 1–5

Develops: Symbol recognition — connecting the written numeral to a quantity the child already knows
Ready when: Child knows the number rods 1–5 by name (can point to "three" when asked)
At home: Cut numerals 1–5 from fine sandpaper and glue to cardboard. Trace each with two fingers while saying the name. The tactile sensation anchors the symbol in muscle memory. Three-period lesson: "This is 3. Show me 3. What is this?"
08

Sandpaper Numerals 6–10

Develops: Full symbol set for base-ten; bridges quantity knowledge to written form
Ready when: Child traces 1–5 confidently and can name them without prompting
At home: Same method — sandpaper on card. Introduce 6, 7, 8 as one group, then 9 and 0 together. Pay special attention to 6/9 reversal — always trace in the correct direction. "Zero means nothing — it's the empty set."
09

Number Rods and Cards

Develops: Association — matching the symbol to the quantity it represents
Ready when: Child knows both the rods (quantity) and the sandpaper numerals (symbol) independently
At home: Write numerals 1–10 on small cards. Lay out the rods in staircase order. The child places the correct card at the end of each rod. This is the moment quantity meets symbol — watch for the click of understanding.
10

Spindle Boxes

Develops: Zero as empty set; quantities as groups of individual units; counting accuracy
Ready when: Child can match numeral cards to number rods; understands quantities 1–10
At home: Label 10 cups or compartments 0–9. Provide 45 identical small objects (beans, pasta, buttons). The child counts the correct number into each compartment. The "0" compartment stays empty — this is the child's first real encounter with zero.
11

Cards and Counters

Develops: Odd and even; visual pattern of quantities; independent counting verification
Ready when: Child can count out specific quantities and place correct numeral with each group
At home: Number cards 1–10 in a row. Under each card, the child places that many counters (buttons or coins) in pairs — two columns. Odd numbers have one counter sticking out at the bottom. "Can a partner walk through? Even. Does one get left out? Odd."
12

Counting Backwards from 10

Develops: Reverse sequence — essential preparation for subtraction
Ready when: Child counts forward to 10 confidently and knows the number sequence
At home: Build a tower of 10 blocks. Remove one at a time, counting: "Ten, nine, eight…" Make it a game — "rocket launch countdown." Also practice with the number rod staircase in reverse order.
13

Memory Game of Numbers

Develops: Working memory; independent counting; confidence with quantities to 10
Ready when: Child can count and fetch specific quantities; beginning to work independently
At home: Whisper a number to the child. They walk across the room, collect that many objects from a basket, and bring them back. Check together. "I asked for seven — let's count. One, two… seven! Perfect." Increase distance for bigger challenge.
14

Number Line Walk

Develops: Number as position on a line; preparation for addition as "steps forward"
Ready when: Child knows numerals 0–10 and counts with accuracy
At home: Tape a number line 0–10 on the floor using masking tape and written numerals. The child stands on a number and takes steps forward or back. "You're on 4. Take 3 steps. Where are you now?" This IS addition and subtraction — they just don't know it yet.
15

Teen Quantity Building

Develops: Understanding that teen numbers are "ten and some more" — the gateway to place value
Ready when: Child has a solid, confident grasp of 1–10; curious about bigger numbers
At home: Bundle 10 straws or sticks together with a rubber band — this IS ten, one bundle. Place loose straws beside it. "Ten and one more is eleven. Ten and two more is twelve." The child builds each teen number physically.
16

Teen Numeral Cards

Develops: Reading and writing teen numerals; seeing "1" in the tens place
Ready when: Child builds teen quantities with bundles and understands "ten and ___"
At home: Make two sets of cards: a "10" card and cards 1–9. Slide the unit card over the zero in "10" to make 11, 12, 13… The child sees that "13" means the "10" is hiding behind the "3." Match cards to quantities.
17

Ten Board — 11 to 19

Develops: Systematic construction of teen numbers; fluency with the ten-plus pattern
Ready when: Child understands teen numeral cards and can build teen quantities
At home: Write "10" ten times in a column on a board or paper. Make small cards 1–9. The child slides a card next to the first "10" to make "11," places one bead beside it. Next line: slide "2" to make "12," place two beads. Continue to 19.
18

Ten Board — 10 to 99

Develops: Counting by tens; the structure of two-digit numbers; decade names
Ready when: Child has mastered the teen board and knows that numbers continue past 19
At home: Write "10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90" in a column. Same sliding cards 1–9. Slide "1" next to "20" to make "21." Add corresponding bead quantities. The child discovers the pattern: every decade is a new set of ten, and the units count up inside it.
19

Hundred Chain (Skip Counting by 10)

Develops: Linear counting to 100; visualizing what a hundred looks like laid end to end
Ready when: Child can count by tens to 100; knows decade names
At home: String 100 beads (pasta or craft beads) in groups of 10 using different colors per decade. Stretch the chain across the floor. The child places number markers at each ten: 10, 20, 30… Count every bead to 100. This is a significant milestone — celebrate it.
20

Golden Beads — Units, Tens, Hundreds, Thousands

Develops: Place value — physically experiencing that 10 units make a ten-bar, 10 ten-bars make a hundred-square, 10 hundred-squares make a thousand-cube
Ready when: Child counts to 100; understands that "ten" is a group; curious about big numbers
At home: Unit: one small bead. Ten: 10 beads glued in a line on a stick. Hundred: 10 ten-bars glued into a flat square. Thousand: a cube made of 10 hundred-squares (use a small box). Let the child hold each. "Feel how heavy a thousand is compared to one."
21

Large Numeral Cards (1–9000)

Develops: Reading large numbers; understanding how digits stack by place value
Ready when: Child can identify golden bead categories (unit, ten, hundred, thousand)
At home: Make four sets of cards in different sizes: green units (1–9), blue tens (10–90), red hundreds (100–900), green thousands (1000–9000). Layer them: 2000 + 300 + 40 + 5 = 2345. The child sees that every digit has a place, and the zeros are hidden beneath.
22

Golden Bead Number Building

Develops: Composing numbers — building any quantity using place value categories
Ready when: Child knows both the golden bead categories and the large numeral cards
At home: Show the child a large numeral card (e.g., 1342). They fetch the golden bead quantity: 1 thousand-cube, 3 hundred-squares, 4 ten-bars, 2 units. Verify by counting. Then reverse: show beads, child builds the numeral card.
23

Golden Bead Addition — Two Addends

Develops: The concept of addition as "putting together" — with concrete materials showing the process
Ready when: Child can build four-digit numbers with golden beads; understands place value categories
At home: Two trays, each with a numeral card and corresponding beads. "Let's put them together." Combine all units into one pile, all tens, all hundreds, all thousands. Count each category. If units exceed 9, exchange 10 units for 1 ten-bar — the child discovers carrying.
24

Golden Bead Subtraction

Develops: Subtraction as "taking away" — with concrete materials showing borrowing/exchanging
Ready when: Child has done golden bead addition and understands the exchange process
At home: Start with a large number in beads (e.g., 3456). "We need to take away 1278." Take from units first — not enough? Exchange one ten-bar for 10 units, then take. The child physically breaks down larger categories. This IS borrowing — they're living it.
25

Golden Bead Multiplication — Introduction

Develops: Multiplication as "the same quantity taken several times" — concrete and visual
Ready when: Child is fluent with golden bead addition and exchanging
At home: Three trays each hold the same number (e.g., 1234). "We're taking 1234 three times." Combine all three trays — units together, tens together, etc. Exchange as needed. The child sees multiplication as repeated addition with their own hands.
Sensitive Period

Numbers and Quantity (3.5 – 5 years)

Peak window: approximately age 4 to 4.5
You will know this period has arrived when your child starts counting everything — stairs, grapes, cars in the parking lot. They become fascinated with "how many" and may insist on knowing the exact quantity of things. They want to know what comes after 100. They line things up and sort by size. This is the moment to pour mathematical materials into their environment. The absorbent mind is building its number sense, and every concrete experience now becomes the foundation for abstract reasoning later. Do not rush to paper and pencil — keep it in the hands.
Phase 2 · Works 26–50

The Working Hand

Operations take form. The child works with stamp games, bead bars, and strip boards — still holding mathematics in their hands, but moving from the bulk of golden beads toward more efficient representations.
26

Golden Bead Division — Introduction

Develops: Division as "sharing equally" — the last of the four operations introduced concretely
Ready when: Child is comfortable with golden bead multiplication; understands exchanging
At home: Build a large number (e.g., 3696) in beads. "We need to share equally between 3 people." Give out one category at a time — thousands first, then hundreds, then tens, then units. Can't share 3 ten-bars among 3? Each gets 1. Exchange remainders down. Each person's tray shows the quotient.
27

Stamp Game — Introduction

Develops: More abstract representation of place value — tiles replace bulk materials
Ready when: Child has done all four operations with golden beads and is ready for something "smaller"
At home: Make small square tiles in four colours: green "1"s, blue "10"s, red "100"s, green "1000"s. These replace the bulky beads. Build numbers by laying out the right tiles. "Show me 2,453." The child counts out tiles by category.
28

Stamp Game Addition

Develops: Addition with a more abstract material — same carrying process, faster execution
Ready when: Child can build numbers with stamp tiles and understands golden bead addition
At home: Two numbers in stamp tiles. Combine categories. 10 or more green "1" tiles? Exchange for one blue "10" tile. Same process, smaller materials. The child now does four-digit addition in minutes rather than the long golden bead layout.
29

Stamp Game Subtraction

Develops: Subtraction with efficient materials; borrowing/exchanging reinforced
Ready when: Child does stamp game addition fluently
At home: Build the larger number in tiles. Take away the second number, category by category. Not enough units? Exchange one "10" tile for ten "1" tiles. The process is identical to golden beads but faster — the child is internalizing.
30

Stamp Game Multiplication

Develops: Multiplication with efficient materials; seeing the pattern of repeated addition
Ready when: Child understands stamp game operations; can multiply with golden beads
At home: Same number built 3 or 4 times in stamp tiles. Combine. Exchange. The child is doing multi-digit multiplication concretely but efficiently — an elegant bridge between the bulky golden beads and the coming pencil-and-paper algorithms.
31

Stamp Game Division

Develops: Long division process — sharing tiles equally among divisor groups
Ready when: Child can do golden bead division and stamp game multiplication
At home: Build the dividend in tiles. Share among 2, 3, or 4 groups. Start from thousands, work down. Exchange any remainder into the next smaller category and continue sharing. The child is doing long division — physically, accurately, with understanding.
32

Addition Strip Board

Develops: All addition combinations to 18; visual/spatial understanding of sums
Ready when: Child understands addition as combining; ready to explore all combinations
At home: Draw a grid numbered 1–18 across the top. Cut red strips (1–9) and blue strips (1–9). To add 5+7: place the red 5-strip, then the blue 7-strip end to end. Read where the blue strip ends: 12. Work through all combinations systematically.
33

Addition Strip Board — Recording

Develops: Connecting physical strip work to written equations; building addition tables
Ready when: Child uses the strip board fluently and wants to write things down
At home: Provide a recording sheet. As the child works each combination on the strip board, they write the equation: 5 + 7 = 12. Over time, the child builds their own addition table — they discover patterns (commutative property) by seeing that 5+7 and 7+5 land in the same place.
34

Subtraction Strip Board

Develops: All subtraction combinations from numbers up to 18; visual understanding of difference
Ready when: Child has completed addition strip board work; understands subtraction as "taking away"
At home: Same grid. Place the red strip for the minuend (e.g., 12). Lay the blue strip for the subtrahend (e.g., 5) from the right end backward. The uncovered portion shows the difference: 7. Work through all combinations and record.
35

Multiplication Bead Bars

Develops: Multiplication as arrays; visual representation of "groups of"
Ready when: Child understands multiplication as repeated addition; ready for more systematic exploration
At home: Make bead bars by stringing beads on pipe cleaners (or use small counting rods): bars of 1 through 10. For 4 × 3: lay out four bars of 3 side by side. Count all beads: 12. The rectangle of beads IS the product — the child sees the shape of multiplication.
36

Multiplication Board

Develops: Systematic exploration of all multiplication facts; building the times tables
Ready when: Child has explored multiplication with bead bars and wants to know "all the answers"
At home: Draw a 10×10 grid. Mark columns 1–10 and rows 1–10. Using small beads or counters, the child fills: 3 × 4 means place 4 beads in each of 3 rows. Count all beads and write the product in the grid. The child builds their own multiplication table through discovery.
37

Division Board

Develops: Systematic exploration of division facts; understanding division as sharing
Ready when: Child has explored multiplication on the board; understands equal sharing
At home: Draw a grid with divisor markings 1–9 across the top. For 18 ÷ 3: place 18 beads, share equally into 3 columns. Each column gets 6 beads — the quotient. The child discovers that 18 ÷ 3 = 6 because 3 × 6 = 18.
38

Skip Counting Chains — 2s

Develops: Skip counting by twos; preparation for multiplication facts; linear number sense
Ready when: Child counts by ones to 100; beginning multiplication work
At home: String beads in groups of 2 (alternating colours). Stretch the chain across the floor. Place an arrow marker at every second bead: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10… Count by 2s all the way. The child is building the 2-times table physically — 2, 4, 6, 8, 10 beads.
39

Skip Counting Chains — 3s and 4s

Develops: Skip counting patterns; multiplication readiness; rhythmic counting
Ready when: Child has counted the 2-chain independently
At home: Same method — bead chains in groups of 3 and groups of 4 with arrow markers. The 3-chain goes to 27 (3³), the 4-chain to 64 (4³). At the square numbers (9, 16), fold the chain into a square shape so the child sees it. At the cube numbers, build a cube.
40

Skip Counting Chains — 5s and 10s

Develops: The anchor multiplication facts; connection to money and time
Ready when: Child has done the 2, 3, and 4 chains
At home: The 5-chain goes to 125 (5³) — stretch it long. The 10-chain goes to 1000 (10³) — this is the same hundred chain from Work 19, now counted by 10s. Place markers at every 50 and 100. The child begins to see the relationships: 5 × 2 = 10, 5 × 20 = 100.
41

Coin Counting and Money Games

Develops: Applied mathematics — skip counting with real value; exchange and equivalence
Ready when: Child skip counts by 5s and 10s; curious about money
At home: Real coins (or paper replicas). Sort by type. Count: "10, 20, 30, 40 — that's 40 cents." Play shop — price items with stickers, make purchases, give change. The child applies addition, subtraction, and exchange to something deeply meaningful.
42

Dot Game — Addition with Carrying

Develops: Recording addition with carrying on paper — the bridge to abstract written algorithms
Ready when: Child does stamp game addition fluently; can write numerals
At home: Draw columns labeled Th, H, T, U in coloured pencils (green, red, blue, green). Write two addends. The child makes dots in each column for each digit, then counts dots. 10+ dots? Cross out ten, add one dot to the next column. Read the answer. This is the bridge to carrying on paper.
43

Dot Game — Subtraction with Borrowing

Develops: Recording subtraction with borrowing on paper; the last step before pure pencil algorithms
Ready when: Child does dot game addition; understands exchanging from stamp game subtraction
At home: Same column format. Write minuend and subtrahend. The child crosses out dots from the top number. Not enough dots in a column? Draw an arrow from the next column — cross out one dot there, add ten dots here. Read the remainder. Borrowing is now a paper procedure rooted in concrete understanding.
44

Fractions — The Whole

Develops: Understanding that fractions begin with ONE whole — and that fractions are parts of that whole
Ready when: Child is comfortable with numbers to 1000; curious when they see a pizza sliced
At home: A whole apple. "This is one whole." Cut it in half. "Now I have two halves. Each half is one of two equal parts." Put them back together — still one whole. Cut into quarters. Name each piece. The key: always start with the whole, always name the relationship to the whole.
45

Fraction Circles — Halves to Tenths

Develops: Visual fraction sense — seeing that ½ is bigger than ¼; that 3/3 = 1 whole
Ready when: Child understands that cutting the whole makes equal parts; can name halves and quarters
At home: Cut identical circles from coloured paper. One stays whole (label "1"). Cut another into 2 equal pieces (label each "½"). Another into 3 pieces ("⅓"), another into 4 ("¼")… up to 10. Place them on the whole circle to show equivalence. "How many fourths make a whole? Let's check."
46

Fraction Equivalence

Develops: The discovery that ½ = 2/4 = 3/6 — different names for the same amount
Ready when: Child has explored fraction circles and can name fractions; begins to notice "these look the same size"
At home: Using the fraction circles: place ½ on the whole circle. "Can you find other pieces that cover exactly the same space?" The child discovers that 2 quarters, 3 sixths, 4 eighths, and 5 tenths all equal one half. Record each discovery.
47

Telling Time — Hours and Half-Hours

Develops: Applied mathematics — reading analog clocks; connection between fractions and time
Ready when: Child counts to 12; understands halves from fraction work
At home: A clock with moveable hands (or make one from a paper plate with a brad fastener). Start with hours only: "When the big hand points to 12 and the little hand points to 3, it's 3 o'clock." Then half-hours: "The big hand has gone halfway around — half past 3." Connect to daily routine: "We eat lunch at 12 o'clock."
48

Measurement — Length

Develops: Non-standard and standard measurement; comparison and estimation
Ready when: Child compares lengths spontaneously; asks "how big is it?"
At home: Start non-standard: "How many hands long is the table?" Then introduce a ruler. Measure everything in the house. Record measurements in a notebook. Compare: "The bookshelf is 90 centimetres. The table is 120 centimetres. Which is longer? How much longer?"
49

Measurement — Weight

Develops: Comparison by weight; estimation; introduction to units of mass
Ready when: Child has explored length measurement; notices that some things are heavier than others
At home: Make a simple balance from a coat hanger and two cups on strings. Place objects in each cup to compare. "Which is heavier — the apple or the orange?" Introduce a kitchen scale for standard measurement. Weigh baking ingredients — applied maths in action.
50

Measurement — Capacity

Develops: Volume and capacity concepts; estimation; pouring control
Ready when: Child loves water play; has done length and weight measurement
At home: Measuring cups and a water basin. "How many small cups fill the big jug?" Estimate first, then test. Introduce standard units: "This cup holds 250 millilitres. The jug holds 1 litre. How many cups to fill the jug?" Record predictions and results.
Sensitive Period

Exactness and Precision (3 – 6 years)

Peak window: approximately age 4.5 to 5.5
This period emerges when your child starts insisting on getting things exactly right — lining up blocks perfectly, counting and recounting to make sure, becoming frustrated when something is "almost" but not exact. They want to measure precisely, cut on the line, count to the last bead. This is not rigidity — it is the mathematical mind awakening. It craves precision because precision is how the mind builds trust in its own reasoning. Feed this hunger with materials that have built-in control of error: strip boards where the answer can be verified, measurement tools that give exact numbers, fraction circles that must fit together perfectly.
Phase 3 · Works 51–75

The Reasoning Mind

From materials to memory. The child begins to hold mathematics mentally — memorizing facts, solving equations on paper, and discovering patterns in numbers. The hands still touch materials, but the mind increasingly works alone.
51

Addition Memorization — Finger Charts

Develops: Automatic recall of addition facts; speed and confidence
Ready when: Child has worked through the addition strip board and recorded all combinations
At home: Make a chart with addends along the top and side. The child finds where a row and column meet: 6 + 8 = 14. Practice with random equation cards — child looks up the answer on the chart, then tries without looking. The chart scaffolds; the goal is independence from it.
52

Subtraction Memorization — Finger Charts

Develops: Automatic recall of subtraction facts; connection to inverse of addition
Ready when: Child is memorizing addition facts; has completed subtraction strip board
At home: Same chart format for subtraction. The child notices that knowing 6 + 8 = 14 means 14 − 8 = 6. Draw equation cards — the child checks against the chart then works toward mental recall. Flashcard games, oral quiz during car rides.
53

Multiplication Memorization — Finger Charts

Develops: Automatic recall of times tables; the foundation for all advanced mathematics
Ready when: Child has built the multiplication table on the board; skip counts fluently
At home: A multiplication chart — but the child BUILT it during the board work, so it's familiar. Draw random fact cards (7 × 8). Look up on chart, then cover the chart and try. Practice one table at a time: all the 3s, then the 4s. Chant them rhythmically. The goal is instant recall.
54

Division Memorization — Finger Charts

Develops: Automatic recall of division facts; inverse multiplication relationships
Ready when: Child is memorizing multiplication facts and sees the inverse relationship
At home: Division chart. The child discovers: "If I know 7 × 8 = 56, then 56 ÷ 8 = 7." Equation cards, timed challenges, daily practice. Division is often the slowest to memorize — patience. The concrete board work gives it meaning; repetition gives it speed.
55

Written Addition — Standard Algorithm

Develops: The formal pencil-and-paper addition procedure — now grounded in deep understanding
Ready when: Child has done dot game addition; is memorizing addition facts; can write numerals neatly
At home: Write two four-digit numbers in columns (units under units, tens under tens). Add each column. "If the sum is 10 or more, write the ones digit and carry the ten." The child already understands WHY from golden beads and stamp game — now the notation catches up to the understanding.
56

Written Subtraction — Standard Algorithm

Develops: Formal subtraction with borrowing — the notation for what the child already does concretely
Ready when: Child does written addition; has done dot game subtraction with borrowing
At home: Same column format. Subtract each column right to left. "Not enough in this column? Borrow from the next — cross out and add 10." The child has done this with beads, tiles, and dots — the algorithm is simply the written record of a process they've lived dozens of times.
57

Written Multiplication — Single-Digit Multiplier

Develops: Multi-digit multiplication on paper; applying memorized facts in a structured algorithm
Ready when: Child knows multiplication facts; has done stamp game multiplication
At home: Write a four-digit number × a one-digit number. Multiply each digit, right to left, carrying as needed. "4 × 7 = 28. Write 8, carry 2." The child verifies with stamp game if unsure — the concrete backup remains available but is used less and less.
58

Written Division — Single-Digit Divisor

Develops: Long division on paper — the most complex algorithm, now supported by deep concrete experience
Ready when: Child knows division facts; has done stamp game division; can do written multiplication
At home: Write dividend inside the bracket, divisor outside. Work left to right: "How many times does 3 go into 7? Twice, with 1 left over. Bring down the next digit." Verify each step with multiplication. If stuck, return to stamp game to see the physical sharing.
59

Word Problems — Single Operation

Develops: Reading comprehension meets mathematical reasoning; identifying the operation in context
Ready when: Child does all four written operations; reads independently or with help
At home: Write simple story problems from daily life: "You had 15 strawberries. You ate 6. How many are left?" The child must decide: add, subtract, multiply, or divide? Then solve. Start with real situations the child has experienced — shopping, cooking, sharing snacks.
60

Number Patterns and Sequences

Develops: Pattern recognition — the heart of mathematical thinking
Ready when: Child skip counts; can continue simple patterns (red, blue, red, blue…)
At home: Start with visual patterns using objects: ABAB, ABCABC, AABB. Move to number patterns: 2, 4, 6, ___, ___. Then: 1, 3, 5, ___, ___. Then harder: 1, 2, 4, 7, 11, ___. The child fills in the blank and explains the rule. "It goes up by one more each time."
61

Squaring — Physical

Develops: Square numbers as actual squares — the geometric meaning of "squared"
Ready when: Child knows multiplication facts; has made square shapes with bead chains
At home: Using bead bars or counters: build a 3 × 3 square (9 beads arranged in a square), a 4 × 4 square (16), a 5 × 5 square (25). "We call it '3 squared' because the beads make a square. 3² = 9." The child SEES that squaring IS making a square. Record: 1² = 1, 2² = 4, 3² = 9…
62

Cubing — Physical

Develops: Cube numbers as actual cubes — extending the squaring concept to three dimensions
Ready when: Child understands square numbers and has built squares physically
At home: Using small blocks or sugar cubes: build a 2 × 2 × 2 cube (8 blocks), a 3 × 3 × 3 cube (27 blocks). "We call it '2 cubed' because the blocks make a cube. 2³ = 8." Count all the blocks. This connects to the bead chain cubes: the 3-chain's 27 beads, the 4-chain's 64 beads.
63

Fraction Addition — Same Denominator

Develops: Operating on fractions — adding like parts
Ready when: Child names fractions confidently; understands equivalence; adds whole numbers on paper
At home: Using fraction circles: place ¼ and another ¼ on the whole. "One fourth plus one fourth is two fourths." Lay 2/4 alongside ½ — they're the same! Record: ¼ + ¼ = 2/4 = ½. Work through many examples: ⅓ + ⅓, ⅛ + 3/8. The circles verify every answer.
64

Fraction Subtraction — Same Denominator

Develops: Subtracting like fractions; fraction sense for comparing results
Ready when: Child does fraction addition with same denominators
At home: Start with ¾ on the whole circle. Remove ¼. "Three fourths minus one fourth is two fourths, which equals one half." Verify with the circles. Record the equation. Mix operations: "You have 5/8 of a pizza. You eat 3/8. What's left?"
65

Fraction Comparison and Ordering

Develops: Intuitive understanding that ⅓ > ¼; the smaller the denominator, the larger the piece
Ready when: Child has worked with fraction circles extensively and adds/subtracts like fractions
At home: Challenge: "Which is bigger — ⅓ or ¼?" Place both on the whole circle to check. Order a set of fractions from smallest to largest: ⅛, ¼, ⅓, ½. "But wait — what about 3/8 vs ⅓?" This forces the child to think about equivalent fractions and common denominators — the next step.
66

Decimals — Introduction via Fractions

Develops: Understanding that decimals are fractions in disguise — tenths, hundredths
Ready when: Child understands fractions to tenths; knows place value to thousands
At home: The fraction 1/10 can also be written as 0.1. Using fraction circles, show that 3/10 = 0.3 and 7/10 = 0.7. Then extend: "If we cut each tenth into ten pieces, we get hundredths. 1/100 = 0.01." Use money: "A dime is one tenth of a dollar. A penny is one hundredth."
67

Decimal Addition and Subtraction

Develops: Operating on decimals using place value alignment; applied fraction arithmetic
Ready when: Child understands decimal notation and can read decimal numbers
At home: Line up the decimal points and add/subtract just like whole numbers: 3.25 + 1.48 = 4.73. Use money as the concrete reference: "$3.25 + $1.48 — how much altogether?" The child already knows how to add with carrying; the decimal point just needs to stay in its place.
68

Telling Time — Minutes and Elapsed Time

Develops: Reading time to the minute; calculating how much time has passed
Ready when: Child tells time to the half-hour; skip counts by 5s
At home: Practice reading the clock to five-minute intervals: "The big hand is on the 3, that's 15 minutes past." Then to the minute. Elapsed time: "We started at 2:15 and finished at 3:00. How long did that take?" Use a real clock during daily activities — cooking timers, walk durations.
69

Graphing and Data Collection

Develops: Organizing information visually; reading and interpreting charts
Ready when: Child counts and sorts; interested in collecting information
At home: Choose a survey topic: "What's everyone's favourite fruit?" Collect data from family members. Make a bar graph with coloured squares. "How many people chose apples? Which fruit was most popular? How many more chose banana than orange?" The child sees numbers become a picture.
70

Geometry — Shape Identification

Develops: Naming and classifying 2D shapes; vocabulary for geometric properties
Ready when: Child notices shapes in the environment; has done sensorial geometry (geometric cabinet)
At home: Go on a shape walk — find circles, squares, triangles, rectangles everywhere. Then get specific: "How many sides does a triangle have? A pentagon?" Cut shapes from paper. Count sides and corners. Sort by number of sides. "Shapes with 4 sides are called quadrilaterals — quad means four."
71

Geometry — Perimeter

Develops: Measuring around a shape; addition applied to geometry
Ready when: Child identifies shapes; measures length with a ruler
At home: Measure around objects: "The book is 20cm + 15cm + 20cm + 15cm = 70cm around." Measure rooms by walking and counting steps. "The perimeter is the distance all the way around." Use string to measure irregular shapes — wrap the string, then straighten and measure.
72

Geometry — Area Introduction

Develops: Understanding area as the space inside a shape; multiplication applied to geometry
Ready when: Child understands perimeter; knows multiplication; understands square numbers
At home: Draw a rectangle on grid paper. Count the squares inside: "This rectangle is 4 squares long and 3 squares wide. That's 4 × 3 = 12 square units." Cover a table with square tiles and count. "Area is how much space the surface covers." Compare: a big book has more area than a small book.
73

Commutative and Associative Properties

Develops: Discovering that 3+5 = 5+3 and (2+3)+4 = 2+(3+4) — mathematical laws from concrete experience
Ready when: Child is fluent with addition and multiplication; notices "these give the same answer"
At home: Build 3 × 5 with beads (3 rows of 5). Rotate the mat: now it's 5 × 3 (5 rows of 3). Same number of beads! "The order doesn't change the answer — that's the commutative property." For associative: "(2+3)+4 = 5+4 = 9" and "2+(3+4) = 2+7 = 9." Same! Discover, don't lecture.
74

Estimation and Rounding

Develops: Number sense — knowing approximately how big an answer should be before calculating
Ready when: Child does multi-digit operations; has a sense of number magnitude
At home: Jar of beans — "How many do you think? Guess first, then count." Rounding: "287 is closest to 300. 312 is closest to 300 too. So 287 + 312 is about 600." Always estimate BEFORE calculating. "Does your answer make sense?" This builds the internal compass that catches errors.
75

Multi-Step Word Problems

Develops: Complex reasoning — reading, planning, executing multiple operations in sequence
Ready when: Child does single-operation word problems; has all four written operations
At home: "You have $10. You buy a book for $3.50 and a pencil for $1.25. How much change do you get?" The child must: add the purchases (3.50 + 1.25 = 4.75), then subtract from 10 (10.00 − 4.75 = 5.25). Teach the habit of underlining what the question asks, then planning the steps before calculating.
Sensitive Period

Order and Pattern (1 – 3.5 years, echoes through age 6)

Peak window: approximately age 18 months to 2.5 years; mathematical echo at 4–6
The sensitive period for order — lining things up, insisting on sequence, needing things "in the right place" — is the earliest root of the mathematical mind. It resurfaces between ages 4 and 6 as a love of pattern, symmetry, and systematic exploration. You will see your child arranging objects by size, counting collections over and over, building repeating patterns with blocks. This is not play — it is the construction of mathematical intelligence. Every pattern discovered, every sequence completed, every set sorted becomes part of the internal framework for abstract reasoning. Honour it with materials that reward precision and reveal pattern.
Phase 4 · Works 76–100

The Passage to Abstraction

The materials fall away. The child now thinks in numbers — solving problems mentally, working with larger numbers and more complex operations, and discovering the elegant structures that underpin all of mathematics.
76

Written Multiplication — Two-Digit Multiplier

Develops: Multi-digit multiplication using partial products; scale and efficiency
Ready when: Child multiplies by single digits fluently on paper; knows multiplication facts
At home: 34 × 26: first multiply 34 × 6 (ones), then 34 × 20 (tens), then add the partial products. Show why the second line shifts left — because you're multiplying by tens, not ones. Use graph paper for column alignment. Verify with estimation: "34 × 26 is about 30 × 25 = 750."
77

Written Division — Two-Digit Divisor

Develops: Long division with estimation; the most complex written algorithm in primary mathematics
Ready when: Child does long division with single-digit divisors fluently
At home: 756 ÷ 18: "How many 18s in 75? About 4. 4 × 18 = 72. Remainder 3. Bring down the 6. How many 18s in 36? Exactly 2." Each step requires estimation, multiplication, and subtraction — the full toolkit working together. Start with friendly divisors (12, 15, 20) before harder ones.
78

Multiples and Factors

Develops: Number theory — understanding the internal structure of numbers
Ready when: Child knows multiplication facts; has done skip counting chains
At home: "What numbers can you multiply to get 12?" The child finds: 1×12, 2×6, 3×4. These are the factor pairs of 12. List multiples of 3: 3, 6, 9, 12, 15… List multiples of 4: 4, 8, 12, 16, 20… "12 appears in both! It's a common multiple." Find the smallest common multiple (LCM). This work has deep Montessori roots in the bead cabinet.
79

Prime Numbers — The Sieve

Develops: Discovery of prime numbers — the building blocks of all numbers
Ready when: Child understands factors and multiples; can identify whether a number divides evenly
At home: Write numbers 1–100 on a grid. Circle 2, then cross out all multiples of 2. Circle 3, cross out its multiples. Circle 5, cross out its multiples. Circle 7, cross out its multiples. The remaining uncrossed numbers are prime. "These are special — they can only be divided by 1 and themselves." The Sieve of Eratosthenes — the child discovers it, not memorizes it.
80

Fraction Addition — Different Denominators

Develops: Finding common denominators; combining unlike fractions
Ready when: Child adds same-denominator fractions; understands equivalence; knows multiples
At home: ½ + ⅓ — use the fraction circles first. "They're different sizes, we can't add them directly." Find a common size: sixths. ½ = 3/6, ⅓ = 2/6. Now: 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6. Verify with the circles. The key skill: finding the LCD (least common denominator) using their knowledge of multiples.
81

Fraction Subtraction — Different Denominators

Develops: Subtracting unlike fractions after finding common denominators
Ready when: Child adds unlike fractions; finds common denominators
At home: ¾ − ⅓ — convert to twelfths: 9/12 − 4/12 = 5/12. Always verify with fraction circles. Real-life problems: "The recipe calls for ¾ cup of flour. You've already put in ⅓ cup. How much more do you need?" The fractions are no longer abstract — they're tools for solving real problems.
82

Fraction Multiplication

Develops: Understanding "of" — ½ of ⅓ means half of a third
Ready when: Child adds and subtracts fractions with unlike denominators
At home: Fold a paper in thirds. Shade one third. Now fold that in half. "Half of one third is one sixth — ½ × ⅓ = ⅙." The algorithm (multiply numerators, multiply denominators) makes sense because the child has SEEN it by folding. Practice with fraction circles: ⅔ × ¾ — shade ⅔ of ¾ of the circle.
83

Fraction Division

Develops: Understanding division of fractions — "how many of these fit into that?"
Ready when: Child multiplies fractions; has a strong conceptual fraction foundation
At home: "How many ¼ pieces fit into ½?" Use the fraction circles: two quarter pieces cover the half. So ½ ÷ ¼ = 2. Then introduce the rule: ½ ÷ ¼ = ½ × 4/1 = 2. "Invert and multiply" has a reason — and the child discovered it by fitting pieces together first.
84

Mixed Numbers and Improper Fractions

Develops: Converting between mixed numbers (1½) and improper fractions (3/2)
Ready when: Child operates on fractions fluently; understands fractions greater than one
At home: Build 1½ with fraction circles — one whole plus one half. "How many halves total? Three halves — we write that 3/2." Convert: 2¼ = how many quarters? The whole = 4/4, so two wholes = 8/4, plus 1/4 = 9/4. Practice both directions. "Why would we want an improper fraction? It's easier to multiply."
85

Decimal Multiplication

Develops: Multiplying decimals; understanding decimal place value in products
Ready when: Child does decimal addition/subtraction; multiplies whole numbers fluently
At home: 2.5 × 3: "That's 2 × 3 plus 0.5 × 3 = 6 + 1.5 = 7.5." Then the shortcut: multiply as if there's no decimal (25 × 3 = 75), count decimal places (1), place the point (7.5). Use money: "$2.50 × 3 = $7.50." The child verifies that the shortcut matches the meaning.
86

Decimal Division

Develops: Dividing decimals; real-world applications of precise sharing
Ready when: Child multiplies decimals and does long division with whole numbers
At home: "$7.50 shared between 3 people — how much each?" 7.50 ÷ 3 = 2.50. Long division with the decimal point carried straight up. Then harder: 7.5 ÷ 0.5 — "How many half-dollars in $7.50? Fifteen." Convert to equivalent whole-number division: 75 ÷ 5 = 15. Both methods, same answer.
87

Percentages — Introduction

Develops: Understanding percent as "per hundred" — linking fractions, decimals, and percentages
Ready when: Child understands fractions and decimals; knows that 1/100 = 0.01
At home: Draw a 10×10 grid (100 squares). Shade 25 squares. "25 out of 100 — we say 25 percent, or 25%." Connect: 25% = 25/100 = ¼ = 0.25. Shade 50 squares: "50% = ½." Real life: "This shirt is 20% off. The original price is $30. 20% of 30 is 6, so you save $6."
88

Ratio and Proportion — Introduction

Develops: Understanding ratio as a relationship between two quantities
Ready when: Child understands fractions and equivalent fractions; can simplify fractions
At home: "For every 2 red beads, we use 3 blue beads." Build the pattern: 2R-3B, 2R-3B, 2R-3B. "The ratio is 2:3." Double the recipe: 4R-6B — still 2:3. Cooking is the perfect context: "The recipe serves 4 but we need to serve 8. Double every ingredient." This is proportional reasoning.
89

Geometry — 3D Shapes and Nets

Develops: Understanding three-dimensional shapes; the relationship between faces, edges, and vertices
Ready when: Child identifies 2D shapes; understands area; can visualize and build
At home: Collect real 3D objects: a ball (sphere), a box (rectangular prism), a can (cylinder), a cone (party hat). Name faces, edges, corners. Then unfold boxes to see the "net" — the flat shape that folds into the 3D form. Cut nets from cardboard and fold: cube, triangular prism, pyramid. Count faces, edges, vertices.
90

Geometry — Volume

Develops: Measuring three-dimensional space; connecting multiplication to spatial reasoning
Ready when: Child understands area; can build 3D shapes; knows cubing
At home: Build a box shape from unit cubes (sugar cubes or blocks): 3 long, 2 wide, 2 tall. Count: 12 cubes. "The volume is 3 × 2 × 2 = 12 cubic units." Connect to capacity: fill the box with water and pour into a measuring cup. "Volume is how much space something takes up — how much it can hold inside."
91

Geometry — Angles

Develops: Understanding angle as rotation; vocabulary (acute, right, obtuse); measurement in degrees
Ready when: Child identifies shapes; understands turns and direction
At home: Open a book to different widths. "This is a small angle — acute. This is a right angle — it makes an L shape. This is a big angle — obtuse." Hunt for right angles in the house (book corners, table edges, door frames). Use a protractor to measure: "This angle is 45 degrees — exactly half of a right angle."
92

Geometry — Symmetry and Transformation

Develops: Line symmetry, rotation, and reflection — foundational geometric reasoning
Ready when: Child recognizes shapes; interested in patterns and art; understands "same on both sides"
At home: Fold paper in half, cut a shape, unfold — symmetry! "The fold line is the line of symmetry." Find symmetry in nature: butterflies, leaves, faces. Reflect shapes using a mirror. Rotate pattern blocks to see which positions look the same. "A square looks the same after a quarter turn — it has rotational symmetry."
93

Negative Numbers — Introduction

Develops: Extending the number line below zero; understanding debits, temperature, and depth
Ready when: Child is comfortable with the number line; understands subtraction as movement left
At home: Extend the number line past zero: …−3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3… "What's 3 − 5? Start at 3, walk 5 steps back: 3, 2, 1, 0, −1, −2. You end at −2." Use a thermometer: "Zero degrees. Five below zero is −5." Elevator: "Basement floors are −1, −2." The child sees that numbers don't stop at zero.
94

Order of Operations

Develops: Understanding that the order of calculation matters; mathematical convention
Ready when: Child does all four operations fluently; encounters problems with multiple operations
At home: "What's 3 + 4 × 2?" If you add first: 7 × 2 = 14. If you multiply first: 3 + 8 = 11. Which is right? Convention says multiply first. Introduce the rule: parentheses first, then multiplication/division, then addition/subtraction. Practice with problems that require the right order. Use parentheses to change the answer: "(3 + 4) × 2 = 14."
95

Algebraic Thinking — Missing Number

Develops: Pre-algebra — finding the unknown; representing the unknown with a symbol
Ready when: Child knows all four operations and their inverse relationships
At home: "___ + 8 = 15. What number goes in the blank?" The child reasons: "15 − 8 = 7." Then use a box or letter: "n × 4 = 24. What is n?" This is algebra — and the child is ready for it because they understand the operations deeply. "If I know three things, I can find the fourth."
96

Algebraic Thinking — Simple Equations

Develops: Solving for an unknown using inverse operations; balance as a metaphor for equality
Ready when: Child solves missing number problems; understands that = means "the same as"
At home: Use a balance scale: place 5 blocks + a mystery bag on one side, 12 blocks on the other. "5 + bag = 12. The bag has 7." On paper: "2x + 3 = 11. Take away 3 from both sides: 2x = 8. Divide both sides by 2: x = 4." Always verify: "Does 2(4) + 3 = 11? Yes!" The balance must stay balanced.
97

Coordinate Graphing

Develops: Plotting points on a grid; connecting numbers to spatial position
Ready when: Child understands number lines; works with graphs; ready for two dimensions
At home: Draw two number lines crossing at zero — the x-axis and y-axis. "The point (3, 2) means go 3 right and 2 up." Plot points to make shapes: (1,1), (4,1), (4,3), (1,3) makes a rectangle. Play "treasure map" — hide a toy and give coordinates. Connect to map reading.
98

Probability — Introduction

Develops: Understanding chance — certain, likely, unlikely, impossible; predicting outcomes
Ready when: Child understands fractions; can reason about what might happen
At home: Bag with 3 red marbles and 1 blue. "If you reach in without looking, which colour are you more likely to pull?" Pull 20 times, record results. "We got red 15 times and blue 5 times — close to our prediction!" Coin flips, dice rolls, spinner wheels — the child discovers that mathematics can describe uncertainty.
99

Mathematical Reasoning — Puzzles and Proofs

Develops: Logical thinking; persistence; the joy of a difficult problem solved
Ready when: Child has a broad mathematical toolkit; enjoys challenge; asks "why does that work?"
At home: Logic puzzles: "I'm thinking of a number. If you double it and add 3, you get 17. What's the number?" Magic squares: arrange 1–9 in a 3×3 grid so every row, column, and diagonal sums to 15. The child learns that mathematics is not just calculating — it's thinking. The reward is the "aha!" moment.
100

The Great Lesson — The Story of Numbers

Develops: Perspective — understanding that mathematics is humanity's greatest invention, not just a school subject
Ready when: The child has journeyed through this entire curriculum and is ready to see the big picture
At home: Tell the story of numbers: early humans using tally marks on bones, ancient Egyptians measuring the Nile, the invention of zero in India, Arabic numerals spreading across the world. "Every civilisation needed mathematics. And now YOU can do things that took humanity thousands of years to discover." Let the child feel the weight of that — they have inherited something extraordinary.
