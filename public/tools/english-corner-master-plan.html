The True AMI Language Sequence

This is the sequence Maria Montessori prescribed. It does not begin with sandpaper letters. It begins with the child's ear, voice, and environment. Written language comes only after a deep oral foundation.

Montessori's Core Insight Writing comes before reading. But spoken language comes before everything. The child must hear sounds, name the world, and develop phonemic awareness long before they touch a sandpaper letter.

Phase 1: Oral Language Foundation

This is where it all begins. No materials on a shelf — this is the teacher's work with the child, every day, through conversation, naming, and structured language games.

1A. Enrichment of Vocabulary (Day 1, never stops)

The teacher names EVERYTHING. Precisely. Consistently. Using the three-period lesson:

The Three-Period Lesson Period 1 (Naming): "This is a pitcher." — Brief, clear, factual.
Period 2 (Recognition): "Show me the pitcher." "Touch the pitcher." "Bring me the pitcher." — The LONGEST period. Movement-based. Repeat 4–5 times.
Period 3 (Recall): "What is this?" — Only ask when you're SURE the child will succeed. If they can't recall, go back to Period 2. Never treat it as a test.

What gets named (progression):

  • Immediate environment: chair, table, shelf, spoon, cup, plate, water, door
  • Child's body: hand, finger, elbow, knee, shoulder, wrist
  • Actions: pouring, sweeping, walking, cutting, folding
  • Qualities (from Sensorial): rough, smooth, long, short, heavy, light, loud, quiet
  • Feelings: happy, sad, frustrated, proud, tired, excited

1B. Grace & Courtesy (These ARE language lessons)

Every Grace & Courtesy lesson teaches exact words for social situations. The child learns what to say, how to say it, and when:

SituationExact Language Taught
Greeting"Good morning. How are you?"
Asking for help"Excuse me, would you help me please?"
Passing by"Excuse me, may I pass?"
Receiving help"Thank you."
Conflict"I don't like it when you... Could you please..."
Joining work"May I watch you?" or "May I work with you?"

1C. Songs, Poems, Fingerplays & Storytelling (Daily)

Not entertainment — structured language absorption. Songs build vocabulary through repetition and rhythm. Stories build narrative comprehension and vocabulary in context. Poems train the ear for sounds and patterns.

  • Circle time: 2–3 songs daily, matched to seasons/themes
  • Storytelling: told from memory (not read), 2–3× per week
  • Poetry: recited aloud by teacher; children absorb and memorize naturally
  • Fingerplays: combine language with motor coordination

1D. Classified Cards / Nomenclature Cards

Three-part cards: Picture only → Picture + Label → Label only. These span the entire 3–6 age range:

StageCards UsedPurposeAge
Presentation 1Picture cards onlyVocabulary enrichment through naming (three-period lesson)3–4
Presentation 2Picture + Label matchingConnecting written word to picture; beginning word recognition3.5–4.5
Presentation 3Label only (reading)Child reads labels independently; decoding practice4.5–5.5

First categories:

  • Tier 1 (age 3–3.5): Common fruits, vegetables, clothing, household objects
  • Tier 2 (age 3.5–4): Animals, furniture, foods, body parts
  • Tier 3 (age 4+): Farm animals, vehicles, tools, botany parts, zoology
AMI Principle: Follow the child's interest. If a child is fascinated by dinosaurs, introduce dinosaur cards immediately — regardless of "sequence." The teacher follows the child's questions.

Phase 2: Sound Games (I Spy) — The 4 Levels

This is the most critical pre-reading work in the entire Montessori language curriculum. The child's ear must be trained to hear individual sounds in words BEFORE they ever see a letter. This work can span 6–18 months.

Non-negotiable: Always use SOUNDS, never letter names. "mmm" not "em." "sss" not "ess." "buh" not "bee." The child is learning to hear phonemes, not recite an alphabet.

1 Initial Sounds Only (age 2.5–3.5)

Child learns to hear the FIRST sound in a word.

"I spy with my little eye something that begins with... /mmm/"
  • Use real objects the child can see (not abstract)
  • Start with 3–5 objects on a tray where the answer is obvious
  • The child names the object, NOT the sound
  • Play daily, 5–10 minutes
  • Duration at this level: 2–4 weeks minimum

2 Initial + Final Sounds (age 3.0–3.5)

Child hears FIRST and LAST sounds in a word.

"I spy something that starts with /b/ and ends with /l/" → ball
  • Place 5–7 objects on a mat
  • Give both beginning AND ending sounds in the clue
  • Objects should have distinct, unambiguous sounds
  • Duration: 2–4 weeks

3 Initial, Middle & Final Sounds (age 3.5–4.0)

Child hears ALL THREE sound positions in a CVC word.

"I spy something that starts with /k/, has /a/ in the middle, and ends with /t/" → cat
  • Use CVC (3-letter) words: cat, dog, mop, pin, bus, hat
  • Always say sounds in order: first → middle → last
  • This is where the child starts to understand that words ARE sequences of sounds
  • Duration: 2–4 weeks

4 Full Segmentation (age 3.5–4.5+)

Child can break ANY word into its individual sounds.

"Let's say 'cat' sound by sound: /k/ /a/ /t/. How many sounds?" → Three!
  • Start with 2–3 sound words, progress to 4–5
  • Child claps or taps for each sound
  • This level continues alongside sandpaper letter work
  • The ear training and letter learning reinforce each other
When is the child ready for sandpaper letters? When they can consistently do Level 3 (hear initial, middle, and final sounds). They don't need to be at Level 4. Levels 3–4 continue IN PARALLEL with sandpaper letters.

Phase 3: Writing Preparation (Parallel Stream)

Metal Insets and Sandpaper Letters run as parallel streams — one prepares the HAND, the other connects SOUND to SYMBOL. They converge in the Moveable Alphabet.

3A. Metal Insets (starts age 3–3.5, parallel with Sound Games)

Purpose: pencil control, lightness of touch, hand-eye coordination. This is NOT letter work — it's hand preparation.

PresentationWhat the Child Does

2Trace the INSET piece inside the frame outline
3Fill with HORIZONTAL lines (left to right)
4Fill with VERTICAL lines

3B. Sandpaper Letters (starts age 4+, after Sound Games Level 3)

The moment of convergence: the child who can HEAR /m/ now SEES and TOUCHES the letter that represents it.

Three channels simultaneously: The child SEES the letter, TOUCHES it (tracing with index + middle finger), and SAYS the sound. Visual + tactile + auditory = powerful memory.

AMI Letter Groups:

Introduce 2–3 letters at a time. Each group has 1 vowel + 3 consonants. New group only when previous group is mastered (Period 3).

3C. Moveable Alphabet (starts when ~10 letters known)

"Writing before reading" — the child composes words with loose letters before their hand can write them with a pencil.

  1. Match moveable letters to known sandpaper letters
  2. Build CVC words with objects (place object → sound out → find letters → build word)
  3. Build CVC words with pictures
  4. Build longer phonetic words
  5. Build phrases → sentences → short stories
Do NOT correct spelling. At this stage the child is encoding sounds they hear. Phonetic spelling is expected and correct. Conventional spelling comes later through reading.

HAVE Moveable Alphabet ✅

Phase 5: Function of Words (Grammar)

Grammar in Montessori is concrete and story-driven. The Grammar Farm is the centerpiece. Each part of speech has a symbol Montessori created.

The Montessori Grammar Symbols

Part of SpeechShapeColorStory
NounLarge triangle ▲BlackThe ancient pyramid — oldest symbol for naming things
ArticleSmall triangle ▵Light blueSmallest servant of the noun — pale and humble
AdjectiveMedium triangle ▲Dark blueDescribes the noun — "Which one? What kind?"
VerbLarge circle ●RedThe sun — energy, action, life. No sentence without it.
AdverbSmall circle ●OrangeSmall sun orbiting the verb — how, when, where
PronounTall triangle ▲PurpleStands in for the noun — a different kind of triangle
PrepositionCrescent ☽GreenA bridge connecting noun to the rest of the sentence
ConjunctionRectangle ▬PinkA bar linking two things together
InterjectionKeyhole 🔑GoldUnlocks emotion — stands alone

The Grammar Farm

HAVE Red barn with animals and fences ✅

Introduction through storytelling:

  • Noun: "Everything has a name. The cow. The barn. The fence. These NAMING words are called nouns." → Place black triangle ▲
  • Article: "Bring me cow. Bring me THE cow. That little word 'the' told you WHICH one." → Place light blue ▵
  • Adjective: "Bring me a cow. Bring me the BROWN cow. The describing word helped you find the right one." → Place dark blue ▲
  • Verb: "The cow EATS. The cow RUNS. The cow SLEEPS. These ACTION words bring the sentence alive." → Place red ●

Implementation Notes

Printing Rules

  • ALL cards: card stock + laminated + cleanly cut. Non-laminated = destroyed in 1 week.
  • Color printing is mandatory — the color coding IS the system.
  • Each card set goes in its own tray or basket, labeled.
  • Print at actual size. Do not scale.
  • Corner punch for rounded edges (optional but professional).

Shelf Management

  • Left-to-right, top-to-bottom = easier to harder on each shelf.
  • Only put out works that have been PRESENTED to at least one child.
  • Day 1 on shelves: Metal Insets, Sandpaper Letters (Groups 1–2), Sound objects, Moveable Alphabet, a few Classified Card sets, Command Cards, Pink Object Boxes.
  • Add new works weekly as children are ready. Follow the child.
  • Each work is self-contained on a tray. Child takes entire tray to workspace.

What the Lead Teacher Will See

  • 7 shelves organized by the AMI developmental sequence
  • Clear progression: Oral → Sound → Writing Prep → Composition → Reading → Grammar
  • Complete Pink/Blue/Green reading program with all sub-materials
  • Grammar Farm with proper symbols and label cards
  • AMI sandpaper letter groupings (not random alphabet order)
  • Every work labeled, in a tray, self-contained
  • This master plan document as evidence of methodological understanding
