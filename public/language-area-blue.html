Blue Phase — Lesson-by-Lesson Content

UFLI lessons 54-83 · VCe, soft c/g, -tch, -dge, y as vowel, inflections, two-syllable words, r-controlled vowels, open syllables, -le syllables, w-influenced vowels, -all family.

The Blue Phase brief

Pink Phase taught every English letter and the digraphs and blends that combine them. Blue Phase teaches the next-most-common 30 patterns. By the end of Blue, a child can decode about 70% of all English one-syllable and two-syllable words on sight.

Posture stays the same. 8-step daily routine. Encoding before decoding. Sandpaper letters (for VCe slider work), movable alphabet, picture-word matching, decodable booklets, heart words. Mirror work for any sound a Mandarin-L1 child finds hard. Daily articulation drill on r-controlled vowels.

How long does Blue Phase take?

UFLI publishes a 30-lesson Blue sequence intended to run over about 30 school weeks (one new lesson per week, four daily 30-min sessions). For a Montessori classroom with mixed-age children and self-paced practice, plan on 4-6 months for a child who has solid Pink Phase mastery. Mandarin-L1 learners often need 5-7 months — especially on r-controlled vowels and on the -ed/-ing inflections, which carry the heaviest L1 transfer load.

The trick: don't wait for whole-class mastery. Some children will sprint through VCe in two weeks. Others will need two months on r-controlled vowels alone. Group flexibility, not lockstep.

Patterns to track per child

Blue Phase introduces explicit grammar (plurals, past tense, present continuous) for the first time. Track these per child:

  • VCe automatic — Can the child read cake/kite/rose/mule on sight, no decoding pause?
  • R-controlled produced — Can the child SAY car/for/her/bird/turn with the r-curl, not the Mandarin retroflex?
  • -ing in speech — Does the child spontaneously use "I am running" rather than "I run"?
  • Past tense -ed in speech — Does the child spontaneously use "yesterday I jumped" rather than "yesterday I jump"?
  • Plurals -s/-es — Does the child add the plural marker in speech as well as in spelling?

The first two are decoding markers. The last three are productive grammar markers — they reveal whether the lesson has crossed from reading into speaking. Crossing matters most for Mandarin L1 children; their Mandarin doesn't have any of these morphological signals, so without explicit teaching they simply won't produce them in English.

VCe — Magic-e (Lessons 54-58)

The single most-leveraging pattern in Blue Phase. Magic-e unlocks several thousand common English words at one stroke. Teach it as a SLIDER: build a CVC word with the movable alphabet, then slide in the silent e card — watch the vowel change.

Lesson 54 — a_e — Magic-e (long /ā/)

/ā/

The rule · A silent e at the end of a word reaches back and makes the vowel say its name. "Mad" + e = "made." The e is silent. Demonstrate with a slider card: "mad" → slide in the magic e → "made."

Soft consonants and trigraphs (Lessons 59-62)

Soft c and soft g teach the same idea: e, i, and y soften the consonant before them. The -tch and -dge trigraphs are spelling-after-short-vowel rules. Teach as a four-lesson family.

Lesson 59 — Soft c — /s/ before e, i, y

/s/

The rule · When c is followed by e, i, or y, it usually says /s/ — not /k/. "Cell, cinema, cycle." Otherwise c says /k/ — "cat, cup, cot."

Words

cell · cent · cement · face · race · pace · lace · place · space · trace · grace · ice · nice · mice · rice · slice · price · twice · fence · pence · since · prince · dance · chance · glance · prance · stance · spice

Phrase cards

a nice price · twice a day · a slice of ice · a face in space · a fence and a prince

Sentence cards

  • A mouse on ice.
  • A nice slice of cake.
  • I dance in a place.
  • A fence by a face.
  • A prince in a place.
Mandarin L1 note · Mandarin has /s/ and /ts/ (cì 次) — both close to English soft c. Easy sound. The lesson is the spelling rule: c before e/i/y = /s/. Children may overgeneralize and write "cake" as "kake"; that's correctable.

Lesson 60 — Soft g — /j/ before e, i, y

/j/

The rule · Same pattern as soft c. When g is followed by e, i, or y, it usually says /j/. "Gem, giant, gym." Otherwise g says /g/ — "go, gum, gap." There are exceptions (get, give, girl) but the rule holds about 80% of the time.

Words

gem · gel · gent · gist · gym · age · cage · page · rage · sage · stage · wage · huge · change · strange · range · engage · hinge · binge · cringe · singe

Phrase cards

a huge gem · a wide page · a gym stage · a change of stage

Sentence cards

  • A gem on a page.
  • A huge stage in a gym.
  • A strange change.
  • A wage of an age.
New heart words this lesson
large, change
Mandarin L1 note · Mandarin has nothing exactly like /j/ but the affricate /tɕ/ (jī 鸡) is similar enough. Acceptable. Spelling rule mirrors soft c.

Lesson 61 — -tch trigraph — /ch/ after short vowel

/ch/

The rule · After a SHORT vowel, the /ch/ sound is usually spelled -tch, not -ch. "Catch, fetch, ditch, notch." Exceptions: much, rich, such (one-syl, no tch).

Words

catch · match · patch · hatch · latch · batch · fetch · sketch · stretch · ditch · pitch · witch · switch · stitch · hitch · itch · notch · botch · clutch · hutch

Phrase cards

a quick catch · a witch with a hat · a pitch on a switch · fetch the ball

Sentence cards

  • I catch a ball.
  • A witch on a hill.
  • Fetch the dog.
  • A patch on a pant.
  • A pitch in a ditch.
Mandarin L1 note · No new sound. The spelling rule -tch after short vowel is the lesson. Connect to soft c (L59) and soft g (L60) as a family: English has spelling rules that signal whether a sound is hard or soft.

Lesson 62 — -dge trigraph — /j/ after short vowel

/j/

The rule · After a SHORT vowel, the /j/ sound is usually spelled -dge, not -ge. "Badge, edge, ridge, dodge, fudge." This rule pairs with -tch from L61 — both happen after short vowels.

Words

Mandarin L1 note · The /j/ sound is hard — see L60 ESL note. The spelling rule -dge after short vowel is parallel to -tch. Pattern recognition is the win here.

Y as vowel (Lessons 63-64)

Two lessons covering y-as-vowel in one- and multi-syllable contexts. The rule: number of syllables decides the sound.

Lesson 63 — Y as long /ī/ — end of one-syllable words

/ī/

The rule · Y at the end of a ONE-syllable word usually says long /ī/. "My, by, fly, try, why." Notice y here is the vowel — it took over the job from a normal vowel because every syllable needs one.

Words

by · my · cry · dry · fry · pry · sky · sly · spy · try · why · shy · ply · sty · wry · fly

Phrase cards

my sky · why try · a shy spy · dry fly · fry by sky

Sentence cards

  • Why cry?
  • My fly in the sky.
  • A shy spy.
  • I try to fly.
  • Dry by the sky.
New heart words this lesson
by, my, try
Mandarin L1 note · Long /ī/ is /aɪ/. See L55 ESL note — diphthong /ai/ exists in Mandarin so the sound is reachable. The new lesson is the y-as-vowel concept. Children sometimes call y a "sneaky vowel" — that mental model works.

Lesson 64 — Y as long /ē/ — end of multi-syllable words

/ē/

The rule · Y at the end of a MULTI-syllable word usually says long /ē/. "Happy, baby, candy, lucky, funny, sunny." The number of syllables decides the sound.

Words

happy · baby · candy · lucky · funny · sunny · penny · pretty · kitty · puppy · silly · fluffy · fuzzy · jolly · lazy · crazy · tiny · rainy · snowy · sandy · windy · easy · busy

Phrase cards

Sentence cards

  • A funny puppy on a sunny day.
  • A pretty penny.
  • A tiny kitty.
  • A rainy windy day.
New heart words this lesson
very, every
Mandarin L1 note · Long /ē/ is easy in Mandarin (yī 一). The rule "one-syl y = /ī/, multi-syl y = /ē/" is the takeaway. Daily contrast drill: my/many · cry/lucky · fly/silly.

Inflections — plurals, -ing, -ed (Lessons 65-67)

Three lessons that introduce English morphology. For Mandarin-L1 children these are conceptually new — Mandarin doesn't mark plural or tense on nouns/verbs. Spend extra time on production drills (oral practice, not just reading) so children START USING these endings in their own speech.

Lesson 65 — Plurals — adding -s and -es

/s/ or /z/ or /iz/

The rule · Most plurals add -s. After s, sh, ch, x, or z, add -es. "Cats, dogs, foxes, dishes, churches, buzzes."

Words

cats · dogs · pigs · cubs · rats · mats · caps · pans · pins · beds · hats · mugs · tubs · jobs · foxes · boxes · taxes · dishes · fishes · wishes · rushes · bushes · inches · kisses · misses · classes · masses · buzzes

Phrase cards

cats and dogs · six foxes · two dishes · a class of kids

Sentence cards

  • Two cats.
  • Six foxes in a box.
  • Three dishes on a desk.
  • A pen and four pins.
  • Six bushes in a yard.
New heart words this lesson
does, goes
Mandarin L1 note · Mandarin does NOT mark plural on nouns. The concept "add -s for more than one" is genuinely new and takes time. Don't shortcut it. The /s/ vs /z/ pronunciation distinction is below conscious awareness — children pick it up by imitation. The -es addition (boxes, dishes) needs explicit rule teaching.

Lesson 66 — -ing — no doubling yet

The rule · Add -ing to a verb to show action happening. For now, just add -ing — no doubling. "Catching, jumping, asking, helping." Doubling rule comes in L70.

Words

catching · fetching · hatching · matching · patching · asking · jumping · helping · thumping · pumping · singing · ringing · kissing · fishing · pushing · rushing · wishing · munching · lunching · punching

Phrase cards

catching a ball · jumping high · singing a song · kissing a cat

Sentence cards

  • I am jumping.
  • A dog catching a ball.
  • Singing a song.
  • Fishing in a lake.
  • Pushing a cart.
Mandarin L1 note · Major Mandarin L1 concept. Mandarin uses 在 (zài) or 正在 to mark continuous aspect — completely different from English -ing. Many learners say "I jump" when they mean "I am jumping." Daily oral drill: "What am I doing right now? I am _____ing." Mirror your own actions.

Lesson 67 — -ed (3 sounds: /t/, /d/, /əd/)

The rule · Add -ed to a verb to show past action. Three sound variants: • /t/ after voiceless sounds — jumped, asked, kissed • /d/ after voiced sounds — played, climbed, hugged • /əd/ after t or d — wanted, ended, landed Children do not need to memorise this; they pick it up. But you should know.

Words

jumped · asked · kissed · fished · wished · pushed · rushed · matched · pitched · thanked · played · rolled · pulled · filled · killed · rained · wanted · landed · ended · mended · lasted · rested · tested · twisted

Phrase cards

jumped and played · rested and ended · matched and rolled · kissed and hugged

Sentence cards

  • I jumped on a bed.
  • A dog played in the grass.
  • I wanted a snack.
  • A bus landed at a stop.
  • I asked for a cup.
New heart words this lesson
walked, played
Mandarin L1 note · The single hardest English grammar concept for Mandarin L1. Mandarin has NO past-tense morphology — past is signaled by time words (昨天, 上周) or particles (了). Adding -ed to mark past requires constant scaffolding. Daily oral drill: "Yesterday I _____ed." Pair with a visual time line poster (yesterday | today | tomorrow).

Compounds, two-syllable words, and doubling (Lessons 68-70)

Three lessons on multi-syllable decoding. Compounds first (two known words glued), then closed-syllable two-syl, then the doubling rule for suffixes.

Lesson 68 — Closed-syllable compound words

The rule · A compound word is two short words joined into one. Each piece keeps its short-vowel sound. "Catnap" = cat + nap. "Sunset" = sun + set.

Words

Phrase cards

Sentence cards

  • A sunset on a hilltop.
  • A catnap on a laptop.
  • A pigpen with a sandbag.
  • A cobweb on a desk.
  • A quick pickup.
Mandarin L1 note · Compounds are easy conceptually — Mandarin builds words the same way (火车 = fire + car = train). Children grasp this fast. The pleasure of "I can read TWO words at once" builds confidence.

Lesson 69 — Closed-syllable two-syllable words (split between two consonants)

The rule · Two-syllable words with a short vowel in each syllable. Where there are TWO consonants between the vowels, split between them: "rab-bit, sud-den, hap-pen, kit-ten."

Words

rabbit · sudden · happen · kitten · mitten · button · cotton · pumpkin · napkin · picnic · public · mascot · cactus · subject · submit · admit · invent · until · unless · unwell

Phrase cards

a small rabbit · a sudden happen · a red mitten · a soft kitten · a public picnic

Sentence cards

  • A rabbit in a pumpkin.
  • A kitten with a mitten.
  • A public picnic on a hill.
  • A sudden gust of wind.
  • A button on a shirt.
Mandarin L1 note · Mandarin is mono-syllabic at the character level but polysyllabic at the word level (rabbit = 兔子 tù-zi, two syllables). The CONCEPT is fine. The CHALLENGE is the syllable break — children often try to read it as one giant word. Teach the split: find the two consonants in the middle, draw a line between them.

Lesson 70 — Doubling rule with -ing and -ed

The rule · When you add -ing or -ed to a short verb that ends in ONE vowel + ONE consonant, DOUBLE the consonant. "Run + ing = running." "Hop + ed = hopped." The doubling protects the short vowel.

Words

running · hopping · stopping · dropping · shopping · chatting · clapping · humming · skipping · tapping · tipping · sitting · getting · cutting · hopped · stopped · dropped · shopped · chatted · clapped · tapped · tipped · patted · rubbed · hugged · grabbed · planned · scanned

Phrase cards

running and jumping · shopping and chatting · hopping and skipping · tapped and stopped

Sentence cards

  • I am running.
  • A bus stopping at a stop.
  • A frog hopping on a log.
  • I shopped for a snack.
  • A kid clapped and smiled.
Mandarin L1 note · The doubling rule is reliable — taught once, applied consistently. The Mandarin learner will struggle most with KNOWING when to apply it; the trick is the "one vowel + one consonant" check. Make it a checklist.

R-controlled vowels (Lessons 71-75)

Five lessons covering ar, or, er, ir, ur. Three of them (er, ir, ur) share one sound. Mandarin-L1 critical — the r-curl is a brand-new mouth shape. Mirror work every single day.

Lesson 71 — ar — /ar/

/ar/

The rule · When a is followed by r, the r changes the vowel sound. Together they say /ar/ — "car, star, farm." The r is "bossy" — it controls the vowel.

Words

The MOUTH POSITION is the lesson: lips slightly rounded, tongue tip curled back (not touching anywhere). Daily mirror work. Compare ar/are/ate to show the r-influence.

Lesson 75 — ur — /er/

/er/

The rule · U + r = /er/. SAME SOUND again. "Turn, fur, burn." Three spellings (er, ir, ur), one sound. This lesson closes the r-controlled vowels.

Open syllables, special patterns, and Blue review (Lessons 76-83)

Lessons 76-77 introduce open syllables. L78 is the -ind/-ild/-old family. L79 is consonant-le. L80 is review. L81-82 are the w-influenced and -all families. L83 closes Blue Phase.

Lesson 76 — Open syllables — one-syllable

The rule · A syllable that ENDS in a vowel says the long sound of that vowel. "He, she, hi, no, go, so, be, me, we." Compare to closed syllables (cat, bed) where the syllable ends in a consonant and the vowel is short.

Words

he · she · me · we · be · hi · no · so · go · fly · cry · try · why · sky · shy

Phrase cards

he and she · go and try · we be free · no sky

Sentence cards

  • He and she go to the park.
  • We try.
  • Be brave.
  • So far.
  • No sky in the dark.
New heart words this lesson
he, she
Mandarin L1 note · The open-syllable concept is a powerful unlock — it explains why "go" doesn't need a magic e. Children sometimes write "goe" for "go"; teach them: "the syllable ends in the vowel, so it's long. No e needed."

Lesson 77 — Open-closed two-syllable words

The rule · A two-syllable word where the first syllable is OPEN (vowel says long) and the second is closed. "Mu-sic, pi-lot, ro-bot, ti-ger, mo-ment." Split between the vowel and the next consonant.

Words

  • A spider in a basic web.
  • A music moment.
  • A baby with a lady.
New heart words this lesson
open
Mandarin L1 note · Open-closed combos are the trickiest decoding pattern in Blue Phase. Children naturally try short vowel first (mus-ic) — wrong. Teach the rule: try long vowel first when one consonant follows. If that doesn't make a word, try short. It takes practice.

Lesson 78 — -ind, -ild, -old, -olt, -ost — long vowel before two consonants

Lesson 79 — Consonant-le (-le) syllables

/əl/

The rule · A two-letter syllable ending in -le. The consonant before the -le is part of the -le syllable: "ta-ble, lit-tle, pur-ple, mid-dle, gig-gle." Pronounce as /əl/ — a soft "ul" sound.

Mandarin L1 note · The /əl/ sound is the schwa + /l/ — soft, unstressed. Mandarin doesn't have schwa. Children may over-pronounce the e ("ay-pul" for apple). Daily oral drill — soft, soft, soft.

Mandarin-L1 focus areas across Blue Phase

Blue Phase introduces three categories of pattern that hit Mandarin-L1 learners particularly hard. Mark these in your daily lesson notes so you can spot the children who need more drill.

  • R-controlled vowels (L71-75). The English /r/-curl is alien to a Mandarin speaker. Daily mirror work. Pair the lesson with the ESL minimal-pair drill on r-vowels: car/care · for/four · her/hair · bird/beard · turn/tern.
  • Inflectional endings (L65-67, L70). Plurals, -ing, -ed. None of these exist in Mandarin grammar. Children may decode them in print but FAIL TO PRODUCE them in speech. Daily oral drill on "yesterday I ___ed" and "right now I am ___ing."
  • VCe spelling (L54-58). The "silent e at the end" is alien to a syllabic writing system. Spelling errors (cak for cake, kit for kite) persist for months. Be patient; reading practice fixes this faster than spelling correction.

Picture sourcing for Blue Phase

Continue the same Canva and Google sourcing approach from Pink Phase. New categories to source for Blue:

  • VCe words. Pictures of: cake, snake, plane, bone, rose, phone, cube, mule, tube. Stick to the same illustration style as Pink.
  • R-controlled vowels. Pictures of: car, star, farm, corn, horse, fork, bird, girl, shirt, turn, nurse, hurt.
  • Inflections. Don't source pictures for jumping/jumped — the child knows what jumping looks like. Instead, use ACTION PICTURE SEQUENCES: child standing → child jumping → child landed. This visualises tense.
  • 2-syl words. Pictures of: rabbit, kitten, pumpkin, picnic, sunset, hilltop.
  • Consonant-le words. Pictures of: little, apple, turtle, table, bottle.
