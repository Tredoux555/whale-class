Pink Readers

Fifteen decodable storybooks for the Pink Phase — every word a child can sound out.

The Pink Phase lessons give a child words, phrases and isolated sentence cards. They do not give stories. A child becomes a reader by reading connected, meaningful text they care about — and these fifteen little books are the missing rung between "a sentence card" and "a real book in my hands".

A decodable reader is a real story in which the child never meets a word they cannot sound out. Every word is a letter-sound already taught, or a heart word already introduced. Each book "unlocks" after a particular lesson — its gate — and is handed to the child as a milestone, a reward for finishing that run of lessons.

This page holds all fifteen reader manuscripts — the page text, an art brief for every page, and a teacher note — followed by the Teacher Guide and the Canva Production Guide. The manuscripts are the inputs for producing the finished, printed books.

Every reader's header carries a word-by-word decodability check. The iron rule of the series: a child never meets an undecodable word. If a line is ever edited in production, the manuscript text — not the edit — is the law.

Pink Reader · Book 1 — "Cat Can Nap"

Gate: read after Lesson 17 (single consonants + short a/i/o + ck). Pages: cover + 9 story pages. One sentence per page (the last page has two short ones).

Word inventory (every word in the book): Sam · and · a · cat · the · can · nap · on · mat · in · pot · map · cap — 13 words total.

Heart words used: a, the (both introduced by Lesson 11). ✓ Decodability check: every other word is pure CVC/CV from letters s, a, t, p, i, n, m, d, c, k, o — all taught by Lesson 13–17. Sam is a decodable proper name (s-a-m). Nothing in this book outruns the child's phonics. ✓

The shape: a pattern book. The frame "A cat can nap on/in a ___" repeats so the child can predict it and feel fluent — then page 7 breaks the pattern with a question, page 8 pays it off, page 9 buttons it warm. The words stay flat and simple on purpose; Sam's face does the storytelling — fond → amused → laughing → delighted → sleepy. The art briefs carry that.

Teacher note — how to use Book 1

When: hand it to the child after Lesson 17, as a milestone — "you've learned your sounds, here is your first real book." It is a reward, not a worksheet.

The pattern: pages 3–6 all run "A cat can nap [on/in] a ___." Let the child notice the pattern — by page 5 most children predict the sentence and read it almost fluently. That feeling of fluency is the goal of Book 1. Don't rush past it.

The three reads (one sitting or three short ones):

  1. Read 1 — decode. Child sounds out each word, finger under the text. Slow is fine. You read nothing for them — they own every word, because every word is decodable.
  2. Read 2 — smooth. Child reads it again, a little faster, "like talking." Fluency and phrasing.
  3. Read 3 — for the story. Child reads it for fun. Now talk about the pictures: Where is the cat napping now? How does Sam feel on this page?

The page-turn: at page 7 ("...on Sam?") pause. Ask "Do you think the cat will?" Let them turn the page to find out.

Comprehension question: "Where did the cat like to nap best?" (Answer: on Sam.) Then: "How do we know Sam was happy about it?" — the child reads Sam's face, not the words. That is the comprehension skill.

ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch: the final /t/ in cat, mat, pot and the final /p/ in nap, map, cap — Mandarin-L1 children drop word-final stops. Use the tissue test from the lesson content. The repeated pattern is a gift here: six clean reps of final /t/ and final /p/ in one short book.

Teacher note — how to use Book 2

When: hand it to the child after Lesson 21, once all five short vowels and r and h are secure. It is the second milestone book — "now you know every short vowel, here is a bigger story."

The pattern: pages 2–6 alternate "The pup can ___" with the cheer ", pup, !" Let the child hear the rhythm — by page 5 most children predict the cheer and read it almost fluently. That feeling of fluency is the goal. Don't rush past the pattern pages.

The three reads (one sitting or three short ones):

  1. Read 1 — decode. Child sounds out each word, finger under the text. Slow is fine. Every word is decodable — the child owns all of them.
  2. Read 2 — smooth. Child reads it again, a little faster, "like talking." The cheers ("Run, pup, run!") are a gift here — read them with bounce.
  3. Read 3 — for the story. Child reads it for fun. Now talk about the pictures: How does the pup feel here? Is the cat being unkind, or just sleepy?

The page-turn: at page 7 ("Up, cat! Up!") pause. Ask "Do you think the cat will get up?" Let the child turn the page to find out.

Comprehension question: "Where did the pup nap at the end?" (Answer: in the sun, next to the cat.) Then: "How do we know the pup was not sad when the cat did not play?" — the child reads the pup's face and tilted ear on page 8, not the words. That is the comprehension skill.

ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch: the final /t/ in cat, hot, not, get and the final /p/ in pup, hop, up — Mandarin-L1 children drop word-final stops. Use the tissue test from the lesson content. run and r generally — keep the daily R/L minimal-pair drill going; this book gives many warm reps of initial /r/ in a happy context.

Teacher note — how to use Book 3

When: hand it to the child after Lesson 24, once b, f, l are secure and the heart words said and where are on the binder ring. This is the first reader with dialogue — point that out: "see the marks? someone is talking."

The pattern: pages 7–8 run the search frame "Not in/on a ___." Let the child feel the rhythm and predict it. Pages 4–5 give the doubled-cheer pattern ("Hop, hop, hop!") — bouncy reps of final /p/ and final /k/.

The three reads:

  1. Read 1 — decode. Child sounds out each word, finger under the text. Slow is fine. Every word is decodable.
  2. Read 2 — smooth. Child reads it again, "like talking." Read Mom's lines warmly — she is never cross.
  3. Read 3 — for the story. Child reads it for fun. Talk about the pictures: Is Mom angry with the hen? How can you tell? (She is not — her face is fond and patient on every page.)

The page-turn: at page 6 ("Where can the hen sit?") pause. Ask "Where do YOU think the hen will sit?" Take a guess or two, then turn the page.

Comprehension question: "Where did the hen sit in the end?" (Answer: on Mom's big red hat.) Then: "Did Mom mind? How do we know?" — the child reads Mom's delighted face on pages 10–11. That is the comprehension skill: the words never say Mom is happy; the picture does.

ESL / Mandarin-L1 watch: the final /n/ in hen, pen, in, can is easy for Mandarin-L1 children (Mandarin has final /n/) — use these as confidence words. Watch the final /t/ in hat, sat, not, sit and final /k/ in peck — the tissue test. The /r/ in red, ran — keep the daily R/L drill.

There is no rush.

Pink Readers — Teacher Guide

A graded series of fifteen decodable readers for the Pink Phase (UFLI lessons 5–53). This guide explains what these books are, why they are built the way they are, and exactly how to use them with a child.

What a decodable reader is

A decodable reader is a real story in which the child never meets a word they cannot sound out. Every word is either a letter-sound the child has already been taught, or a heart word already introduced.

That is the whole discipline — and it is what makes these books different from the "levelled" or "predictable" readers a child might meet elsewhere.

A predictable reader teaches a child to guess. The text repeats a frame ("I see a dog. I see a cat. I see a…") and the pictures give the answer, so the child reads by memorising the pattern and looking at the picture — not by decoding. It feels like reading. It is not. When the patterns run out, the child has no strategy left.

A decodable reader teaches a child to decode. Because every word is within reach, the child sounds words out, builds them, and earns them. The picture supports the story and the feeling — it never rescues the word. When the child closes a Pink Reader, every word in it was genuinely read.

This is why the series slots straight into the Montessori language sequence: it is the missing rung between the lesson content's isolated sentence cards and "a real book in my hands."

The iron rule, in one line

For every book, every word is a letter-sound or pattern taught up to that book's gate lesson, or a heart word already introduced by that gate (plus and, decodable a-n-d from Lesson 13). Nothing in any book outruns the child's phonics. That is a promise to the child — and it is checked, word by word, in the header of every reader.

A book is a milestone, not a worksheet

Each reader "unlocks" after a particular lesson — its gate. The book is a reward for finishing that run of lessons, not a daily exercise.

Hand a child Book 1 after Lesson 17 with a real sense of occasion: "You have learned your sounds — here is your first proper book." A child who has worked through seventeen lessons of sandpaper letters and movable alphabet has earned a story. Treat it that way. A book handed over as a milestone is read with pride; the same book handed over as a worksheet is read with a sigh.

Do not race a child through the series. One book marks one milestone. Between books, the child keeps doing lessons. Re-reading a favourite Pink Reader is always welcome — but the next book waits for the next gate.

The three reads

A decodable reader is read three times — in one sitting or across three short ones. Each read does a different job.

Read 1 — decode. The child sounds out each word, finger moving under the text. Slow is completely fine. You read nothing for them. Every word is decodable, so every word is the child's to earn. Resist the urge to supply a word — wait, and let them build it. This is the read that proves the book.

Read 2 — smooth. The child reads it again, a little faster, "like talking." Now the work shifts from decoding to fluency and phrasing — reading words in groups, with the rhythm of speech. The repeated frames and cheers ("Run, pup, run!", "Dig, dig, dig!") are gifts here: read them with bounce.

Read 3 — for the story. The child reads it for fun. The words are no longer an obstacle, so attention is free for meaning. This is the read where you talk about the pictures and the story.

A child may want a fourth read — aloud, to someone: a parent, a teacher, a younger friend. From around Book 5 onward, reading TO another person is the natural next step, and the warm full-cast books are built for it.

Page-turn hooks — keep the child turning

Most pages end on a small hook: a question, a trailing sentence, an implied "what next." The child should want to turn the page.

Use the hook. When you reach a hook page — Book 1 page 7 ("A cat can nap on Sam?"), Book 4 page 7 ("the box did tip!"), Book 8 page 9 ("Will the pup get the bell?") — stop. Ask the child what they think happens next. Take a guess or two. Then turn the page. That pause is where a child learns that reading is something you do to find out — the root of all reading for meaning.

Each book's teacher note names its hook page and the question to ask.

The comprehension questions

After Read 3, talk about the story. Every book's teacher note gives two questions. They come in a deliberate pair.

The first question is about the events. "Where did the cat like to nap best?" "Who was hiding in the box?" "Why was the pup hot at the start, and not hot at the end?" The answer is in the words and the pictures together — a check that the child followed the story.

The second question is about a feeling — and the answer is in the picture, not the words. "How do we know Sam was happy about it?" "How did the fox feel when the cat came out?" "Was Mom cross? How can you tell?"

This second question is the heart of comprehension teaching in this series. The decodable vocabulary is too small to say "Sam was overjoyed" or "the fox was startled." So the art carries the emotion — and the child learns to read the picture: the wide grin, the flat ears, the fond eyes. Reading a face is a real comprehension skill, and these books teach it on every page. Always ask the feeling question. Always let the child point to the picture for the answer.

The Mandarin-L1 watch points

The Pink Readers are written for a classroom of Mandarin-L1 ESL children — concrete, universal situations, short sentences, warm and simple feelings. A few sound patterns need steady attention. Each book's teacher note flags the ones relevant to that book; here is the overview.

Final stops — the most common slip. Mandarin syllables do not end in /t/, /p/, /k/, /d/, /g/, /b/. A Mandarin-L1 child will drop the final consonant — "cat" becomes "ca", "nap" becomes "na", "dig" becomes "di". The Pink Readers are full of final stops on purpose: cat, mat, pot, hat, nap, hop, cap, pig, dig. Use the tissue test from the lesson content: hold a small piece of tissue in front of the mouth — on a real final /t/ or /p/ the tissue flutters. If it does not flutter, the stop was dropped. The repeated patterns give many clean reps of the same final sound in one book — that is by design.

The hard consonants. Keep the daily minimal-pair drills from the lesson content running alongside the readers: - /r/ (Books 2, 3, 13) — the hardest single sound. R/L drill: red/led, rip/lip, run/… Months of patience. - /th/ (Book 10) — no th in Mandarin; "moth" drifts to "mos" or "mot". Tongue tip between the teeth, visible. Mirror work every th page. - /v/, /z/, /j/ — substituted with /w/, /s/, /z/-ish. Mirror work.

Consonant clusters — the Phase 3 wall. Mandarin has no consonant clusters at all. A Mandarin-L1 child will either break the cluster with an extra vowel ("se-led" for "sled") or drop a consonant ("fas" for "fast"). Books 11–14 lean hard on blends for exactly this practice. The fix: keep the mouth moving from one consonant straight into the next — no reset, no vowel between. Slow first, then quick. Triple blends (Book 14) often take Mandarin-L1 children three to four months to master. That is completely normal. There is no rush.

The vowels. Short /ă/ and short /ĕ/ are the hardest English vowels for Mandarin-L1 children. Book 7 (Cat? Cot? Cup?) is built as a vowel- discrimination game — use it, and pair it with the Lesson 40 minimal-pair drill.

A few practical notes

  • Let the child hold the book. A decodable reader is a physical object the child owns the experience of. Finger under the text, their own pace.
  • Do not pre-teach the words. The point is that the child can decode them cold. If a word genuinely stumps them on Read 1, sound the first letter with them and wait — do not just say the word.
  • Re-reading is reading. A child who wants Book 6 again for the fifth time is building fluency. Let them.
  • Recognise the cast. Sam and the cat anchor every book; the pup, Mom, Dad, the hen, the fox and the frog join as the phonics unlocks their names. From Book 4 onward, ask: "Have you met this friend before?" The crossover is part of the joy.
  • A child who is not ready is not behind. If a book feels hard, the child is simply not at its gate yet. Go back to the lessons, re-read an easier Pink Reader, and return. Finishing well matters more than finishing fast.

What comes after the Pink Readers

A child who reads Book 15 (The Big Pink Trip) confidently — including the blends in splash, trip, frog — has finished the Pink Phase and is ready for the Blue Phase: the silent-e / VCe pattern, longer words, and the first vowel teams. The decodable-reader discipline continues there — the child simply has new tools.

A child who is not quite there yet should re-read favourite Pink Readers and run more Phase 3 review. Come back to Book 15 when it feels easy. Every word in these fifteen books was earned — that is the foundation everything else is built on.

Pink Readers — Canva Production Guide

How to turn the fifteen reader manuscripts in this folder into finished, printed little books using Canva. Written for a teacher, not a designer — every step is concrete.

Each reader's .md file already gives you everything you need: the page text exactly as it should be printed, an art brief for every page, and a composition note saying where the text sits. This guide explains how to build those into pages and assemble a book.

The golden rule: one locked style for all fifteen

The fifteen books must look like one shelf of books — the same world, the same friends, the same hand. A child bonds with Sam and the cat across the whole series; that only works if Sam and the cat look identical in every book. So:

  • Decide the look once, on Book 1, and never drift. Sam, the cat, the palette, the font, the layout — all locked on Book 1 and copied forward.
  • Make a Canva template, not fifteen separate designs. Build the Book 1 layout, then "Duplicate" it for every other page and every other book. The template is the consistency.

Illustration style

  • Flat, warm, modern children's-book illustration. Soft rounded shapes, clean outlines or no outlines, no clutter, no gradients-gone-wild.
  • One style for all fifteen books. If you use Canva's flat-illustration elements, stay in that family. If you use Canva Magic Media (AI images), use the same prompt style words every time — see below.
  • Backgrounds stay simple. The scene supports the sentence; it never competes with it. A window, a plant, a rug — one or two shapes, lots of calm space. Empty space is good. It is where the child's eye rests.

The characters — design once, copy forever

Design these on their first appearance and save each as a Canva element you re-paste into every later page:

  • Sam (every book) — a young child, about five, gender-neutral, round friendly face, simple warm clothing. Designed on Book 1 page 1.
  • The cat (every book) — a plump, friendly tabby, expressive, often napping. Designed on Book 1 page 1.
  • The pup (from Book 2) — small, round, floppy-eared, eager.
  • Mom / Dad (from Books 2–3 / Book 5) — warm, soft, gentle faces.
  • The hen (from Book 3) — plump, expressive, slightly comic.
  • The fox (from Book 4) — slim, orange, bright-eyed, a touch of mischief.
  • The frog (from Book 13) — rounded, friendly, soft green.

Each book's cover art brief says "design [character] here — every later book draws it exactly the same." Honour that. The single most important consistency check across the series is: does Sam look like the same child in Book 1 and Book 15?

If you use Canva Magic Media (AI images)

AI images drift in style. To keep fifteen books coherent:

  • Build a fixed style suffix and append it to every image prompt: "…flat warm children's-book illustration, soft rounded shapes, soft cream background, sage green and terracotta and gold, gentle, uncluttered, no text."
  • Generate the characters as separate elements on transparent / plain backgrounds, then place the same character image into many scenes — do not re-generate Sam for every page, or Sam will look different every page.
  • Always generate "no text" — you add the text yourself, in the font below.

Typography — non-negotiable for early readers

The child is matching letters to the sounds they learned. The print must match the letters they were taught.

  • Font: Andika. It is free, and it was designed for beginning readers — it has a single-storey a and a single-storey g, exactly the letterforms on the sandpaper letters. Add it in Canva via Brand Kit → Add a font, or search "Andika" in the font list. If Andika is ever unavailable, the flashcards' fallback (Comic Sans MS) is acceptable — but never a decorative or serif font. The child must recognise every letter.
  • Lowercase print. Type the text in lowercase, exactly as the manuscript prints it — only the first word of a sentence and names (Sam, Mom) are capitalised. Children learn lowercase first.
  • Large. Body text around 28–36 pt. One short line per line of text where possible.
  • Generously spaced. Wide line spacing (1.5+) and slightly widened letter spacing. Crowded text is hard for a decoding eye.
  • High contrast. Near-black text (#2E2A22) on the clean cream text band.
  • Never over a busy image. Text always sits on the clean band the composition note describes — almost always the bottom third of the page. If the illustration creeps into the band, fade or clear that area behind the text. The words must never fight the picture.

Page size and layout

  • Page size: a square page works beautifully for a little book — set the Canva design to square, e.g. 20 × 20 cm (or US letter folded — see assembly). Square is forgiving and child-friendly.
  • One manuscript page = one Canva page. The cover is its own page.
  • The text band: reserve the bottom third of every page for text. Place a soft rounded rectangle in the band colour (#FFFDF7) across that third, and set the text on it. Keep the band in the same place on every page of a book — the composition notes are written to this rule.
  • The art fills the upper two-thirds, following the page's art brief. Keep the main character and the "key object" of the page centred and clear.
  • Margins: keep all art and text at least 1.5 cm from the page edge — printing and trimming eat the edge.

Building one page, step by step

  1. Duplicate your locked template page.
  2. Read the manuscript page's art brief. Build the scene: background shapes first, then the key object, then the characters (paste your saved character elements — do not redraw them), then expressions. The art brief tells you every character's expression — get those right; they carry the story.
  3. Read the composition note. Place the characters and object where it says; place the text band where it says.
  4. Type the page text, exactly as printed in the manuscript — same words, same punctuation, lowercase. Andika, ~32 pt, near-black, on the band.
  5. Check: is the text clear of the busy art? Is every character on-model? Is there calm space?

Assembling, exporting and printing a little book

  1. Order the pages in Canva: cover, then page 1, 2, 3 … to the button page.
  2. Export as PDF Print (Canva → Share → Download → PDF Print). Tick "crop marks and bleed" off for a simple home/school print; on for a print shop.
  3. Print on the heaviest paper your printer takes (160 gsm if possible — little hands are hard on books). Print single-sided for the simplest assembly: each manuscript page becomes one printed sheet.
  4. Make the book: - Simplest (stack and staple): stack the printed sheets in order, fold the stack once down the middle if you sized pages to print two-up, and staple twice along the fold (a long-arm stapler helps). Trim the outer edges straight. - Even simpler (no fold): keep single sheets in order, stack them, and staple twice down the left edge. A perfectly good little book.
  5. Laminate the cover if the book will live in a busy classroom — or laminate every page for the most-loved early titles.
  6. Write the book number small on the spine or back so the set stays in order on the shelf.

Print the set as a matched shelf

Print all fifteen the same size, the same paper, the same way. Fifteen matched little books on a shelf — numbered 1 to 15 — look like an achievement, and a child works through them like one.

A consistency checklist before you print

Run this over every finished book:

  • [ ] Sam looks like the same child as in Book 1.
  • [ ] The cat (and every returning character) is on-model.
  • [ ] The palette is the locked palette — no stray bright colours.
  • [ ] The font is Andika (or the agreed fallback), lowercase, large, well spaced.
  • [ ] The text band is in the same place on every page of the book.
  • [ ] No text sits over a busy part of the picture.
  • [ ] The page text matches the manuscript exactly — this is a decodable reader; a single swapped word can break the decodability promise.
