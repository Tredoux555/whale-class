A short circle-time song for every lesson of the Pink Phase — UFLI lessons 5 to 53, forty-nine songs. Each one is built around that lesson's target sound, and each one sings a handful of that lesson's words.

What these are, and why

A sound lands in the ear and the body before it ever lands on paper. Sing the song at the very start of the lesson: the children hear the target sound, say it, and move it — then they go to the sandpaper letter and the word work already primed. Two or three minutes at the circle. Do the movement every time. Sing the same song all week.

These are heard, not decoded. The children sing a song; they do not read it. So — unlike the Pink Readers — the connecting lyrics use simple, natural, ESL-friendly English. What is drilled is the lesson's target sound and a handful of its words. Every song's header lists the lesson words it sings.

They are Suno-ready. Every song shares one base music style (below); each song then adds a short accent line. Paste base + accent into Suno to generate the track. Keep the whole set in one musical family so it feels like one songbook.

The base Suno style prompt — use for every song

Bright, warm children's singalong for a kindergarten circle. Gentle acoustic ukulele or soft nylon-string guitar, light hand percussion — claps, shaker, a woodblock. One clear, friendly, unhurried voice. Nursery-rhyme simple: a short, easy melody a four-year-old can sing back after one listen. Very repetitive. Slow enough that every word is crisp. No competing backing vocals, no busy production.

Then add each song's own Suno accent line.

ESL / Mandarin-L1 note

Every song's movement is tied to how the sound is made — the snake's hiss for /s/, the tissue-flutter pop for /p/, the hand on the throat for the voiced sounds. For Mandarin-L1 children this matters most on the hard sounds: the final stops, /r/, /th/, /v/, /z/. The movement is not decoration — it is the articulation lesson, set to music.

Phase 1 — The Alphabet (Lessons 5–34)

The single letters, one sound at a time, then three review songs to close the phase.

Lesson 5 · s — /s/ — "The Snake Says Ssss"

Sound: /s/  ·  Keyword: the snake  ·  voice off, long Words sung: the song features the /s/ keyword words — snake · sun · sock

Suno accent — a touch slower and softer; hold every ssss long and breathy, like a hush.

Lyrics

Ssss, ssss, ssss,
that is what the snake says.
Ssss, ssss, ssss —
soft and long, like this.

A snake in the sun, ssss.
A snake on a sock, ssss.
What does the snake say?

Ssss, ssss, ssss,
can you say it too?
Ssss, ssss, ssss —
s, s, s!

Movement — one hand becomes a snake, wiggling slowly through the air the whole time the /s/ is held. Long, smooth, hissing. The /s/ never gets a vowel after it — no "suh," just ssss.

Lesson 6 · a — /ă/ — "A Is for Apple"

Sound: short /ă/  ·  Keyword: apple  ·  mouth wide, short Words sung: apple · ant

Suno accent — bright and bouncy; the /ă/ is short and snappy, never held.

Lyrics

A, a, a — open up wide!
A, a, a — apple inside.

A is for apple, red and round.
A is for ant on the ground.
A, a, a — what a happy sound!

A, a, a — open up wide!
A, a, a — say it with pride.
A, a, a, a!

Movement — both hands open wide beside an open mouth on each /ă/ — jaw dropped, mouth as wide as it goes. Short and quick: a little snap of /ă/, not a long Mandarin "ah."

Lesson 7 · t — /t/ — "Tick-Tock, T!"

Sound: /t/  ·  Keyword: the ticking clock  ·  voice off, sharp, short Words sung: top · tap · at · sat

Suno accent — crisp march tempo; every /t/ clipped and clean, like a tiny drum tap.

Lyrics

T, t, t — tick-tock, t!
A clock goes t, and so do we.

T for the top that spins around.
T for the tap, tap, tapping sound.
Sat on a mat — hear the t at the end!

T, t, t — tick-tock, t!
Tap your hands and say it with me:
t, t, t!

Movement — one finger taps the air sharply on each /t/. On sat, mat, tap once more at the very end of the word — the final /t/ is the part Mandarin-L1 children drop. Hold a tissue up: a real /t/ makes it flutter.

Lesson 8 · p — /p/ — "Pop, Pop, P!"

Sound: /p/  ·  Keyword: the popping bubble  ·  voice off, a puff Words sung: pat · tap · pup · sap

Suno accent — playful and poppy; a tiny "pop" feel on every /p/.

Lyrics

P, p, p — lips go pop!
Press them shut, then let them pop.

P for the pat — pat, pat, pat.
P for the pup on the mat.
A little tap, a sticky sap — p!

P, p, p — lips go pop!
Pop your hands and never stop:
p, p, p!

Movement — press both lips tight shut, then "pop" both hands open on each /p/. A real puff of air — the tissue flutters. Catch the final /p/ in tap, pup.

Lesson 9 · i — /ĭ/ — "I, I, Itsy I"

Sound: short /ĭ/  ·  Keyword: the itsy insect  ·  small smile, short Words sung: sit · tip · sip

Suno accent — light and tickly; the /ĭ/ quick and small, a relaxed little smile — never the long "ee."

Lyrics

I, i, i — a little i,
small as an insect going by.

I when we sit, sit, sit on the mat.
I for the tip of a tiny hat.
Take a sip — i, i, i!

I, i, i — a little i,
say it small and say it spry:
i, i, i!

Movement — pinch finger and thumb almost shut to show how small the sound is, a relaxed little smile. Quick — /ĭ/, not a stretched "ee." Daily this is the sit / seat contrast for Mandarin-L1 ears.

Lesson 10 · n — /n/ — "N for the Nose"

Sound: /n/  ·  Keyword: the humming nose  ·  voiced, long, through the nose Words sung: nap · pin · pan · ant

Suno accent — gentle and humming; let the /n/ ring out long and warm.

Lyrics

N, n, n — touch your nose!
That is where the n-sound goes.

N for a nap — nnn, off to sleep.
N for the pin and the pan you keep.
An ant goes by — n, n, n!

N, n, n — touch your nose,
humming soft from your head to your toes:
n, n, n!

Movement — one finger on the nose, humming a long nnn — pinch the nose and the sound stops, which proves it goes through the nose. Tongue tip up on the ridge.

Lesson 11 · m — /m/ — "Mmm, That's Good!"

Sound: /m/  ·  Keyword: yummy food  ·  voiced, long, lips closed Words sung: map · mat · man · mom

Suno accent — warm and cosy; the /m/ long and contented, like tasting something delicious.

Lyrics

M, m, m — lips together,
mmm, like yummy food forever.

M for the map and the mat on the floor.
M for the man at the door.
M for mom — we love her more!

M, m, m — lips together,
hum it soft and hum it whether
rain or sun: m, m, m!

Movement — lips gently closed, one hand rubbing the tummy, humming a long happy mmm — the sound of enjoying food. Easy and warm; no transfer trouble here, a confidence song.

Lesson 12 · d — /d/ — "D for the Dog"

Sound: /d/  ·  Keyword: the dog  ·  voiced — throat buzz, short Words sung: dog · dad · dip

Suno accent — friendly and warm; each /d/ a soft little drum-thud with a buzz, not a sharp /t/.

Lyrics

D, d, d — hand on your throat,
feel it buzz on every note.

D for the dog — woof! — wagging its tail.
D for dad and the dip on the trail.
D, d, d — feel it buzz!

D, d, d — say it with me,
buzzing soft so the throat goes free:
d, d, d!

Movement — one hand flat on the throat, feeling the buzz on each /d/. Tongue taps the ridge — same place as /t/, but the voice is ON. For Mandarin-L1 children, the buzz is the whole lesson: no buzz, and /d/ slips into /t/.

Lesson 13 · g — /g/ — "G for the Goat"

Sound: /g/  ·  Keyword: the goat  ·  voiced — throat buzz Words sung: goat · pig · dig · tag

Suno accent — bouncy and cheerful; a buzzy little /g/ on every line.

Lyrics

G, g, g — hand on your throat,
feel the buzz of the gobbling goat.

G for the goat going up the hill.
G for the pig that will not be still.
Dig, dig, dig — tag — g, g, g!

G, g, g — hand on your throat,
buzz it deep like a goat in a coat:
g, g, g!

Movement — hand on the throat for the buzz; the other hand makes little goat horns that "butt" forward on each /g/. Back of the tongue lifts. Voice ON — without the buzz, /g/ becomes /k/.

Sound: short /ŏ/  ·  Keyword: the octopus  ·  mouth open, round, short Words sung: pot · top · dog · mop · on

Suno accent — wobbly and fun; the /ŏ/ open and round but short, like a quick surprise.

Lyrics

Movement — both arms make a big round O above the head; eight wiggly octopus-arm fingers on each o. Mouth open and round — short, a quick /ŏ/, not a long rounded Mandarin "o."

Lesson 15 · c — /k/ — "C for the Cat"

Sound: /k/  ·  Keyword: the cookie, the cat  ·  voice off Words sung: cat · can · cap · cot · cookie

Suno accent — crisp and clicky; a clean little /k/ at the front of every word — no buzz.

Lyrics

C, c, c — a clicking sound,
crunchy as a cookie, round.

C for the cat with a cap on its head.
C for the cot — a little cat bed.
A can, a cookie — c, c, c!

C, c, c — a clicking sound,
back of the tongue, way at the back it's found:
c, c, c!

Movement — a "crunch the cookie" bite gesture, or a soft cat-paw tap, on each /k/. Back of the tongue lifts and clicks. Voice OFF — no throat buzz; pair this with the goat song to feel the difference.

Lesson 16 · k — /k/ — "K Says It Too"

Sound: /k/  ·  Keyword: the kite, the king  ·  same sound as c Words sung: kid · kit · kite · king

Suno accent — light and breezy, a kite-in-the-wind lilt; the /k/ stays crisp.

Lyrics

K, k, k — the very same sound!
C and k — look what we found.

K for the kid with a kit and a kite.
K for the king — his crown is bright.
Two letters, one sound — k, k, k!

K, k, k — say it with c,
they make one sound, just wait and see:
k, k, k!

Movement — fly a hand like a kite up high on each /k/. Teach the little secret here: c and k make the same sound — two letters, one job.

Lesson 17 · ck — /k/ — "Two Letters, One Kick"

Sound: /k/  ·  Keyword: the end-of-word k  ·  ck says one sound Words sung: kick · sock · pick · stick · pack · dock

Suno accent — punchy, with a strong beat to land the /k/ hard at the end of each word.

Lyrics

C-k, c-k — at the end!
Two little letters, the very best friends.

Give it a kick — kick, kick, kick!
Pull on a sock and pick up a stick.
Pack on the dock — c-k, click!

C-k, c-k — they hold hands tight,
one little sound, and they say it right:
ck, ck, ck!

Movement — one big finger-sweep under "ck" on each ck word — one sweep, one sound, never "kuh-kuh." Land it hard at the end of kick, sock, pick — that final /k/ is the Mandarin-L1 watch point. Tissue test.

Suno accent — light and quick; the /ĕ/ short and clear, halfway between /ĭ/ and /ă/.

Lyrics

Lesson 20 · r — /r/ — "Rrr Goes the Engine"

Sound: /r/  ·  Keyword: the running engine  ·  lips round, tongue curls Words sung: run · red · rat · rug · rock

Suno accent — slow and steady, with a low rolling engine hum; give the /r/ plenty of room.

Lyrics

R, r, r — start the engine,
r, r, r — round and rumbling.

R for run — run, run, run!
R for the red rug in the sun.
A rat on a rock — r, r, r!

R, r, r — start the engine,

Movement — both hands "drive" a wheel, low engine rumble rrr. Lips round, tongue tip curls back and does not touch the roof. This is the hardest English sound for Mandarin-L1 children — pair it daily with the l song and run red / led, rip / lip. Slow progress is normal; keep it warm.

Lesson 24 · l — /l/ — "La-La-La Goes L"

Phase 2 — Vowel Power (Lessons 35–41)

Phase 2 drills the five short vowels to automaticity, one at a time, then closes with the FLSZ doubling rule. These songs are faster — the children already know the sounds; now they sing them quick and sure.

Phase 3 — Digraphs and Blends (Lessons 42–53)

Phase 3 brings letters together: digraphs — two letters, one new sound — and blends — two or three letters, each keeping its own sound, slid together. These are the hardest songs to sing cleanly, and the most important for a Mandarin-L1 ear, which has no consonant clusters at all. Sing them slow first, then quick.

Using the songs — the weekly routine

  • Sing it first. The sound-song opens the lesson — two or three minutes at the circle, before the sandpaper letter and the word work. The children hear the sound, say it, and move it, then go to the materials already primed.
  • Do the movement every time. The movement is the articulation lesson set to music — not optional decoration. For the hard sounds (the final stops, /r/, /th/, /v/, /z/) it is the most important part of the song.
  • Sing the same song all week. Repetition is how a four-year-old comes to own a song. By Friday they sing it without you.
  • Slow first, then quick. Especially the Phase 3 blend songs — sing them slow enough that every sound is clean before you let the tempo lift.
  • Heard, not read. The children sing these; they do not decode them. Keep the songs joyful and loose. The point is the sound in the ear and the body, not words on a page.
  • Pair them with the Pink Readers. When a child reaches a gate, the matching reader is the milestone book — and the songs from the lessons up to that gate are a warm-up the child already knows by heart.

Forty-nine songs, one for every lesson — sing the Pink Phase from the first soft hiss of the snake to the triumphant hurray of the triple blends.
