Foundations — the science behind every lesson

The Simple View of Reading

Reading comprehension = decoding × linguistic comprehension. Both factors are necessary; neither alone is sufficient. Gough & Tunmer (1986). For Mandarin-L1 ESL learners, BOTH factors typically start near zero in English. That's why this framework runs vocabulary work (Stage –1, classified baskets) alongside phonics — multiplication only happens when both factors grow together.

Ehri's four phases of word reading

Linnea Ehri (2009) describes how every reader progresses through four developmental phases. Knowing where a child is tells you what to teach next.

PhaseWhat the child doesWhere in Pink Phase
1. Pre-alphabeticReads logos and shapes (the M for McDonald's). No letter-sound knowledge yet.Before Phase 1 / Stage –1
2. Partial alphabeticKnows some letters; reads using the first letter or two as a clue.UFLI lessons 5-15 (SATPIN through C)
3. Full alphabeticDecodes every letter of a word reliably. Slow but accurate.UFLI lessons 15-34
4. Consolidated alphabeticReads chunks (digraphs, syllables, common patterns) without sounding out each letter.UFLI lessons 35+ (Pink end, Blue, Green)

The first three CVC words a child decodes (sat, pat, tip) are Phase 3 moments. Don't rush past them — the slow decoding builds the orthographic mapping that powers fluent reading later.

UFLI's 8-step daily routine

Every UFLI lesson uses the same 8-step structure. The pattern itself is the lesson — children know what's coming, which lowers anxiety and frees attention for new content.

  1. Phonemic awareness warm-up (5 min, oral, no letters). Today's sound, drilled.
  2. Visual drill — flash through known letters, see the grapheme say the phoneme.
  3. Auditory drill — hear the phoneme, write the grapheme on a whiteboard.
  4. Introduce the new concept (sandpaper letter, three-period lesson, articulation in the mirror).
  5. Word work — encoding first. Build today's target words with the movable alphabet. Always before reading.
  6. Word work — reading. Read today's words from cards. New + review interleaved.
  7. Sentences. Read short decodable sentences using only known letters + introduced heart words.
  8. Heart words. Introduce or review 2-3 high-frequency irregular words.

Steps 1-4 are warm-up + new concept. Steps 5-8 are application. About 30-40 minutes total, or split into two 20-minute sessions for 3-4 year olds.

How this beats traditional Pink-Blue-Green (NAMC, AMS)

Both traditions agree on more than they disagree. The this framework keeps Montessori's physical materials (sandpaper letters, movable alphabet, metal insets) and developmental posture (writing before reading, child-led pace), and replaces NAMC's untested sequence with UFLI's RCT-proven one (Lane et al. 2025).

How to use this document

For each lesson, you get the words, phrases, sentences, picture suggestions, heart words, and Mandarin-L1 articulation notes you need to produce the per-lesson card sets (Material 11 in the Making Guide). Print the words at large size on 240gsm card; print the pictures from Canva or Google Image search using the prompts given; laminate; slice. Make 3-5 copies of each decodable booklet so several children can read at once.

Order of operations every lesson: articulation (mirror, sound game) → sandpaper letter (three-period lesson) → spelling words (movable alphabet, encoding) → reading words → phrase cards → sentence cards → decodable booklet → heart words.

Encoding before decoding. Always. Even when the child can already read the words.

Phase 1 — The Alphabet (Lessons 1-34)

Lessons 1-4 are pre-reading review: rhyming, alphabet sequence, sound matching, and concepts of print. Run these as oral group activities; no letter cards yet. From lesson 5 the daily 8-step routine kicks in with one new letter per lesson.

Lesson 5 — s  /s/ letter pool: s

s (snake sound)

Articulation  ·  Teeth almost closed. Tongue tip just behind upper teeth (does not touch). Air hisses out steadily. Voice OFF — no vibration when you put a hand on your throat. Long sound — say "ssssss" and hold it.

Mandarin L1 note · Mandarin L1 critical: Mandarin syllables do not end in /t/. Speakers will drop the final /t/ — "sat" becomes "sa." Practice the final aspiration explicitly. Mandarin L1 note · The hardest sound. Real Mandarin transfer problem. Mandarin r (as in rè 热) is a retroflex fricative — totally different from English /r/. Accept slow progress; this sound takes months.

Picture sourcing — Canva and Google playbook

For every reading word you'll want a matching picture for the picture-word matching set (Material 11K in the Making Guide). The picture should be unambiguous, child-friendly, and consistent in style.

Search prompts that work

  • Canva — search the noun + "illustration" or "icon" or "kids style." E.g.: cat illustration kids, cup icon flat, fox cartoon child.
  • Google Images — append "png transparent" or "clipart" if you want isolated objects. E.g.: pan png transparent, net clipart simple.
  • For abstract words (on, in, at, the article "a") — show the function visually. "on" → a ball on a box. "in" → a cat inside a box. "a" → finger pointing at one object.
  • For positional/verbs (sit, run, hop) — child + action illustrations, search "verb kid illustration".

Consistency tips

  • Pick ONE visual style (line-drawing, flat illustration, photograph) and stay with it for at least a whole Phase. Mixing styles fragments the child's visual memory.
  • White or transparent backgrounds always. The picture itself does the work; backgrounds add noise.
  • Print at 6 × 8 cm minimum. Smaller and the child squints; larger and the cards become unwieldy.
  • Same picture for the picture-word matching card AND the dictionary/heart-word card (when the word appears). Children learn faster when the same image keeps reappearing.
