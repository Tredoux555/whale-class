# Yo-yo — Sleep Pattern Briefing for the Principal

**Prepared:** 27 May 2026
**Child:** Yo-yo (Whale Class, age ~6.5)
**Audience:** Principal (preparing for a meeting with Yo-yo's mother)
**Source data:** 8 photo-documented sleep events (Mar 18 → May 25), 5 teacher-logged behavioural observations, 5 Guru analytical sessions, the child's mental profile
**Purpose:** Help the principal hold a sensitive but constructive conversation with the mother about a pattern we are seeing, without putting the mother on the defensive

---

## 1. The conversation in one sentence

Yo-yo is sleeping or going still through significant portions of the school day, in a pattern that our observations suggest is his body's way of managing how much it is being asked to do — and we would like to partner with the family to support him.

---

## 2. What we are observing (the facts, no interpretation)

These are not opinions; they are documented observations from the past ten weeks:

- **Frequency.** On May 25 alone, photographic records show Yo-yo lying down or going still in **five separate moments** between 09:12 and 15:49 — on mats, on a wooden bed frame, on floor cushions, hands empty in each case, no work in hand.
- **Earlier examples.**
  - 22 May — found lying flat on a floor mat in the middle of the work cycle, a teacher kneeling beside him.
  - 13 May — slumped forward over the armrest of a child-sized chair, head hanging down, mid-morning.
  - 13 May — lying on a padded mat in a corner of the classroom while peers continued their work.
  - 15 April — restless rather than settled during the scheduled nap period itself, lying face-down on the cot with shoes still on.
- **Mealtime pattern (Apr 3–4).** During the same week we documented a related pattern: Yo-yo wandering the room with works rather than sitting at the meal table, and when redirected, preferring to sit on the floor doing nothing rather than eat. The mother independently reported the **same pattern at home that week.**
- **March 13 incident.** After being struck by another child before lunch, Yo-yo did not react, cry, retaliate, or seek a teacher. He immediately lay down and "slept." This is the observation that first prompted us to look carefully at the pattern.

---

## 3. What we believe is going on (the working interpretation)

We hold this lightly and want to share it with the mother as something to think about together — not as a diagnosis.

What our observations suggest is that Yo-yo's body uses **rest as a way of regulating itself when the demands of the day exceed what he has energy for.** This is not laziness, not defiance, and not a sleep-hygiene problem in the ordinary sense. It is his nervous system reaching for the calmest possible state when other strategies feel out of reach. The food refusal pattern at midday and the slipping-into-sleep after being hit are the same mechanism showing up in different situations.

Children at this age are still developing the equipment that lets them stay regulated through a full day of structured demands. Some children fidget. Some children become loud. Some children withdraw. Yo-yo withdraws. Each of those is information about what the child needs, not a fault in the child.

---

## 4. What we have already done in the classroom

So that the mother knows we are not coming to her empty-handed:

- His daily shelf was adjusted in early April toward **physical, rhythmic, low-stakes work** — spooning, plant care, Pink Tower, Number Rods — designed to build regulatory capacity rather than spend it.
- We have shortened work periods and front-loaded the more demanding language and writing work into the **morning**, when his available energy is highest. (His mental profile records "morning" as his optimal time of day.)
- We use **invitation rather than direction** wherever possible — offering a work, not demanding it — because pressure consistently produces shutdown.
- When he chooses to rest, we let him. Forcing him to "stay engaged" makes the pattern worse, not better.

These adjustments have been gentle and ongoing. The mother does not need to feel that anything is being "done to" her son or that we have been treating him as a problem.

---

## 5. The conversation we recommend — actual script

This is what we suggest the principal says, in this order. Tone is warm and curious throughout; the mother should leave feeling she has been listened to, not lectured.

### Opening (2 minutes — warm + thank her for coming)

> "Thank you for making time to come in. We wanted to invite you in not because anything is wrong, but because we have been noticing something about Yo-yo that we'd like to think about together. You know him in places we don't — at home, at weekends, when he is most himself. We see him only in the classroom. Between us, we'll have the fuller picture."

### Share the observation (3 minutes — neutral language, no jargon)

> "Over the past two months we have noticed that Yo-yo, during the school day, **rests** more than most of the other children his age. He will lie down on the floor mats, on the cushions, sometimes during the work cycle and sometimes during the meal time. He is not unwell — he simply seems to need these moments of quiet. Last Monday alone we counted five separate moments where he chose to lie down between morning arrival and the end of lunch."

> "We are not concerned in the sense of something being wrong with him. We are curious. Children rest when their bodies are asking them to. We would like to understand what his body might be asking for, and how we can support him."

### Ask, don't tell (5 minutes — this is where you learn)

This is the most important part of the meeting. Ask, and then **be quiet** and let her talk.

> "Can we ask you a few things?"
>
> - "How does he sleep at night? What time does he go to bed, and what time does he wake?"
> - "Does he nap on weekends? When? For how long?"
> - "Are mornings rushed for him before school? Or calm?"
> - "Is he eating well at home? Especially at the family dinner table?"
> - "Have there been any changes recently — a new schedule, a new sibling, a move, a change of caregiver, a change in his routine?"
> - "What is his energy like on a Saturday morning compared to a Monday morning?"

These questions are not interrogation. They give the mother a way to volunteer information without feeling she is being accused of anything. If there is anything happening at home that is contributing — a hard schedule, late nights, household stress, anything — these questions open the door for her to mention it on her own terms.

### Offer the partnership (2 minutes — what we'll do together)

> "Here is what we'd like to do, with your permission: keep observing him gently, keep his rhythm in the morning where he is at his best, let him rest when his body asks for it, and stay in close contact with you about what we see. Could we ask the same of you — that you watch him at home with the same curious eye, and let us know what you notice?"

### Close (1 minute — affirm and reassure)

> "He is a thoughtful boy. He has a strong phonetic foundation, his copying is beautiful, and we love having him in the class. We are not worried — we just want to make sure we are giving him what he needs. Thank you for hearing us out."

---

## 6. Things the principal should NOT say

These are the lines that, based on prior conversations with the mother, will cause her to shut the meeting down:

- **Do not say "trauma."** Even though our internal notes use this word, it is not the principal's place to introduce it and the mother will hear it as an accusation.
- **Do not say "autism" or "spectrum."** This is on his mental profile but it is not for this meeting.
- **Do not say "we are worried about him."** Use "we have noticed" / "we are curious."
- **Do not mention the hitting incident** unless the mother asks directly. It is in our records; it does not belong in this conversation.
- **Do not compare him to other children** ("the others don't do this") — this triggers performance pressure.
- **Do not promise specific outcomes** ("if you do X, he will Y"). Offer partnership, not prescription.
- **Do not suggest seeing a doctor or therapist.** That is a step for a later, more trust-based conversation, after she has come to the same conclusion herself.

---

## 7. If she pushes back

A few likely things the mother might say, and a gentle way to hold the ground:

**"He sleeps fine at home."**
> "That is genuinely helpful to know. So what we are seeing here is something that happens specifically in the school environment. That tells us his body finds something about the school day demanding in a particular way, and we'll keep watching to understand what."

**"He is just bored — give him harder work."**
> "We have noticed that when we offer him more challenging work, the resting pattern actually increases rather than decreases. So we don't think it's boredom — it is more likely the opposite. We are pacing his day deliberately, and we are watching his English progress carefully. He is in a good place there."

**"Why are you telling me this? Is he sick?"**
> "Not at all. We share what we are seeing because you are his parent and the partnership between us is what helps him most. If we kept it to ourselves we would not be doing our job."

**"What do you want me to do about it?"**
> "Nothing immediate. Just be aware of the pattern, watch with us at home, and let us know what you notice. The most important thing is that home stays calm and predictable — that is the single biggest support we can give him at this age."

## 9. After the meeting

Whatever is said, the principal should send a short follow-up note within 24 hours — warm, two or three sentences, no clinical content — reaffirming the partnership and inviting her to call any time. The relationship is the work. The observations will keep accumulating; the trust is what unlocks any future conversation.
